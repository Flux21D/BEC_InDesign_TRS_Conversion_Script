Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Plan the opinion statement and the order of supporting paragraphs for my opinion essay.
  • Identify reasons and text details that support my opinion statement.
  • Share ideas through collaborative conversation.

Additional Materials

  • Opinion Text Planning chart

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Ask students to plan their essay elements by answering in writing the following questions. Model the sentence frames for writing their answers.

  • Introduction that states opinion
  • What is your opinion? [Model answer:]
  • … is more important than…
  • Body that supports opinion
  • What details support your idea? [Model answers:]
  • He led the way for others.
  • Sweatt’s case happened before Brown v. Board of Ed
  • Conclusion that summarizes opinion [Model answers:]
  • How do the details support your opinion?
  • The court case shows that Sweatt was a role model by inspiring others.
  • His decision to challenge the law school meant that Sweatt was ambitious because he refused to accept the norm.

Invite pairs to read each other’s answers and ask questions about anything that is unclear. Provide them with the question frames in the teaching materials as support.

Moderate Support

Invite students to complete the chart, providing the following directions to help clarify what is needed in each section:

  • Introduction that states an opinion
  • Present your opinion.
  • Body that supports opinion
  • Give details that support your idea.
  • Conclusion that summarizes
  • Explain how the details support your opinion.

Have pairs read each other’s answers and ask questions to clarify. Provide them with the question frames in the teaching materials as support.

Light Support

Ask students to complete the chart provided. Have them work together to help one another if there is anything they do not understand.

Have pairs read each other’s charts and ask questions to clarify information. Provide them with the question frames in the teaching materials as support.

10 Plan an Opinion Text (15–20 min.)  W.5.1a, W.5.1b, W.5.1d, W.5.4, W.5.5, W.5.8, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Remind students that in the last writing lesson, they reread to find text evidence to support an opinion about which of two court cases was more important. Ask students what the next step in the writing process is. Review that planning comes next. Tell students that in this core lesson, you will model how to plan a text that meets the requirements of the prompt.

Model

Display an Opinion Essay Planning chart. Read aloud the elements of an opinion essay, listed in the left column. Then use the elements to model how you would organize the essay.

Sample modeling: An opinion essay must clearly state my opinion, give reasons for that opinion, and provide supporting details as evidence. I will state my opinion in the opening paragraph. The body paragraphs will include reasons why I have the opinion and will flow in a logical order. I will include text details in each body paragraph to support my opinion.

Sample modeling:The prompt asks for my opinion about which of two court cases was more important. My opinion is that Sweatt v. Painter was more important. I will state that in my introduction, support it in the body, and restate it in a way that sums up my ideas in the conclusion.

Elements of an Opinion Essay

My Ideas

Introduction that states a clear opinion

Sweatt v. Painter was more important than Brown v. Board of Education

Body that supports the opinion with reasons and specific details and examples
  • Sweatt was an example for others, “an ordinary man who had an extraordinary dream.”
  • Sweatt blazed the way for others and kept fighting, showing “determination to attain the best and a refusal to settle for anything less.”
  • Sweatt v. Painter in 1950 influenced “the landmark Brown v. the Board of Education ruling of 1954.

Conclusion that restates the opinion and sums up ideas

Sweatt v. Painter set the precedent for Brown v. Board of Education, so it was a critical step in the process.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Distribute or ask students to create a two-column chart. Ask students to work in pairs to reread the opinion text writing prompt in their Texts for Close Reading and then spend a few minutes working individually to record an opinion statement and a few ideas for their body paragraphs. Then have them work together to plan their essays. As students share ideas, remind them to listen carefully, ask clarifying questions, and state their own ideas clearly. You may wish to provide question stems to help them organize and express their ideas:

  • Could you give me another reason to explain why _____?
  • What text evidence do you have to support _____?
  • How will you restate your opinion for a conclusion?

Share

Invite a few students to share their partner’s ideas with the whole class. Ask other students to tell how sharing and discussing ideas helped them clarify their essay plan. Use this discussion time to determine who may need additional support.

Manage Independent Writing

During independent time or as homework, ask students to complete their plan, focusing on how they will order their body paragraphs to make the most logical sense.

Students who feel ready may begin drafting. Meet one-on-one with students who need additional support.
