Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Write the bold words you are about to read on the board and have pairs copy each word on an individual strip of paper (anniversary, serve, inspiration, learn, turn, pleasure, firm, heard, feature, disturb). Ask them to create a chart and place the word in the appropriate column as they hear you read it.
Provide partners with the list of words they might find when they search the text (pursue, liberty, earlier, homeowners, tolerated, another, difference, either, whatever, ever, liberates, generates). Invite them to locate them in the text and write them in the appropriate column of their chart. Ask if there are other words they can find in the reading that were not already mentioned.

Moderate Support

Have students write the words you dictate on individual strips of paper (anniversary, serve, inspiration, learn, turn, pleasure, firm, heard, feature, disturb). Then ask them to check their spelling using a dictionary. Finally have them place the words in the appropriate columns of their charts.
Read aloud the list of words they might find when they search the text (pursue, liberty, earlier, homeowners, tolerated, another, difference, either, whatever, ever, liberates, generates). Ask them to write them as they hear them. Then have them locate the words in the text and check their spelling. Finally, have them write them in the appropriate column of their chart. Ask if there are other words they can find in the reading that were not already mentioned.

Light Support

Ask students to create the chart that is modeled.
Then have them write the words you dictate in the appropriate columns of the chart (anniversary, serve, inspiration, learn, turn, pleasure, firm, heard, feature, disturb). Have them then check their spelling with a dictionary and edit their chart as needed.
Ask them to locate examples of these words in the text and write them in the appropriate column of their chart. Examples are pursue, liberty, earlier, homeowners, tolerated, another, difference, either, whatever, ever, liberates, generates.

9 Introduce r-Controlled Vowels er, ir, ur (er, ear, ere, ir, ur, ure) (15–20 min.) RF.5.3a, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Introduce/Model

Reread the introduction to “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” and underline the words Thurgood, lawyer, and first. Circle the ur, er, and ir and stress those sounds as you say the words aloud. Explain that these letter combinations all stand for the sound /r/. Point out that when the letter r follows a vowel, the sound of the vowel changes. It is not long or short. Students will learn to recognize these spelling patterns and read and spell words with /r/.

Display the words early and nature. Circle the letters ear and ure. Point out that these letter combinations also stand for the /r/ sound.

Guided Practice

Display a five-column word-sort chart and a list of words to sort: liberty, thirty, survey, earned, signature. Read each word aloud. Ask students to repeat the word after you and identify the letters that stand for /r/ and the column in which the word belongs.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Distribute blank five-column charts. Invite partners to work together to sort the words into the appropriate columns on the chart as you read aloud a list of words with r-controlled vowels, for example: anniversary, serve, inspiration, learn, turn, pleasure, firm, heard, feature, disturb. Provide dictionaries for students to use to check spelling. After students have finished sorting the words, have them look for additional words with the target sound spellings in paragraph 6 of the “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” and add them to the appropriate columns of the chart. (Possible answers: pursue, liberty, earlier, homeowners, tolerated, another, difference, either, whatever, ever, liberates, generates)

Share

Encourage partners to share their charts with the class. Ask students to discuss any challenges they had differentiating between r-controlled vowel sounds or with spellings. Use this time to correct any spelling errors and to point out that -ear can also stand for the vowel sound in the paragraph words years and fear as well as the vowel sound in heart in the first paragraph.

Reread to Apply Word Knowledge

Ask students to reread paragraph 6 of “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” with a partner, paying attention to words with r-controlled vowels. Ask partners to tell each other how understanding the ways in which the /er/ sound can be spelled helps them read words correctly in the text. If necessary, model your thinking about one or two of the words.

Challenge Activity: Have partners work independently to list as many words with the r-controlled vowels er, ir, and ur as they can in one minute. Ask them to compare lists and see how many of the words they listed were the same and how many were different. Finally, ask students to sort their words by spelling pattern.

Spelling

Display the spelling words and read them aloud. Ask partners to take turns reading the list and pronouncing each word correctly, using what they know about r-controlled vowel spelling patterns. Then have partners collaborate to write each word in a sentence. Students should check each other’s spelling.
