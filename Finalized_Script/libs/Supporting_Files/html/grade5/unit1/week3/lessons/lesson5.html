Ways to Scaffold the First Reading

Use your observational assessment to determine the intensity of scaffolding your students need.

IF . . .

THEN consider . . .

Students are English learners who may struggle with vocabulary and language demands . . .

Read the text TO students.

  • Conduct a before-reading picture walk to introduce vocabulary and concepts.
  • Stop after meaningful chunks to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase difficult sentences.

Students are struggling readers who may decode with little comprehension . . .

Read the text WITH students.

  • Stop after meaningful chunks to ask who, what, when, where, how questions.
  • Work with students to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase key ideas.

Students need some support to read unfamiliar texts with comprehension . . .

Have students PARTNER-READ.

Partners should:

  • take turns reading aloud meaningful chunks.
  • ask each other who, what, when, where, how questions about the text.
  • circle unfamiliar words and define them using context clues.

Substantial Support

Provide students with a sample chart for recording key details. Once they finish, have them share and explain why they are important.

Key Details

7

Marshall says the Sweatts and Shelleys hoped for justice and equality. He thinks this has not happened.

8–9

He sees a divided nation. He says there is tension, despair, and rage because of division and isolation.

10–12

He thinks America can do better and must not have apathy, fear, hatred. We cannot divide and have liberty for all.
  • These details are important because they describe Marshall’s opinions.
  • Marshall thinks that the nation is divided.
  • He describes apathy, fear, and hatred.
  • Marshall believes that America can do better.

Moderate Support

Provide students with the same chart, but expect more sophisticated language when they report their main idea. Provide examples to support their language production when they are reporting.

  • Marshall expresses/shares
  • He provides/illustrates
  • He urges/implores

Light Support

Ask students to list key details from paragraphs 7, 8–9, and 10–12. Encourage them to share what they have recorded, and explain why each is important.

Engage Thinking

Display and have students open “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.” Ask students to recall what they read yesterday. Revisit the key details and main ideas chart from Lesson 3. Explain that today students will finish reading this persuasive speech.

Ask:Why did Sweatt want to change laws that dealt with education?

Model: Read to Find Key Details

Display and read aloud the purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraph 7 to find out why Justice Marshall wishes he could be more proud of progress made. Identify key details and determine the main idea of this paragraph.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Display and ask students to follow along as you read aloud, model, and annotate this paragraph of “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.”

Sample modeling, sentence 1: This sentence tells me that Justice Marshall is thinking back to the Shelleys and the Sweatts and “what became of the challenge.”

Sample modeling, sentences 2–5:Here, I will underline the detail that they were all hopeful that “America would grow toward justice and expand toward equality.”

Sample modeling, sentences 7–9:The word “But” gives me a clue that what he hoped for is not true at the time of his speech. In his opinion, America has not “come to appreciate diversity and to see and accept similarity.” I think this is the main idea of the paragraph.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 8–9 to learn about examples of racism in different parts of the country. Identify key details and determine the main idea of these paragraphs.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Work as a class to fill out a Key Details and Main Idea chart. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as they look for key details. For example:

  • What words does Justice Marshall repeat? Do you think this is important to note?
  • Is the story about the porter important or simply interesting?

Reread and Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to reread paragraphs 10–12. Ask them to underline key details in the text and generate a main idea statement on their own. Discuss with students their opinions on the reasons democracy could not “flourish” at the time of Thurgood Marshall’s speech. Ask students to write their main idea statement on paper to hand in. Use their writing to evaluate their understanding of the strategy and the text.
