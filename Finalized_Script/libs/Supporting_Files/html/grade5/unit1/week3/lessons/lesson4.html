Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Read and analyze a writing prompt.
  • Share ideas with a partner and build on ideas through collaborative conversation.

Additional Materials

  • Analyze the Writing Prompt chart

Observation Checklist for Productive Engagement

As partners discuss the writing prompt, look for evidence that they are truly engaged in the task.

Partners are engaged productively if they . . .

  • ask questions and use feedback to address the task.
  • demonstrate engagement and motivation.
  • apply strategies with some success.

If the engagement is productive, continue the task. Then move to Share.

Partners are not engaged productively if they . . .

  • apply no strategies to the task.
  • show frustration or anger.
  • give up.

If the engagement is unproductive, end the task, and provide support.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Ask students to record their own and their partner’s answers for each question in the Analyze the Writing Prompt chart. Provide this model:

  • My ideas/My partner’s ideas
  • An opinion/an essay
  • Marshall’s speech/the speech
  • Details of the cases/facts about the cases
  • Support my choice/support my opinion

Ask them to create a single list that uses information from both people to answer the questions. Then summarize the task.

  • An opinion essay
  • Marshall’s speech
  • Facts and details of each case
  • Support my opinion

I must write an opinion essay to explain why I think one case is more important, using facts and details from each case presented in Marshall’s speech.

Moderate Support

Ask students to record an answer for each question in the Analyze the Writing Prompt chart, using their own ideas and the ideas of their partners. Examples:

  • An opinion essay, making a choice
  • Marshall’s acceptance speech for the Liberty Medal
  • Details explaining the two cases presented
  • Support my opinion with the information

Then have them summarize the task.

Write an opinion essay explaining which case is more important, using information from Marshall’s acceptance speech.

Light Support

Ask students to report the key ideas that they had when answering each question. Then ask them to summarize the writing task. Their summary must answer each of the questions presented in the Analyze the Writing Prompt chart.

4Read and Analyze an Opinion Prompt (15–20 MIN.) W.5.4, W.5.5, W.5.8, W.5.9b, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Explain to students that this week they will write an opinion text based on the writing prompt on page 31 of their Texts for Close Reading. During today’s lesson, you will model how to read and analyze a writing prompt. Review that when writing in response to a prompt, students should carefully analyze the prompt to be sure they understand what they are being asked to do.

Model

Display the writing prompt you will use during this week’s core lessons. This is again a different prompt from the one students will write to independently.

In his “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech,” Thurgood Marshall mentions two important Supreme Court cases: Sweatt v. Painter, which integrated law schools, and Brown v. Board of Education, which integrated public schools. Which of these two cases do you think was more important? Write an opinion essay in which you answer this question, using details and evidence from “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.”

Model Lesson Prompt

Display Analyze the Writing Prompt chart and model how you answer each question in order to fully understand the task laid out in the prompt.

Sample modeling: The first question asks what type of text genre I will be writing. This is important to write the correct type of response, and the answer is at the end of the prompt: “Write an opinion essay. . .” telling which of two cases I think is more important, Sweatt v. Painter or Brown v. Board of Education. I will need to find and give reasons for my opinion, so I am to use “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” to find facts and details to support my reasons.

Analyze the Prompt Question

Answer

What text type are you being asked to write?

An opinion essay in response to a question

What are the characteristics of this writing form?

An opinion essay states a point of view and supports it with evidence.

What source must you use as the basis for your writing?

“Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech”

What details or evidence from the source will you need to look for?

Facts and details about Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. Board of Education

How will you use the details or evidence?

I will use the details to support my opinion about which case I think is more important.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask students to open to the writing prompt on page 31 of their Texts for Close Reading. Invite a volunteer to read aloud the prompt.

Encourage partners to work together to analyze the prompt by answering the questions on the chart. Remind students to take turns sharing their ideas and leading the discussion.

Share

Invite different pairs of students to share their answer to each question. Ask other students to ask clarifying questions or add any additional ideas they may have in a constructive manner. Use the conversation to make sure all students understand the writing task for the week.

Manage Independent Writing

Direct students to look back through “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.” Ask them to think about which kind of freedom is more important and what details might help them support their ideas. Tell them that in the next writing lesson, you will model how to find and use text evidence to support an opinion.
