Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Share information I have learned about the Constitution and how laws change.
  • Explain how the laws of the Constitution affect me.
  • Use formal English to summarize my partner’s ideas.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Ask students for short phrases that describe their partner’s answers. Provide examples.

  • [Name] learned about slaves.
  • [Name] said one man tried to be free.
  • He went to court.
  • His issue was important.
  • Lincoln used his ideas.

Ask them to combine the ideas that they share. Examples for the above might include:

  • [Name] learned about slaves and trying to become free.

Have them think about classroom language to express the ideas. Ask them to revise their thoughts and present them.

Moderate Support

Ask students to report their partner’s ideas using complete sentences.

  • [Name] learned about …
  • A man went to court to …
  • This was important because …

Prompt them to determine only the most important points, combine them in sentences, and share them using formal language.

Light Support

Ask students to share only the most important points of their partner’s ideas. Ask them to report more than one idea in each sentence that they use, and to be sure to use formal classroom language.

ELD.PI.5.4, ELD.PI.5.5, ELD.PII.5.7

1 Build Knowledge and Integrate Ideas  (10 min.) SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d, SL.5.2, SL.5.6

Engage Thinking

Ask students to open to Build, Reflect, Write on page 21. Remind students that their primary goal in this unit is to think about how and why laws in the U.S. Constitution continue to evolve. Last week, they read an article about the Dred Scott decision. This week, they will read a speech given by a justice of the Supreme Court.

Say: Before we begin this week’s reading, let’s talk about what we’ve already learned.

Turn and Talk to Share Knowledge

Ask students to turn to a partner and engage in a brief conversation to address three questions. Encourage students to refer to their Build, Reflect, Write notes. Tell students they will be asked to summarize their partner’s comments, so they should listen carefully and jot notes.

  • What new information did you learn from last week’s reading?
  • How has your thinking about the evolution of laws changed?
  • How do the laws of the Constitution affect you personally?

Share

Call on a few students to share their partner’s answers to the questions. Encourage other students to build on those ideas by adding their own. Review with students that during their partner discussion, they may use informal English. They can have a conversation about the questions. However, when they summarize for the class, they should speak in complete sentences and use more formal English.

Sample modeling of informal English: [Name] said he learned a lot about one man’s case to become free. He didn’t win the case but it helped lots of other slaves.

Sample modeling of formal English: [Name] talked about the Dred Scott decision as the most important issue of the time. Scott did not give up and, even though he did not win the case, Abraham Lincoln used Scott’s case to help win the election and later to issue the Emancipation Proclamation.
