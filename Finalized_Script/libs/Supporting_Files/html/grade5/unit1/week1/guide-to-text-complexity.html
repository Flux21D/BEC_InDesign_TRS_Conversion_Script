Guide to Text Complexity

Text complexity dimensions from CCSS are scored on the following scale:

1 Low 2 Middle Low 3 Middle High 4 High

Grades 4-5
Lexile®: 740L-1010L

Short Read 1: “Creating the Constitution”


Informational
Social Studies
Quantitative Lexile® 1090L
Qualitative Analysis of Text Complexity
Purpose and Levels of Meaning 2
  • This reading provides a very brief overview of the origin of United States Constitution, establishes its importance as a groundbreaking written framework for government, and reflects on its lasting impact among other democratic nations.
Structure 2
  • This informational text includes sequence of events text and descriptive structures.
  • Graphic elements include direct quotes from a primary source and an image with caption.
Language Conventionality and Clarity 2
  • Sentences are simple and straightforward, unfamiliar words supported with description and other context clues.
Knowledge Demands 2
  • A general background of U.S. History and U.S. government and citizenship is helpful but not required.

Short Read 2: “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address”


Speech
Quantitative Lexile® 1030L
Qualitative Analysis of Text Complexity
Purpose and Levels of Meaning 3
  • The purpose of Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights speech was to unite and persuade his constituents—all American citizens—of the idea that the right to vote is a right secured by the United States Constitution, and that it is the duty of both state and federal governments to recognize and protect that right for all Americans and not allow the states to trample over the Constitution.
Structure 3
  • This selection of excerpts quotes a primary source, and a persuasive speech that is organized sequentially but also includes some problem and solution text.
  • Graphic features include photos and captions, and other primary source images.
Language Conventionality and Clarity 3
  • Sentences are generally simple and clear-cut, with some complex constructions, however content vocabulary is largely unsupported.
Knowledge Demands 3
  • Prior knowledge of U.S. History, U.S. Government and Citizenship, and U.S. voting rights is necessary to provide historical context and would aid in understanding the text.
