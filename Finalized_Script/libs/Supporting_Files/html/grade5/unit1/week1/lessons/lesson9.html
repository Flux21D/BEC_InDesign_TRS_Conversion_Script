Assist students by using the Planning chart and rereading information about the U.S. Constitution from the two texts.

Elements of Informative Writing

Ideas

Introduction

Should introduce what the Constitution is and why it was created.

Say: I can restate the reason from the preamble, and use ideas from the third sentence of paragraph 1: “Never before . . .”

Body

Three paragraphs should support main idea.

Say: The article answers what and why. I could also tell how it’s evolving. I can use information from both texts. These ideas will be my three body paragraphs.

Seek evidence with students.

Conclusion

Should restate point made in introduction in different words.

Provide sentence frames/stems for partners to plan their main idea.

  • Created in _____ to _____, the Constitution established a _____ for _____.

Engage Thinking

Remind students that you have modeled how to analyze a writing prompt and how to find information in a text for their informative essay. Explain that the next step is to plan the essay, and in this lesson you will model planning a text that will meet the requirements of the prompt.

Ask: How do you plan an informative essay? What elements do you think the essay should have?

Model

Display and use a two-column chart to create an Informative Essay chart. Read aloud the elements in the left column. Explain that an informative/explanatory essay tells information about a topic. The writer starts by stating a main idea, or telling the reader what the essay is about, in a thesis statement. Then the writer goes on to provide facts, definitions, details, and other information and examples as evidence to show that the main idea is true. These details are organized in a logical way and presented clearly. The writer provides a sense of closure by writing a concluding statement at the end. The conclusion sums up what’s been said and often repeats the main idea.

Model your thinking for how to plan your informative essay.

Sample modeling:Before I get started, I’m going to reread the writing prompt. It will help me determine the topic and main idea of my essay. The topic of my essay is James Madison’s role in creating the U.S. Constitution. My main idea, or thesis statement, is that James Madison played an important role in creating the U.S. Constitution.

Sample modeling:In the body of my essay, I will give details and evidence from the text that support this main idea. For example, I will mention that the delegates who drafted the Constitution followed Madison’s Virginia Plan.

Elements of Informative Writing

My Ideas

Introduction that states a main idea about the topic

James Madison played an important role in creating the U.S. Constitution.

Body that provides facts, details, information, and examples as evidence to support the main idea
  • In 1787, Madison was part of the Constitutional Convention.
  • Madison previously created the state constitution for Virginia. The delegates were interested in his ideas.
  • Madison wanted to give the national government more power.
  • Eventually the delegates drafted the Constitution. They followed Madison’s Virginia Plan. Madison became the principal writer.
  • Madison went on to write the Federalist Papers and the Bill of Rights.

Conclusion that sums up the essay and restates the main idea

Madison had a crucial role in writing the U.S. Constitution and getting it approved.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Distribute a two-column chart. Ask partners to reread the informative writing prompt in their Texts for Close Reading (page 11). Then have them work together to plan their essays. Suggest that partners start by planning their ideas individually and then get together to exchange ideas. As students share, have the listening student ask questions to help the partner clarify thinking or find additional evidence in the source text.

Students should be prepared to summarize their partners’ ideas for the class and explain how the ideas reflect information from the source text.

Provide students with the following questions to help them organize and express their ideas.

  • What is the U.S. Constitution?
  • Why was the U.S. Constitution created?
  • What facts or details did you find in “Creating the Constitution” to support this?

Share

Invite several students to share the ideas their partner described with the whole class. Ask other students to tell how the discussion and their partner’s questions helped them focus thinking about their own ideas.

Manage Independent Writing

During independent time or as homework, ask students to fine-tune the main ideas in their writing plans. Students who feel ready may begin their draft. Use conferring time to meet one-on-one with students who need additional support.
