4 Analyze Text Structure: Chronology (15–20 MIN.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Tell students that text structure means how authors organize information in a text. Informational texts about historical events are often written in chronological, or time, order. This text structure helps readers understand the sequence in which events happened and the relationships between events in history.

Ask: How can recognizing the order in which events happened help you understand how the U.S. Constitution was created and how it has evolved?

Model

Read aloud paragraph 2 as students follow along. After reading, stop to model thinking and annotate the text to show how you pay attention to the chronological text structure. Point out that sometimes dates as well as time-order words or phrases, such as first, last, soon after, or before long indicate chronology. Record the chronological relationships and the signal language on a chart.

Sample modeling, sentence 1: As I read, I’m going to look for and underline time-order words and dates that help me understand relationships between events in the text. Sentence 1 tells me I am going to read about how the Constitution was created. There are no dates or time-order words, so I’ll keep reading to see if I can find out when this happened.

Sample modeling, sentence 2: Sentence 2 contains a date in 1787. This date tells me specifically when James Madison and the other delegates met to create the Constitution, so I’m going to underline it.

Sample modeling, sentences 3–4: These sentences tell me that the meeting was called the Constitutional Convention and its goal was to make changes to the government that had formed following the Revolutionary War. I know that the Revolutionary War was over in 1783. The word following tells me that the government was formed after 1783. So I understand that the purpose of the convention was to change the existing government.

Sample modeling, sentences 5–7: The word previously in sentence 5 is another word that signals time order. It helps me understand that the delegates were interested in Madison’s ideas because he had experience creating the state constitution for Virginia.

Guided Practice

Ask partners to read paragraph 3 and underline examples of chronology and the signal language that helps them recognize the text structure. Distribute a three-column chart for partners to write their events and signal words. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as needed. For example:

  • Pay attention to the phrase At the time at the beginning of sentence 2. To what time is the author referring? How does this help you understand the events?

Reflect on the Strategy

Ask partners to reflect on the strategy and its relevance to other content-reading experiences by turning to a partner and briefly addressing a question: What are some other historical events that could be explained in a text using a chronological text structure? Continue the discussion on how chronological text structure aids in understanding historical texts like this one.

Call on volunteers to provide examples. Reinforce students’ understanding that recognizing the chronological text structure will help them understand many texts.

Write to Apply Text Structure Knowledge

Ask students to read paragraphs 4 and 5 and write down the chronological relationship described and the signal language that helps them recognize the structure. Use students’ text structure analysis to evaluate their strategy development and help you make instructional decisions.

Challenge Activity: Ask students to write a paragraph that reflects the sequence of events of a special day in their lives. Remind them to include dates and/or time-order words to make the sequence clear.
