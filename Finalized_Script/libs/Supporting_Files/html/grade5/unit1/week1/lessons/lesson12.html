Engage Thinking

Ask:What do you think of when you hear the word draft?

Explain that a draft is a preliminary piece of writing. The drafting stage comprises a writer’s first attempt to put ideas into an organized text. Emphasize that a draft does not need to be perfect. Explain that today you will discuss a draft of an informative essay based on the model lesson prompt.

Model

Display and read aloud an exemplar informative essay.

Introduction clearly states the focus and main idea.

Body breaks the information into paragraphs, grouping related information together logically. Writer uses conjunctions to join two ideas in one sentence in a logical way.

Paragraphs develop the topic with facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples.

Writer links ideas using words and phrases that tell about time (after many months) and importance (significantly).

Writer uses precise language and domain-specific vocabulary.

Essay has a conclusion that brings the ideas together and restates the main idea.

James Madison has been called the “Father of the U.S. Constitution” because he was instrumental in drafting this important document.
In 1787, Madison was part of a group of delegates who came together in Philadelphia at the Constitutional Convention. The other delegates were very interested in Madison’s ideas because he had created a state constitution for Virginia. According to “Creating the Constitution,” at the time of the convention the thirteen states had a lot of power to govern themselves. Madison wanted to change this situation because he believed too much state power made it difficult for the national government to collect taxes and form a military.
After many months, the delegates approved what we now know as the U.S. Constitution. Significantly, the document followed Madison’s Virginia Plan. It established a stronger federal government. Madison’s role did not stop with drafting the Constitution. He went on to help write the Federalist Papers and the Bill of Rights. The Federalist Papers helped persuade readers to accept the new Constitution. The Bill of Rights are the first ten amendments to the Constitution, giving U.S. citizens freedoms such as free speech.
It is easy to see how James Madison got the nickname “Father of the Constitution.” He played a crucial role in drafting the document and its first ten amendments.

Unit 1 Exemplar Informative Essay

Productive Engagement: Partner

Invite students to collaborate to draft an introduction and first paragraph of another informative essay using the same prompt. One student should be the notetaker, and both students should contribute to the writing. Encourage them to keep in mind the elements of an informative essay that you have discussed. Emphasize that you do not expect partners to draft a complete essay in the time allowed.

Share

Invite volunteers to read aloud their sentences to the class and identify the elements of an informative essay that they were able to incorporate. Observe students’ understanding of the genre and review the features, if necessary.

Build Language: Recognize Sentence Fragments and Run-ons

Review with students that a sentence has a subject and a verb and expresses a complete thought. Explain that a sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence. A fragment may be missing a subject, a verb, or both. Or it may have a subject and verb and be missing a complete thought. In an earlier draft, the conclusion contained these sentence fragments: “He played. A crucial role in drafting the document and its first ten amendments.” The first fragment has a subject (He/Madison) and a verb (played), but it does not express a complete thought. The second fragment is missing a verb.

Explain that when a sentence has too many complete thoughts it is called a run-on sentence. Point out and reread the following run-on sentence from an earlier draft:

James Madison has been called the “Father of the U.S. Constitution” he was instrumental in drafting this important document.

Draw students’ attention to the words “James Madison has been called the ‘Father of the U.S. Constitution’” and “he was instrumental in drafting this important document.” Explain that each of these clauses can stand on its own, but together they make a run-on sentence. Point out that there are several ways to correct a run-on sentence. For example:

  • You could make two sentences with a period between them.
  • You could put a semicolon between them.
  • You could use a conjunction between them. Explain that a conjunction is a part of speech that connects two words, phrases, or clauses. Examples include and, but, or, since, although, because.

Demonstrate how these corrections apply to the model sentence, and remind students to keep them in mind as they make revisions of their drafts.

Manage Independent Writing

Ask students to draft their informative essays based on the Texts for Close Reading prompt during independent time or as homework. If students need support with their drafting, use conferring time to meet with them one-on-one.
