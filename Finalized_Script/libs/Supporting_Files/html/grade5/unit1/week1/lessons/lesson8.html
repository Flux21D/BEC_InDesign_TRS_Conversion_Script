ELD.PI.5.1, ELD.PIII, ELD.PIII

8 Introduce Short Vowels a, e, ea, i, o, u  (15–20 min.) RF.5.3a, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Introduce/Model

Reread paragraph 1 of “Creating the Constitution” and underline that and impact. Circle the a in that and the i and a in impact. Say each word aloud, emphasizing the short a and short i vowel sounds. Remind students that the letters a, e, i, o, and u are vowels. Explain that a vowel is usually short in a syllable in which a single vowel is followed by a consonant, as in the word that. The vowel pair ae can also stand for the short e sound, as in bread.

A syllable with a single vowel followed by a consonant is called a closed syllable. In a multisyllabic word, such as impact, a closed syllable will often have a short vowel. Explain that knowing syllabication patterns will help students read unfamiliar words.

Display the words establish, document, and running. Break each word into syllables, then help students identify the closed syllables and the short vowel sounds: e stab lish (short e, a, i); doc u ment (short o); run ning (short u, i). Point out that in multisyllabic words the unstressed syllable often has the schwa sound, not a short vowel sound. This is the case with document.

Guided Practice

Create and display a five-column Word-Sort chart and a list of words to sort national, delegate, historical, popular, ugly). Read aloud the five headings and then read each word aloud and ask students to identify the column in which it belongs. Discuss the meaning of the word in each column. For example:

Something national relates to an entire nation or country.

A delegate is a person chosen to represent others.

If something is historical, it is about the past, not the present.

When something is popular, a lot of people like it.

The opposite of ugly is pretty.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Provide a list of additional words with short vowel sounds such as man, Madison, section, dread, listed, dignity, single, complex, adoption, sudden, clutch. Ask partners to take turns reading each word and identifying the column or columns in which the word belongs. Ask partners to discuss the meaning of each word. Provide dictionaries if students need support.

Share

Invite partners to share the words in each column of their Word-Sort charts. Use this opportunity to point out that unstressed syllables usually do not have a short or long vowel sound.

Reread to Apply Word Knowledge

Ask students to reread paragraph 1 of “Creating the Constitution” with a partner, paying attention to the wordsthat and impact. Invite partners to tell each other how their understanding of syllabication patterns and short vowel sounds helped them to read and pronounce the words correctly so as to get more meaning out of the selection. If necessary, model your thinking about the words.

Challenge Activity: Ask a group of students to reread paragraph 2 of “Creating the Constitution” and generate a list of additional words with short vowel sounds. Have students identify each short vowel sound in a word by circling the letter that stands for the sound. Point out that some words, such as discuss, have more than one short vowel sound.

Spelling

Display the spelling words and read them aloud. Ask partners to pronounce each word correctly, using what they know about syllabication patterns and short vowel sounds. Review the meanings of any words students do not know. Encourage students to spell each word.

Prompt students to collaborate with a partner to use each spelling word in an oral sentence. Then have partners copy the spelling words and check each other’s spellings.
