Ways to Scaffold the First Reading

Use your observational assessment to determine the intensity of scaffolding your students need.

IF . . .

THEN consider . . .

Students are English learners who may struggle with vocabulary and language demands . . .

Read the text TO students.

  • Conduct a before-reading picture walk to introduce vocabulary and concepts.
  • Stop after meaningful chunks to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase difficult sentences.

Students are struggling readers who may decode with little comprehension . . .

Read the text WITH students.

  • Stop after meaningful chunks to ask who, what, when, where and how questions.
  • Work with students to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase key ideas.

Students need some support to read unfamiliar texts with comprehension . . .

Have students PARTNER-READ. Partners should:

  • take turns reading aloud meaningful chunks.
  • ask each other who, what, when, where and how questions about the text.
  • circle unfamiliar words and define them using context clues.

Substantial Support

Model for students.

Say: Text features like the introduction provide key details:

1) While the 15th amendment gave African Americans the right to vote, 100 years later, problems remained.

2) Johnson gave a speech calling for an end to the disenfranchisement.

I know there was a solution to this problem so this speech should tell me more about both.

Assign groups stems for key details in the introduction and captions.

  • Nearly 100 years after _____, they still faced _____.
  • It was during the _____ in the mid-1960s, that Johnson _____.
  • March 15, 1965: Johnson _____.
  • March 7, 1965: protesters _____.
  • March 30, 1870: the fifteenth _____.
  • August 6, 1965: Johnson _____.

Have students turn to partners to tell what is probably the main idea of the speech (Johnson wants a new act to pass that will guarantee African Americans the right to vote). Have them explain using the key details. Work together to read the rest of the speech to find key details with evidence of the problems.

  • In many places, _____. (8)
  • _____ discrimination. (9)
  • local officials _____. (9)

Moderate Support

Say: Paragraphs 6–8 should support the main idea of the speech.

Have partners use frames/stems to find key details that support the main idea about the right to vote.

his right to be _____. (6)

an equal right _____ _____ .(7)

_____ the denial of that right. (7)

_____ to ensure that right. (7)

Yet the harsh fact _____. (8)

Assign groups to paragraphs 6–8 to find two sentences that state the main idea.

  • “ _____” and “_____” give the main idea because

    • _____.

Light Support

Assign groups to paragraphs 9–11 to find key details with these stems.

  • “_____ discrimination/_____ when local

  • “The Constitution says _____.” (10)

Ask: How do these support the main idea?

Challenge students to tell how well the ideas were supported in the speech.

7“Voting Rights Act Address”: Identify Main Idea and Key Details  (15–20 MIN.)  RF.5.4a, RI.5.1, RI.5.2, RI.5.10, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Model: Preview the Genre

Display and ask students to open to “Voting Rights Act Address.” Remind students that it is helpful to preview and make predictions about a text before they read. Model how you notice text features (such as the title) and graphic features (such as the photographs) as you skim and scan “Voting Rights Act Address.” Model thinking about how you ask questions and make predictions about the genre and content.

Sample modeling: The first thing I look at is the title. In small print I see President Lyndon Johnson’s name and I see the word “Address” in the title. I know that an “address” can be a speech, so I predict this is a speech given by President Johnson. I wonder what the speech is about. The title includes the words “Voting Rights Act.”

Sample modeling: The photographs show people protesting and voting, and President Johnson after signing the Voting Rights Act into law. I predict the speech will tell why the Voting Rights Act was important.

Invite students to turn and tell a partner what they observe and predict. Invite a few students to share their predictions with the class.

Model: Read to Find Key Details

Display and read aloud the purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read the introduction and paragraphs 1–5 to learn about the impact of this Voting Rights Act at that time. Identify key details and determine the main idea.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Display the text and ask students to follow along as you read aloud, model thinking, and annotate the introduction, photo caption, and paragraphs 1–5.

Sample modeling, introduction: The introduction lets me know why President Johnson gave the speech, and when and where he gave it. I think these are important details.I will underline the details that tell me that President Lyndon Johnson gave a speech to Congress in 1965 calling for an end to the disenfranchisement of black voters. Disenfranchisement is a big word that means taking away someone’s right to vote.

Sample modeling, paragraphs 1–2: President Johnson begins his speech by saying that all citizens deserve the freedoms granted by the Constitution. The details in paragraphs 1 and 2 are interesting, but I don’t think they are essential, so I won’t underline them.

Sample modeling, paragraphs 3–4: President Johnson explains that the protest march in Selma, Alabama, is a problem for the entire country, not just one group of people, and it is the responsibility of the entire country to solve the problem. Since the speech is about calling for an end to the discrimination against African American voters, I think these details are important. I will underline the last sentence in paragraph 3: “There is only an American problem.” I will also underline these words in paragraph 4: “And we are met here tonight as Americans . . . to solve that problem.”

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 6–8 and 9–11 to learn why this law needed to be established and enforced when President Johnson spoke. Identify key details and determine the main ideas in this part of the text.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Give students three to five minutes to read and annotate. Observe their annotations to assess their ability to identify important versus unimportant information. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as they look for key details. For example:

  • Reread paragraph 7. What important details did you read?
  • Does the information in sentence 3 support the main idea? How?

Collaborate with students to construct a main idea statement for each section based on the key details they found. Then challenge students to summarize these paragraphs of the “Voting Rights Act Address” in one sentence.

Key Details

Possible Main Ideas

6–8

Every citizen “shall share in freedom, he shall choose his leaders, educate his children, provide for his family...”

“Every American citizen must have an equal right to vote.”

“Yet…in many places in this country men and women are kept from voting simply because they are Negroes…”

All Americans have the right to vote, but there are places where African Americans are being denied the right to vote.

9–11

Existing laws are not sufficient to overcome discrimination in places where local officials are determined to prevent African Americans from voting.

The Constitution states that all U.S. citizens have the right to vote. We must defend that right.

Congress needs to pass a new law to ensure that all states follow the Constitution and allow all Americans to vote regardless of their race or ethnicity.

Summary Statement: The right to vote is a promise made to every citizen in the United States of America, and it is our duty to uphold and defend this right for every citizen.

Read and Write to Apply the Strategy

Ask students to reread paragraphs 9–11 to underline key details and generate a main idea statement for this part of the speech. Ask students to write their summary statement about the long-term impact of this Act and hand in their writing. Use their ideas to evaluate their strategy development and help you make instructional decisions.
