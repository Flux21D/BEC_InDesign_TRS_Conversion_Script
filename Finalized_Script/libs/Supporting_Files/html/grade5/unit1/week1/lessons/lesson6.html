Engage Thinking

Say: In the last writing lesson we learned how to analyze a writing prompt. What do you think we should do next to get ready to write an informative essay?

Explain that one thing students need to do is to read the source text carefully to find the information they will need for their essays. Display and reread the model lesson prompt, and remind students that this prompt asks them to write an essay based on information they read in “Creating the Constitution.” In today’s writing lesson, you will model how to reread the text to find this information.

In a brief essay, explain James Madison’s role in creating the U.S. Constitution. Support your explanation with facts, definitions, and details from “Creating the Constitution.”

Model Lesson Prompt

Model

Reread aloud paragraphs 1 and 2 of “Creating the Constitution” as students follow along. Model your thinking for how you identify potential information to write about and add the information to a two-column chart.

Sample modeling: I am looking for information about James Madison and his role in creating the Constitution. Paragraph 1 gives information about the Constitution, but it doesn’t give any details about James Madison or how the Constitution was created. I’ll look further.

Sample modeling: In Paragraph 2, the author provides facts about a series of meetings during which the Constitution was created. The second and third sentences say, “In 1787, James Madison and fifty-six other delegates had a series of meetings … called the Constitutional Convention …” These meetings led to the writing of the Constitution, so I’m going to write down these details.

Sample modeling: The text goes on to say that the delegates were interested in Madison’s ideas and he became the main writer of the Constitution. I’ll write that down, too.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask partners to reread paragraphs 3–5 to find at least one additional detail pointing to Madison’s role in creating the Constitution. Partners should identify the text evidence and write their related idea in the annotation column of their Texts for Close Reading (or on a two-column chart). Explain that partners should be prepared to share their evidence and ideas with the class. Display the Informative Essay Ideas chart you created, or provide a blank chart if students want to chart the evidence they have gathered.

Text Evidence (paragraph)

Ideas for Informative Essay

“In 1787, James Madison and fifty-six other delegates … held a series of meetings called the Constitutional Convention.” (2)

“The delegates were interested in his ideas. . .” Madison became the principal writer, or framer, of the Constitution.” (2)

Madison was a part of the Constitutional Convention, which was a series of meetings at which the Constitution was created.

Madison became the principal writer of the Constitution.

“Madison argued for a strong central government.” (3)

“After months …, the delegates decided on a final document. They mostly followed Madison’s Virginia Plan and established a stronger federal government.” (3)

Madison’s ideas about a strong central government had a strong influence on the shape of the U.S. Constitution.

Madison also helped write newspaper articles that persuaded readers to accept the new Constitution. (4)

Madison’s influence went beyond creating the Constitution. His writings persuaded readers to accept it.

Madison helped create the Bill of Rights. For all his work, Madison has been called the Father of the Constitution.

(5)

Madison also helped create the Bill of Rights—the first ten amendments to the Constitution.

Informative Essay Ideas Chart

Share

Bring students together to share their text evidence and explain how it supports their topic. Remind students that when they use evidence from a source, they can restate the evidence in their own words or directly quote the text, in which case they need to use quotation marks. They should credit the source, either way. Ask other students to listen carefully to evaluate the evidence, and encourage them to express their agreement or disagreement in a respectful way.

Manage Independent Writing

Remind students that they will be writing an essay in response to the writing prompt on page 11 of their Texts for Close Reading. During independent time or as homework, students should find evidence from “Creating the Constitution” that they can use as supporting details in writing their essays. Suggest that they use an Informative Essay Ideas chart to record their ideas. Use conferring time to meet one on one with students who may need support, and make time for students to engage in peer collaboration.
