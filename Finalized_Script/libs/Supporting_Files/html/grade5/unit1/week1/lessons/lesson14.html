14 Compare and Contrast Informative and Persuasive Texts (15–20 MIN.) RI.5.1, RI.5.2, RI.5.5, RI.5.8, RI.5.9, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Explain that today students will compare and contrast the two texts you read this week. One, “Creating the Constitution,” is an informational text. The other, “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address,” is persuasive. Comparing these two texts will add to students’ understanding of the U.S. Constitution and how laws continue to evolve.

Ask: How can reading two different texts on the same topic help you gain a deeper understanding of the topic?

Model

Display a Sample Compare/Contrast chart and distribute a three-column chart (see p. 31). Review the categories you will compare and contrast, and make sure students understand them. Model comparing and contrasting the first category and add your information to the chart as you model. Be sure to use compare/contrast signal language and call attention to it, if necessary.

Sample modeling, purpose: “Creating the Constitution” and “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address” both relate to the Constitution, but the authors had different purposes in writing the texts. “Creating the Constitution” is intended to inform readers about how the U.S. Constitution came to be, while the “Voting Rights Act Address” is intended to persuade Congress to pass a new law to ensure that all states follow the Constitution.

Guided Practice

Invite partners to focus on the other categories in the chart to compare and contrast the texts. Suggest that partner A describe one text in relation to the category and partner B compare or contrast it to the other text. You may wish to call on a student to help you model your expectations for the student conversation. For example:

Partner A: “Creating the Constitution” uses an objective point of view. It gives facts about the history of how the U.S. Constitution came to be.

Partner B: I agree. On the other hand, “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address” uses a subjective point of view. It expresses the author’s personal opinion about what he considers a problem for everyone in America: the discrimination against African American voters. The author believes that some states are deliberately ignoring the part of the Constitution that says every citizen must have an equal right to vote, and that the government has an obligation to ensure all states follow the Constitution.

Call on partners to share their ideas. Provide feedback as needed to support students’ efforts.

Evidence is given of historical events and documents to support opinions about the principles on which our country was founded.

Apply Cross-Text Knowledge

Ask students to write two paragraphs to compare and contrast “Creating the Constitution” and “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address.” One paragraph should address ways the texts are alike, and the other should address ways they are different. Encourage students to use evidence from the selections to support their points. Use students’ writing to evaluate their ability to state and support comparisons and contrasts, particularly in dealing with social studies content. You may also use this and other writing samples to assess students’ use of English conventions and their need for support to write fluidly and legibly in cursive or joined italics.
