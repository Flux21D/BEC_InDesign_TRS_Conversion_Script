Pose Essential Question

Ask students to access the Unit 1 Opener in their texts or on their devices. Read aloud the question. Explain that in each unit of study this year, they will encounter a question like this. Point out that an essential question does not have one simple answer. It encourages students to think deeply about the topic. As they read and listen to selections, they will keep this question in mind, and they will add to and revise their ideas.

Why do laws continue to evolve?

State Unit Objectives

Say: More than 200 years ago, the leaders of our country wrote a set of laws called the U.S. Constitution. Many of you know that the Constitution is the most important set of laws in the United States. It explains how our government works and also describes the rights or freedoms that all American citizens have. The Constitution is called a “living document” because it changes. In this unit, we will learn some reasons why laws continue to evolve, or change, by reading, viewing, and listening to informational and primary sources. We will learn that laws continue to evolve because our country and its people continue to grow and change.

Link to Prior Knowledge

Model how you draw on your prior knowledge to think about the Essential Question.

Sample modeling: This question makes me think of a law that affects me personally: the dangers of riding a bicycle without a helmet. I know that people used to be able to ride their bicycles without wearing a helmet. However, many children were injured while riding their bicycles. As a result, many states passed laws requiring people under 16 or 18 to wear a helmet. This change in the law was helpful.

Ask students to turn to a partner and share their initial thoughts about why laws that seem so “set” could change. Encourage them to share an experience or insight they may have related to laws changing. Ask them to write down their ideas.

View Multimedia

Display the Unit 1 video and ask students to write down any questions or new ideas that come to mind as they watch the video. Listen for domain-specific vocabulary that students do or do not have facility with, and support them as needed.

Collaborative Conversation: Peer Group

Ask students to work in peer groups to generate questions to guide their inquiry throughout the unit. Distribute the Guiding Questions/Initial Ideas chart for students to capture their ideas in the chart. Remind them to construct strong, open-ended questions rather than narrow questions that have one answer. You may wish to have each group designate a discussion director, notetaker, and summarizer. Model sentence frames to support participation of all students. For example:

I wonder what laws were changed or created as a result of _____.

I wonder how _____ affects _____.

Share

Call on a member of each peer group to share a guiding question his or her group generated. Call on a different student to share the group’s initial ideas related to that question. Capture the questions and ideas on a class Guiding Questions/Initial Ideas chart. Invite members of other groups to contribute by adding their own ideas based on the other groups’ questions. Listen for opportunities to model how speakers build on the ideas of others in constructive ways.

Sample modeling: [Name] pointed out that some laws evolve because people think that they are unfair.

Sample modeling: I agree with [Name] who pointed out that some new laws evolved because people think that they are unfair. I bet there many different ways people show that they do not like a certain law. Some people may write to government leaders to get them to work to change the law.

Use the brief conversation to help you benchmark students’ knowledge around the unit topic and to build their interest.
