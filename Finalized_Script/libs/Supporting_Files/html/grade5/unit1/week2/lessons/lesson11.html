Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Reread a text to find text evidence and draw an inference from the evidence.
  • Use my understanding of drawing inferences to answer a close reading question.
  • Share my ideas in collaborative conversation.
  • Communicate my inference and text evidence in writing.

11Close Reading: Use Text Evidence to Draw Inferences  (15 20 MIN.)  RI.5.1, RI.5.3, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d, L.5.5c

Engage Thinking

Remind students that when they draw inferences, they put together pieces of evidence in order to better understand the text, especially ideas that are not stated directly. Today, you will model how to draw an inference using facts and details, and then students will draw their own inferences about the text.

Ask: Can you always find the answer to a question directly in a text? What do you sometimes have to do to answer a question?

Model: Reread to Find Text Evidence

Display and read aloud the Close Reading Prompt and annotation instructions. Model how you think about the prompt.

Sample modeling: The prompt asks me to “imagine” a particular scenario and to use text evidence to draw an inference about what might have happened if a similar case had happened in the 1820s instead of 1847. I need to reread paragraphs 13–16 and look for evidence of “legal and social factors” that might have led to a different ruling. “Legal” means having to do with the law, and “social” means having to do with people and public opinion. I need to keep both those terms in mind.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 13–16. Imagine that someone had filed a case similar to Dred Scott’s in the 1820s instead of in 1847. What legal and social factors might have contributed to a different ruling in this case? Refer to specific text evidence to support your answer.

Annotate! Underline key words or phrases from that text that support your answer.

Display and distribute blank two-column charts. Read aloud paragraphs 13–16 as students follow along. Then model how you think about the text.

Sample modeling: In sentences 3 and 4 of paragraph 13, I read “slave states like Missouri had sometimes freed slaves” but that “opinions against freeing slaves had hardened.” Freeing slaves happened “earlier” than the Dred Scott case; “earlier” could refer to years before 1857, including the 1820s. They relate to “social factors.”

Sample modeling: Paragraph 6 is about Justice Taney’s decision, but this information is not evidence I need. Taney’s decision “canceled out the Missouri Compromise of 1820, which for nearly forty years had limited where slavery could be practiced.”

Sample modeling: That means the Missouri Compromise law had been in place from 1820 to 1857. That sentence relates to “legal factors.” I can use these pieces of evidence to draw an inference that a similar case in the 1820s might have ended with a different ruling because of different social and legal ideas in that time period.

Sample Text Evidence (paragraph)

Sample Inference

“Earlier, slave states like Missouri had sometimes freed slaves who had lived in a free state for a long time. This is why Dred Scott thought he had a chance, but opinions against freeing slaves had hardened. . .” (13)

A slave who had filed a case similar to Dred Scott’s in the 1820s would have had a better chance at getting a different ruling—and being set free. Socially, “opinions against freeing slaves had hardened” by the 1840s. In addition, the Missouri Compromise was in place in the 1820s and “limited

Justice Taney’s decision “canceled out the Missouri where slavery could be practiced.”(16)

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second Close Reading Prompt.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 11–15. How did the court decisions throughout the Dred Scott case reflect the feelings of the nation in the 1840s and 1850s? Refer to specific text evidence to support your answer.

Annotate! Underline key words or phrases from the text that support your answer.

Ask students to reread and annotate the text independently or with a partner to respond to the prompt. Observe students’ notes to determine if they need additional modeling or directive feedback to find text evidence and draw an inference.

Share

Call on several students to share their answers to the close reading question, using the discussion to encourage students to build on their ideas. Students should use text evidence to support the inference that the back-and-forth nature of the decisions reflected the divided opinion of the nation over the issue of slavery.

Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to write their answer to the second close reading question, citing evidence from the text in support of their inference. Remind students to quote accurately from the text.

RI.5.1 Quote accurately from a text when explaining what the text says explicitly and when drawing inferences from the text. RI.5.3 Explain the relationships or interactions between two or more individuals, events, ideas, or concepts in a historical, scientific, or technical text based on specific information in the text. W.5.10 Write routinely over extended time frames (time for research, reflection, and revision) and shorter time frames (a single sitting or a day or two) for a range of discipline-specific tasks, purposes, and audiences.
