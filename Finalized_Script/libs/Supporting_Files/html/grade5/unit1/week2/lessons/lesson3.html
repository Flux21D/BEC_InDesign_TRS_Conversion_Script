Ways to Scaffold the First Reading

Use your observational assessment to determine the intensity of scaffolding your students need.

IF . . .

THEN consider . . .

Students are English learners who may struggle with vocabulary and language demands. . .

Read the text TO students.

  • Conduct a before-reading picture walk to introduce vocabulary and concepts.
  • Stop after meaningful chunks to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase difficult sentences.

Students are struggling readers who may decode with little comprehension . . .

Read the text WITH students.

  • Stop after meaningful chunks to ask who, what, when, where, how questions.
  • Work with students to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase key ideas.

Students need some support to read unfamiliar texts with comprehension . . .

Have students PARTNER-READ.

Partners should:

  • take turns reading aloud meaningful chunks.
  • ask each other who, what, when, where, how questions about the text.
  • circle unfamiliar words and define them using context clues.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Model for students.

Say: I read that in 1857, the Supreme Court decided “no black person, free or slave, could ever become a U.S. citizen. . .” But I know something changed that so I’ll look closer. I see a caption by the petition.

Ask: Is this the court decision we read about?

Elicit that this case began in 1846, 11 years before.

Ask partners to skim paragraphs 8–10 for key words like free/freedom and claim/claimed about the 1846 case in order to find the main idea.

  • The third sentence in paragraph 9 gives the main idea of this section because _____.

Ask partners to use stems to find key details.

  • Where they lived and for how long became the key facts on which Dred Scott based his claim for freedom.
  • In April 1846, he asked a Missouri court for freedom.
  • No one knows why he didn’t make the claim earlier.

Moderate Support

Assign groups to find the main idea and key details for paragraphs 8–10, using:

  • the petition’s caption
  • key words claim/claimed, free/freedom
  • reason for filing the 1846 case

Bring groups together to decide on a main idea and details. Appoint a recorder to record.

  • Main idea: Scott claimed _____ because _____.
  • He based his claim on where _____ and _____.
  • He made his claim to _____ in _____.
  • No one knows _____.

Light Support

Assign partners to provide main ideas for paragraphs 1–4, 5–7, and 8–10 to summarize. Provide stems.

  • The Supreme Court took Scott’s case because _____.
  • While Scott had been _____, he lived in _____.
  • Scott claimed _____ because _____.

Then have partners share/comment.

  • That’s a key detail because _____.
  • That’s a main idea because _____.

3“The Dred Scott Decision,” Part 1: Identify Main Ideas and Key Details  (15–20 min.) RF.5.4a, RI.5.1, RI.5.2, RI.5.10, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Model: Preview the Genre

Display and ask students to open to “The Dred Scott Decision.” Review how to skillfully preview and make predictions about a text as they prepare to read. Model how you skim and scan the “The Dred Scott Decision.” Point out text and graphic features as you make predictions about the genre and content using your observations.

Sample modeling: The title tells me that this is about something called the Dred Scott decision. I see a portrait with the name Dred Scott and dates, so I can guess that Dred Scott was a real person. I also see quotations dated from the 1860s and a map of slave and free territories in the United States. Based on these observations, I predict this is an informational text. As I continue skimming, I notice the words “court” and “case” appear many times. I’m curious about this court case and wonder what it was about. I predict the selection will tell me.

Invite students to turn and tell a partner what they observe and predict. Invite a few students to share their predictions with the class.

Model: Read to Find Key Details

Display and read aloud the purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 1–4 of “The Dred Scott Decision” to find out who Dred Scott was and what significant role he played in the African American fight for freedom. Identify key details and determine the main idea of this portion of the text.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Ask students to follow along as you read aloud, model, and annotate paragraphs 1–4.

Sample modeling, paragraph 1: This paragraph explains what the Dred Scott decision was, who made it, and when it happened. I will underline these key details—”March 6, 1857” and “the United States Supreme Court made a decision.” I will also underline the words that tell what the decision said: “Dred Scott, an enslaved African American, had gone to court asking for his freedom in 1846 . . . . the court decided against him.”

Sample modeling, paragraph 2: Before the 1840s, people disagreed on the issue of slavery but compromised. The country was divided between free and slave states. I will underline that detail and the last sentence.

Sample modeling, paragraph 3: This paragraph tells me more about how people felt about slavery. Some thought there should be two countries, one slave and one free. I will underline that detail.

Sample modeling, paragraph 4: I read that the Dred Scott case was about only one man’s fate but it had a much bigger impact—the Supreme Court took the case to solve the issue of whether new territories and states should allow slavery. I will underline that sentence. These details will help me construct a main idea sentence.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 5–7 to find out more about Dred Scott’s life as a slave. Identify key details and determine the main idea in this portion of the text.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Give students three to five minutes to read and annotate. Observe their annotations to assess their ability to identify important versus unimportant information. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as they look for key details. For example:

  • Reread paragraph 6. What do you think is most important to remember about the places Scott lived with John Emerson?
  • Reread paragraph 7. What happened when John Emerson died?

Collaborate with students to construct a main idea statement for the section based on the key ideas they found.

Section Key Details Idea

Paragraphs 1–4

In 1857, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that Scott, a slave, was not to be a citizen or have any rights.
The country was divided between slave and free states. Many people wanted two nations, one slave and one free.
The Supreme Court hoped to use the Dred Scott case to determine whether a new state should be slave or free.

The Dred Scott decision came at a critical time in the debate over slave versus free territories, and the court’s decision against Dred Scott fanned the flames surrounding the issue. (stated)

Paragraphs 5–7

Dred Scott “was born to enslaved parents in Virginia.” Scott was sold and moved many times but spent several years with John Emerson in free territories. When Emerson died, ownership of his slaves, including Scott, was transferred to his wife.

Dred Scott was born into slavery and moved many times with his owners, living in both slave and free territories. (unstated)

Paragraphs 8–10

“As slaves, Scott and his family had no choice about where they lived.”
Scott asked the court for freedom for himself and his family “because they had lived in a free state.”

Dred Scott said he and his family should be freed from slavery because they had lived in a free state and a free territory. (stated)

Reread and Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to read paragraphs 8–10 to underline key details and generate a main idea statement for this section that showed why Dred Scott wanted to fight for freedom for himself and his family. Ask students to write their key details and main idea statement on a paper to hand in. Use their ideas to evaluate their strategy development and help you make instructional decisions. You may also want to prompt students to connect what they read to the Essential Question.

RF.5.4a Read on-level text with purpose and understanding. RI.5.1 Quote accurately from a text when explaining what the text says explicitly and when drawing inferences from the text. RI.5.2 Determine two or more main ideas of a text and explain how they are supported by key details; summarize the text. RI.5.10 By the end of the year, read and comprehend informational texts, including history/social studies, science, and technical texts, at the high end of the grades 4-5 text complexity band independently and proficiently. W.5.10 Write routinely over extended time frames (time for research, reflection, and revision) and shorter time frames (a single sitting or a day or two) for a range of discipline-specific tasks, purposes, and audiences.
