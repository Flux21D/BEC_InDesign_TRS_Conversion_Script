8Close Reading: Evaluate Author’s Use of Language

(15–20 min.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d, L.5.3a

Engage Thinking

Ask: What’s the difference in reading a selection that hooks you in the first sentence and one that takes many sentences?

Discuss students’ responses about strong openers and ask if students recall texts they have read this year that immediately caught their interest. Even informative text often includes a strong beginning and a strong ending. Ask a volunteer to read the opening sentence of “The Dred Scott Decision.” Tell students you will show them how to read closely to analyze and evaluate the author’s use of language at the beginning and ending of the selection.

Model: Reread to Find Text Evidence

Display and read aloud the close reading prompt and annotation instructions. Model how you think about the prompt.

Sample modeling: This prompt asks me to think about the text’s “strong beginning that hooks the reader.” To do that, I am supposed to “analyze how the author uses language, including figurative language.” I can look for specific words that the author uses that I find interesting and that make me want to read more. I can also look for figurative language that is more descriptive and appeals to my senses.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 1–4. You’ve learned that one of the features of an informative text is a strong beginning. Analyze how the author uses language (including figurative language) to create a strong beginning that “hooks” the reader.

Annotate! Underline words, phrases, or facts that “hook” you into the text.

Read aloud paragraphs 1–4 as students follow along. Then model how you think about the text.

Sample modeling: The first sentence of paragraph 1 gives a strong example of a “hook” to grab the reader. The sentence says the Supreme Court made a decision that “stunned the nation.” I immediately want to know more—what was the decision? Why were people stunned by it? I will underline that phrase.

Sample modeling: In paragraph 3, I read that the country was divided about slavery. The author uses the phrase “just at this critical time” to describe the timing of the Dred Scott case. I want to know more about why it’s a “critical time.” Will something happen because of the case? I want to read more to find out.

Sample modeling: The end of paragraph 4 has an example of figurative language. The author writes “their decision added flames to the fire.” That descriptive phrase helps me imagine the country arguing about slavery. People are angry. The image of flames and fire helps me understand the feelings in the country. I’m hooked. I want to know how the Dred Scott decision affected people.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second Close Reading Prompt.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 18–20. You’ve learned that another feature of an informative text is a strong ending. Analyze how the author uses language to create a strong ending that keeps the reader thinking.

Annotate! Underline words, phrases, or facts that will keep you thinking after you’ve finished reading.

Ask students to read and annotate the text independently or with a partner to respond to the prompt. Observe students’ notes and annotations to determine if they need additional modeling or directive feedback to find examples.

Share

Call on several students to share their answers to the close reading question. Encourage other students to build on their ideas, ask clarifying questions, or express their reactions to the last decision to free Dred Scott. Use this opportunity to assess how students deal with conflicting opinions, providing corrective feedback, or validation based on students’ responses.

Students should find several examples of the author’s use of language designed to keep readers thinking: “a free man at last” (18), “Sadly, Dred Scott did not have very long to enjoy his freedom” (19), “case had considerable ramifications” (20). They should understand that the author used specific words and phrases to make a lasting impression on the reader’s thoughts and emotions.

Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to write one or more paragraphs detailing how the author successfully used language to hook the reader or to keep the reader thinking, using specific examples from the text.

Challenge Activity: Ask students to answer this question: Find another example of the author’s use of language that you find interesting or memorable in paragraphs 5–10. Explain why you think the language is effective.
