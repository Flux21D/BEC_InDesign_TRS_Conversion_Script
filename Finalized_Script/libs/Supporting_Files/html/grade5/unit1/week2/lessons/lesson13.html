Engage Thinking

Review with students that during the drafting stage of the writing process, writers begin putting their ideas into an organized text. A draft is not a finished piece; it is the place for writers to put all of their ideas down. Tell students that you will show and discuss a first draft of a reflection based on the model lesson prompt.

Model

Display and read aloud an exemplar reflection.

Strong opening introduces first-person narrator and situation

Careful verb use helps the reader go between past and present

Clear sequence of events unfolds naturally; transition words help manage the sequence of events

Narrator shares her thoughts and feelings about events, using concrete words and sensory details

Reflection has a sense of closure

My goodness, I cannot believe so much time has passed. When I think about how many years all this court nonsense has taken, I am completely exhausted. There were many days when I thought I should just give up, that I should just let Dred Scott and his family have their freedom. However, I was determined to fight.

It all began when my beloved husband died in 1843. I was devastated and frightened. Naturally, John left the slaves to me. I was surprised when Dred asked if he could buy his freedom. Certainly not. He was my property, and I needed the income I received from hiring him out to others. What else was I to live on? I never expected we would go to court, and I never expected to lose. Of course I had to appeal that ruling in 1847, and I thought the decision in 1852 was the end of it. It was not. Not until 1857 did we get the final court decision.

With that last decision by the Supreme Court I was so relieved. I never expected that Peter Blow’s family would buy and free the Scotts. I was stunned. I did respect Dred and his unending pursuit of freedom. I never wanted to harm him or his family, since I’ve known them for so many years. I hope they find a new life and are happy now. I am content to be at home, and I never want to see the inside of a courtroom again.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask students to work with a partner to draft a few sentences of another possible reflection from Eliza Emerson. Tell them this reflection should focus only on her thoughts and feelings about the first case that Dred Scott won and that she appealed. Select one student to act as notetaker, but have both students contribute to the partner writing. Remind students that their draft should incorporate at least some of the elements of a reflection that you discussed. Explain that you do not expect students to draft an entire reflection, just a few sentences.

Share

Invite volunteers to read aloud their sentences to the class and to identify the elements of a reflection that they were able to incorporate.

Build Language: Recognize Shifts in Verb Tense and Identify Perfect Tense

Explain that in narrative writing, narrators will be in one time looking back at a previous time. These shifts in time require a shift in verb tense. Students may want to include a time shift in their fictional reflection. If so, they will want to make sure they use verb tenses correctly. Point out and reread this example from the draft reflection:

My goodness, I cannot believe so much time has passed. When I think about how many years all this court nonsense has taken, I am completely exhausted. There were many days when I thought I should just give up, that I should just let Dred Scott and his family have their freedom. However, I was determined to fight. . . .

I did respect Dred and his unending pursuit of freedom. I never wanted to harm him or his family, since I’ve known them for so many years. I hope they find a new life and are happy now.

Draw students’ attention to the verbs believe, think, am, were, and thought. Explain where the shift happens between present (believe, think, am) and past (were, thought). Remind students that writing about the past requires using a past tense verb. Writing about the present requires a present tense verb.

Point out that writers also use the perfect tense in their writing. They use the perfect tense when discussing unfinished actions or situations, changes over time, or life experiences. Examples of the perfect tense include; I’ve been to France; I’ve loved ice cream my whole life; or, She has been my friend since childhood.

Work with students to find a shift in verb tense and the perfect tense in the exemplar text. (Students should find the shift in end of the first paragraph. The last two sentences shift back to the present tense. The perfect tense example is in the second paragraph.)
