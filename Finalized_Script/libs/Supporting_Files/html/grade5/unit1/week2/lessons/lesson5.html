Ways to Scaffold the First Reading

Use your observational assessment to determine the intensity of scaffolding your students need.

IF . . .

THEN consider . . .

Students are English learners who may struggle with vocabulary and language demands . . .

Read the text TO students.

  • Conduct a before-reading picture walk to introduce vocabulary and concepts.
  • Stop after meaningful chunks to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase difficult sentences.

Students are struggling readers who may decode with little comprehension . . .

Read the text WITH students.

  • Stop after meaningful chunks to ask who, what, when, where and how questions.
  • Work with students to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase key ideas.

Students need some support to read unfamiliar texts with comprehension . . .

Have students PARTNER-READ.
Partners should:

  • take turns reading aloud meaningful chunks.
  • ask each other who, what, when, where, how questions about the text.
  • circle unfamiliar words and define them using context clues.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Model for students.

Say:I’ll use text features to recall what happened. The quote from the Missouri State Supreme Court in 1852 says Scott was returned to slavery due to changing ideas about it. The caption on page 17 says that a court decision caused fighting. We know Scott also lost the U.S. Supreme Court case.

Ask: Which case do you think caused fights? Why?

Elicit that the fights were probably provoked by the Supreme Court decision because Northerners and Southerners had a “big stake” in the results.

Assign groups to skim paragraphs 15–17 for key events, using repeated wording:(Court’s) decision/ ruling and slaves/slavery/pro-slavery.

Ask partners to state the key events.

  • The U.S. Supreme Court ruled against Dred Scott.
  • The 1857 ruling canceled the Missouri Compromise of 1820, which had limited the practice of slavery.
  • The country became even more divided about slavery and moved closer to civil war.

Moderate Support

Assign groups to find the key events for paragraphs 15–17, using:

  • text features: what the newspapers said
  • repeated words: decision/ruling, slaves/slavery/
  • pro-slavery

Bring groups together to decide on a key event. Appoint a notetaker to record.

  • The 1857 Supreme Court ruling against Dred Scott canceled the Missouri Compromise of 1820, made the country more divided about slavery,and brought it closer to civil war.

Light Support

Tell students the key events of the last paragraphs should reveal more about the case, such as its importance. Ask them to use text features to predict. Assign partners to partner-read the rest. Have them summarize, using the stems above and the following stems for paragraphs 18–20.

  • Dred Scott died a free man but would never know how his case affected a presidential election, escalation to a war, or the end of slavery.

Ask students to discuss how one event could have such an impact on history.

Engage Thinking

Revisit the key details and main ideas chart from Lesson 3 with students. Call on specific students to paraphrase important information they read in the first part of “The Dred Scott Decision.” Explain that students will read the second half of the selection today and will work on summarizing informational text.

Model: Read to Key Events

Display and read aloud the purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 11–14 of “The Dred Scott Decision” to find out about Scott’s experiences with taking his issue to the courts. Identify key events and summarize this portion of the text.

Annotate! Place a star next to the key events as you read.

Invite students to follow along as you read aloud, model, and annotate the paragraphs.

Sample modeling, paragraph 11: In this paragraph, I read that Scott started his case in the Missouri State Court in 1847 and lost. In a new trial, “the court found for Scott” and the family was declared free. I’ll place a star next to those key events. They are important in the chronology of events.

Sample modeling, paragraph 12: I read that Mrs. Emerson wasn’t happy and “appealed the decision.” In 1852, a higher court overturned the earlier ruling. The Scotts were still considered slaves.

Sample modeling, paragraph 13: Scott took his case higher, to the U.S. Circuit Court, but in 1854 that court also ruled that “Scott and his family were still slaves.” The text explains “opinions about slavery had hardened.”

Sample modeling, paragraph 14: Next, I read that Scott “fought on.” He took his case to the Supreme Court in 1854. The trial started in 1856 and “both Southerners and Northerners had a big stake in the results.” I can use all these key events to construct a summary of these paragraphs and the chronology of events leading up to the Supreme Court trial.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 15–17 to learn what happened when Scott’s case was tried before the Supreme Court. Identify key events and summarize this portion of “The Dred Scott Decision.”

Annotate! Place a star next to the key events as you read.

Give students three to five minutes to read and annotate. Observe their annotations to assess their ability to identify important versus unimportant information. Provide a three-column chart for students to note key events and generate summary statements. Give directive and/or corrective feedback as they look for key events. For example:

  • Reread paragraph 15. Which sentence tells the key event in the paragraph?
  • Reread paragraph 16. What additional effect did the decision have?

Collaborate with students to construct a summary statement for the section.

Key Events

Possible Summary

Paragraphs 11–14

Scott lost first trial in Missouri State Court in 1847; won new trial, declaring him and his family free Mrs. Emerson appealed and won in 1852; the Scotts “still slaves”
U.S. Circuit Court upheld ruling in 1854 December 1854, Scott took case to U.S. Supreme Court.; Southerners and Northerners had “big stake” in results

Dred Scott began fighting for his freedom in 1847 with his first case in the Missouri State Court and continued up to the U.S. Supreme Court in 1854.

Paragraphs 15–17

Supreme Court’s ruling March 6, 1857: Scott “was not a citizen and had no right to sue”
Also, federal government “had no right to interfere with the free movement of property,” referring to slaves “Southerners were happy”; “Northerners were outraged”; country moved toward civil war

In 1857, the Supreme Court ruled against Dred Scott and said “property” could be freely moved in any territory, which angered those against slavery and moved the country closer to civil war.

Paragraphs 18–20

Blow family purchased the Scotts and set them free Dred Scott “died on September 17, 1858.”
Abraham Lincoln used case to help him win presidency in 1860, leading to South’s secession, Civil War, then freeing slaves and ending slavery

Dred Scott died a free man before the election of Abraham Lincoln, the Civil War, or the end of slavery, all of which were connected to the Dred Scott decision.

Possible Summary Statement: Dred Scott began fighting for his freedom in 1847. In 1857, the Supreme Court ruled against him, saying that “property” (slaves) could be freely moved in any territory. This decision angered those against slavery and moved the country closer to civil war. It influenced the election of Abraham Lincoln and was one of the factors that led to the Civil War and the end of slavery.
