14Close Reading: Compare and Contrast Informative Texts (15–20 min.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, RI.5.9, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Remind students that they know how to use text evidence to draw inferences about what they have read. When readers compare information from two texts to answer a question and draw an inference, they expand their understanding of both texts. In this close reading, students will draw inferences based on information in two texts they have read, “Creating the Constitution” and “The Dred Scott Decision.”

Ask: How could it be helpful to use two different texts to answer a question?

Model: Reread to Find Text Evidence

Display and read aloud the Close Reading Prompt and annotation instructions. Model how you think about the prompt.

Sample modeling: This prompt asks me to draw an inference from two texts to show how James Madison might have reacted to Justice Taney’s reasoning. I need to use evidence from both texts to support my inference. First, I will reread what Justice Taney’s ruling specifically said. Then I can reread “Creating the Constitution” to find evidence of what James Madison believed.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 15–16 of “The Dred Scott Decision” and paragraph 3 of “Creating the Constitution.” How do you think James Madison would have reacted to Justice Taney’s reasoning in the Dred Scott case? Refer to specific evidence from each text to support your answer.

Annotate! Take notes on Justice Taney’s ruling and Madison’s views on government.

Display and distribute a blank box chart. Read aloud paragraphs 15–16 and paragraph 3 and then model how you think about the text.

Sample modeling: Paragraph 15 says Justice Taney wrote that black people have “no rights” and that Scott “was not a citizen and had no right to sue under the Constitution.” I will circle it and come back to it if I need it. Paragraph 16 says that Taney also “said that the federal government had no right to interfere with the free movement of property throughout the territories.” I will note that.

Sample modeling: In “Creating the Constitution,” I read in paragraph 3 that James Madison “argued for a strong central government.” That’s a statement of what he thought so it is important to note. I see the end of the paragraph states that delegates “followed Madison’s Virginia Plan and established a stronger federal government.” Madison thought the federal government should have more power than the states. I’ll note that. Looking at the evidence, I can quote from both texts and draw an inference about how Madison would have reacted to Taney’s reasoning.

Text Evidence from “The Dred Scott Decision”

Text Evidence from “Creating the Constitution”

“Taney also said the federal government had no right to interfere with the free movement of property throughout the territories.”

“Madison argued for a strong central government.”

“The delegates mostly followed Madison’s Virginia Plan and established a stronger federal government.”

Answer/Inference: Based on what we have read in “Creating the Constitution,” we can infer that Madison would most likely have objected to Taney’s ruling. Madison “argued for a strong central government,” and his Virginia Plan “established a stronger federal government.” Taney’s ruling “that the federal government had no right to interfere with the free movement of property throughout the territories” decreased the power of the federal government.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second Close Reading Prompt.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraph 2 and the map in “The Dred Scott Decision” and paragraph 3 of “Creating the Constitution.” How do you think James Madison would have felt about the process used for determining whether new territories should be slave or free? Use specific evidence from each text to support your answer.

Annotate! Take notes on how territories were determined as slave or free, Madison’s ideas about government, and how the delegates created the Constitution.

Ask students to read and annotate the text independently or with a partner. Observe students’ notes and annotations to determine if they help in drawing an inference.

Share

Call on several students to share their answers to the close reading question. Lead students in a discussion of how laws do keep evolving in the light of Madison and the Dred Scott decision. Encourage other students to build on their ideas, asking and clarifying questions. Students should draw the inference that Madison would have approved of the process based on text evidence that the process involved compromise and came from the federal government (Dred Scott). Students should note that Madison believed in a strong federal government and that the process of creating the Constitution also involved compromise (Creating the Constitution).

Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to write their answer to another question: Do you think James Madison would have objected to the other half of Justice Taney’s ruling (that African Americans had no rights)?
