Engage Thinking

Remind students that during Week 1, they used strategies to help them read two complex texts about the U.S. Constitution and the laws of this country. In both of the selections they practiced recognizing the features of informational text. They also learned to pay attention to the chronological structure of the texts to understand the order in which events happened.

Say: This week you will use those same strategies, as well as others, to help you read a longer tale. Before we read, I want you to reflect on the strategies you used.

Turn and Talk to Reflect on Strategies

Say:Turn and talk to a partner. Tell your partner the features of informational text that were evident in the selections you read yesterday. Explain how chronological order can be used in informational text. Then listen carefully as your partner adds his or her ideas.

Give students one or two minutes to share information with their partner.

Share

Bring students together and call on several students to briefly summarize their partner conversation. Remind them to use formal English as they talk about ideas from the Constitution and from President Johnson’s speech on voting rights. Use the share time to assess whether students understand key features of informational text, including the use of chronological order as a text structure for historical writings. You may wish to display and review in the discussion the features of informational text.

Features of an Informational Text Web

SL.5.1a Come to discussions prepared, having read or studied required material; explicitly draw on that preparation and other information known about the topic to explore ideas under discussion. SL.5.1b Follow agreed-upon rules for discussions and carry out assigned roles. SL.5.1c Pose and respond to specific questions by making comments that contribute to the discussion and elaborate on the remarks of others. SL.5.1d Review the key ideas expressed and draw conclusions in light of information and knowledge gained from the discussions. SL.5.2 Summarize a written text read aloud or information presented in diverse media and formats, including visually, quantitatively, and orally. SL.5.6 Adapt speech to a variety of contexts and tasks, using formal English when appropriate to task and situation. (See grade 5 Language standards 1 and 3 for specific expectations.)
