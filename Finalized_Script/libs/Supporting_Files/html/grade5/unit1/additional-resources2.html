Award a Medal of Freedom


ELA RI.5.7, W.5.6, W.5.7, SL.5.4, SL.5.4a

Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Research information about Presidential Medal of Freedom winners.
  • Analyze qualifications and rationales for award winners.
  • Select personal and group choices for awards and provide evidence for decisions.
  • Write presentation speeches.
  • Share my thinking with peers.

Materials

  • Internet sources on Presidential Medal of Honor history and winners

Investigate

  • Invite students to consider individuals to whom they would award the Presidential Medal of Freedom. Ask them to begin by researching online the more than 500 recipients of the medal since 1963. Students should note the categories of winners (e.g. artists, sports figures, scientists, and so on) as well as noting individual names.
  • Have a student read aloud the official text that accompanies the medal. Then open a discussion of why presidents would consider it important to honor these particular people? Why is the medal given in the name of freedom? In what way have these individuals contributed to the nation?

Create

  • Instruct students to work in groups. Each student should nominate two individuals to whom they would like to award this honor and explain why. (Unless the class decides otherwise, individuals should be living.) Then have each group negotiate one group choice.
  • Have students write brief presentation speeches in which they present their award winners to the class. They should prepare presentation copies of the speech, with a photograph of the individual if possible.

Present

  • Have students present their winners to the class. Post the speeches on the bulletin board as well, so that students can study and discuss the choices.

Reflect and Respond

  • Encourage students to discuss the range of choices and compare reasons why the awards were made to these individuals.
  • Students should reflect on their group’s decision and consider whether they would do anything differently after the class discussion.

Create a Radio Interview Show


ELA RI.5.7, W.5.6, W.5.7, SL.5.4

Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Define the word “law” and compare political and scientific laws.
  • Research the law of conservation of matter and create a poster board to demonstrate the law.
  • Analyze other students’ displays.
  • Share my thinking with peers.

Materials

  • Internet sources on political and scientific laws

Investigate

  • Ask students to imagine what it would be like if they could actually talk to one of the Founding Fathers, such as James Madison. What would they like to ask him? Invite students to create a call-in radio talk show with James Madison as the featured guest. It might be called On the Spot or Get the Real Story.
  • Students should begin by researching more about Madison including biographical details, his role at the Constitutional Convention, his role in the compromises, and his positions in the Federalist Papers. Ask them to consider the effect that Madison had on the federal system of government we now have.

Create

  • Within each group, students should select one person as the radio interviewer and another to take on the role of Madison. The group should develop questions and answers for each actor.
  • Remind students that these will be call-in shows. They should script an interview and then be prepared to take “call in” questions. Encourage students to consider different people who might “call in” with questions, such as a former Tory, Alexander Hamilton, or others.
  • Have students make audio recordings of their programs.

Present

  • Have students present their winners to the class. Post the speeches on the bulletin board as well, so that students can study and discuss the choices.

Reflect and Respond

  • Have students reflect on what they learned about Madison and federalism in creating the radio programs. What if anything would they do differently after hearing other groups’ recordings?




NGSS

Make a Science Safety Rule Poster

Challenge

ELA RI.5.7, W.5.6, W.5.7, SL.5.5 NGSS 5-PS1-2

Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Research information about James Madison
  • Plan, write, and record scripts for a radio interview program.
  • Develop questions on federalism.
  • Share my thinking with peers.

Materials

  • Internet sources including biographical sites on Madison, the National Constitution Center, and iCivics • Audio recording equipment

Investigate

  • Open a discussion on the meaning of the word “law.” Ask students what a political law is (an official rule that people are expected to obey) and to describe some laws they have read about in this unit.
  • Then ask students to research the law of the conservation of matter as an example of a law of science. Lead students to understand that a scientific law is based on evidence, summarizes observed facts, and explains why something happens. Help students compare: a political law describes something that should happen, while a scientific law describes something that does happen.
  • Invite students to research online experiments and demonstrations for a Science Fair on the law of conservation of matter.

Create

  • Have each group select a Science Fair demonstration to prove the law of conservation of matter. Have students use visuals to create poster board displays showing the steps in their experiments.

Present

  • Set up the poster board displays and invite students to study all of them. Students should post comments and questions using sticky notes.

Reflect and Respond

  • Give students time to answer questions and respond to comments posted on their displays. Then ask them to reflect on what they learned about scientific laws in the process of preparing their displays. How does the conservation of matter reflect a scientific law?
