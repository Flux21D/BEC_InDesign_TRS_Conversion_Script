Establishing Routines (5 MIN.)

Smooth Transitions.

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Introduction to Questioning

Make a blank T-chart and introduce it to students.





Tell them that they will be using the T-chart to organize their questions about their reading.

Say: When I read, I ask myself questions. I ask myself many kinds of questions. For example, sometimes I ask questions to clarify what the author is saying. Sometimes I ask myself whether or not I agree with what the author has said. In stories, I wonder about the characters and what will happen next. My questions keep me interested in reading on.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.

Additional Materials

  • Blank T-chart

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (17 Min.)

Read Aloud: Introduction to Questioning

Decide on a thought-provoking image such as a photo, book without words, or book cover. Project the photo or image so that all student can see it.

Say: Take a look at this photo. What do you see? There is no text or story to support the picture. What kinds of questions does this picture make you curious to ask?

Write down one question per self-stick note as students call out. Help students consider many different perspectives of the photo or book with some guiding questions and statements. When students are finished generating questions, reread each self-stick note aloud to the class. Without explanation, place each self-stick note on the left or right side of the chart. Once all questions have been sorted and placed on the chart, ask the students why the questions might be sorted in this way.

Say: I placed some of the questions on the right side of the chart and some on the left side of the chart. Why do you think that is? What do you notice about the two groups of questions?

Allow time for students to think of an answer. Have students turn and talk, sharing their thoughts with a partner.

Ask: Why do you think I sorted the questions in this way?

Talk through students answers. Once students have communicated an accurate reason, label each side of the chart.

Say: The questions on the left side of the chart are questions that can be answered by information found in this picture. These are called “thin” questions. The answers are “right there.” The questions on the right side of the chart can only be answered by thinking, reasoning, and putting more than one piece of information together. These types of questions are called “thick” questions.

Say: Both types of questions are important. Readers use thick and thin questions to better understand what they are reading. When you begin at the Reading Workstation today, I would like you to be thinking about the types of questions you ask yourself as you are reading.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Practice reading independently.
  • Learn to generate and evaluate questions.

Additional Materials

  • Blank T-chart
  • Thought-provoking image
  • Self-Stick notes

Independent Reading (15 Min.)

Independent Reading: Recording Questions about Reading

Say: As you read, I would like you to write down any questions you have while reading today. You will be using self-stick notes to write down your questions. Write one question per self-stick note just like I did with the picture earlier.

Allow students the opportunity to choose a picture book from your collection or an independent reading book, get their self-stick notes, and get a pen or pencil before settling down to read. Walk around and monitor students as they read and ask questions. Make sure students have written at least three questions as they read.


Writing to Sources (30 Min.)

Thick and Thin Questions

Using the established signal, call students to the meeting area. Ask students to leave their independent reading books at their desks.

Say: We just finished practicing asking questions while reading. How did it go?

Say: I want us to take a closer look at the questions you asked when we looked at this photo. I organized the questions into two different categories. Can someone explain to the class what they remember about thick questions? Can someone explain to the class what they remember about thin questions?

Say: I would like you to sort your self-stick notes based on whether they were thick questions or thin questions. Think carefully about whether the answers to your questions are “right there” or if you have to put together more than one piece of information to determine an answer.

Have the students go back to their tables, and invite them to take turns sharing their questions and justifying their answers to their table team. Remind students to practice respectful conversations as they work together to complete the task.

Once the students have completed the task, ask if there are any questions. Distribute the thick/thin questions exit ticket.

Say: Now that we have practiced sorting thick and thin questions, I would like to see you put this new learning into words. Take a few minutes to write down your understanding of these questions.

Some optional exit tickets:

  • What is a thick question? Give an example.
  • What is a thin question? Give an example.
  • What is the difference between a thick and a thin question? Give an example of each.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Write about the type of questions being asked while reading.

Additional Materials

  • Thick and Thin Questions Chart
  • Blank Thick and Thin Charts (enough for each student to have their own or one per table team)
  • Students’ independent reading books or a basket of books
  • Thick/thin questions exit ticket
  • Self-Stick notes

Independent Writing (20 Min.)

Introduce the Computer Workstation

Determine how the Computer Workstation will operate in your classroom. Where will materials be stored? Where will students sit when they are at this station? What are the expected behaviors in this station?

Build a Computer Workstation chart with the class. Answer any questions students may have.

Computer Workstation
Activites Expectations
  • read books online
  • play learning games
  • do research
  • use an online search engine to research classroom assignments
  • work on a piece of writing
  • work independently or with a buddy
  • use an inside voice when working with a buddy
  • practice respectful conversations
  • clean up after yourself
  • help others keep the station neat and organized
  • be purposeful
  • be productive

Say: Let me show you how to use the computers.

Talk about how to turn the computers on and off, log into and off of the computer, use the headphones, adjust the volume, and access appropriate learning activities and word processing tools. Explain how to troubleshoot when there is a problem with the equipment.

Ask: What questions do you have?

Answer any questions the students may have. With any remaining time, either allow a group of students to practice at this workstation or arrange for the class to practice one-on-one.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Learn how the Computer Workstation works.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
