Establishing Routines (15 Min.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Creating a Collaborative Atmosphere within Reader’s Workshop

Have chart paper available as you talk with students about the skills needed to have respectful conversations. You may decide to have students in a whole-group sitting area.

Say: Today, we are going to learn some strategies for having respectful conversations. We will continue to practice our listening skills while adding speaking skills too.

Have a chart ready to fill in as you introduce each skill. Below is a suggested chart:

Respectful Conversations
  • Make eye contact with the speaker.
  • Shift body toward speaker.
  • Use appropriate facial expressions.
  • Say something meaningful.
  • Be a polite and thoughtful speaker.
  • Use stems when appropriate.

Say: Here are some stems that might be helpful when you are in a conversation.

Have stems available on sentence strips or chart paper to share with students. Some stems you might use are:

  • I like/don’t care for ______________ because _____________________.
  • I agree/disagree with you because ___________________.
  • Why do you think that?
  • Where can I find that in the text/story?
  • Could you explain what you mean?

Have picture cards or images to project for all students to see. Some examples might be: favorite season, favorite activity, or favorite food item.

Say: Now let’s practice a respectful conversation. I am going to show you a group of pictures. Decide which you like the best and why. Think about how you might share this information with a partner. Take turns speaking and listening. Remember, we are practicing respectful conversations, so think about your body language, making eye contact, and trying out some of our stems.

Give students a few minutes to practice. Pay attention to what students are saying and how they are talking with one another. When the group is finished talking, take a moment to praise students for their good listening and speaking skills. Offer a few suggestions for how students can strengthen their skills.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.
  • Learn strategies for having respectful conversations.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Sentence strips with stems for conversations
  • Pictures of images

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (25 Min.)

Read-Aloud: Understanding Author’s Purpose (to persuade)

Read any book from the suggested read-aloud list, or any other available book that is of interest to you. Choose a book that is meant to persuade an audience. Have the Author’s Purpose Chart available to review before beginning the read-aloud.

To Inform

to convince the reader of a certain point of view
(student examples) (student examples) (student examples)

Say: Yesterday we read a book that was meant to entertain. Today, we will read a story that the author wrote to persuade the audience. What does it mean to persuade?

Give students an opportunity to share what they know about the word.

Say: As I read, I invite you to think about what the author is trying to convince you of.

Read the book aloud to the class. Afterward, pair students up to practice respectful conversations using the suggested questions below or other questions you wish to use. Remind students to use the stems that were introduced at the beginning of Reader’s Workshop.

Suggested Questions:

  • What is the main point of the story?
  • What are some supporting details?
  • Did the author make the point successfully? Why or why not?
  • Do you agree or disagree with the point the author is trying to make?

Once the class has had a few minutes to talk with one another, ask them to finish up their conversations and turn themselves toward the front of the room. Briefly share what you noticed about the conversations. Pay particular attention to addressing positive attempts to use stems, body language, and eye contact appropriately. If needed, suggest skills the class can continue to work on.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.
  • Learn that some books are written to persuade.

Additional Materials

  • Author’s Purpose Anchor Chart
  • Markers
  • Selected persuasive book to read aloud
  • Additional examples of books that persuade
  • Questions for discussion

Independent Reading (20 Min.)

Independent Reading: Use a Reading Log

Students will read independently while you model reading independently and monitor the class.

Allow students fifteen minutes to read before directing them to begin completing their reading logs. Monitor students as they complete the reading logs. If needed, provide time to answer any questions before moving on.

Writing to Sources (15 Min.)

Model: Persuasive Writing

Using the established signal, Call students to the meeting area.

Say: The author in our read-aloud book wanted to convince the audience to ____________. The author was passionate about the topic. What might be some other topics a writer might have very strong opinions about?

Allow students time to think of some ideas. Call on students to share their ideas and chart them on paper. Choose one of the ideas listed to discuss in further detail. For example:

Say: Let’s look at this idea about school uniforms. What would be some benefits, or pros, to having uniforms in school? What are some challenges, or cons? Who is impacted by the decision to have school uniforms? Are there any other perspectives we need to consider? Who might the audience be for our writing today? Would it make sense for us to write a picture book about school uniforms? Considering our audience, would it be better to write a letter? Yes, I agree! Let’s get started. We are going to use a graphic organizer to help us write our letter.

Topic: School Uniforms (for) Topic: School Uniforms (against)
Opinion: School uniforms would be very beneficial for students, parents, and administrators. Opinion: School uniforms should not be required at (school’s name).
Reason 1: Uniforms would reduce the time it takes to decide what to wear to school in the morning. Reason 1: School uniforms are expensive.
Reason 2: Popularity would not be an issue because all students would be dressed the same. Reason 2: Uniforms do not allow students to express their own personalities.
Reason 3: Administrators would not have students coming to the office for dress code infractions. Reason 3: Uniforms would require parents to do more laundry.
Opinion: School uniforms offer many benefits to all members of the school community. Opinion: The are many challenges to requiring uniforms in school.

Say: Think about which letter you think we should write. Tomorrow, we will vote on whether to write a letter for school uniforms or against them.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Practice reading their selected books independently and filling in their reading log.

Additional Materials

  • Students’ independent reading books
  • Reading log

Independent Writing (10 Min.)

Persuasive Writing

Say: We are going to take some time to make a list of writing ideas. We have a pretty good list already on the board and I am guessing you have quite a few other ideas, too. You may use some of the ideas from our list and any others you may be thinking about. Label your paper “Persuasive Writing Ideas” and begin a list of ideas that could make a good opinion writing piece. Consider anything you might have a strong opinion about.

Allow students time to start their lists. Discuss where the lists can be stored, possibly in a writing folder or a process writing notebook.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Discuss some topics a writer might have opinons about.

Additional Materials

  • Read-aloud book from earlier
  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Response journals or loose leaf paper/writing folder

Support Tips

Decide on where the students are going to keep their writing. They can keep loose papers in a writing folder. They can also have a response journal that could be divided into sections for multiple uses. The sections could include: brainstorming ideas, interesting words, response to reading, drafts, and reading comprehension strategies.
