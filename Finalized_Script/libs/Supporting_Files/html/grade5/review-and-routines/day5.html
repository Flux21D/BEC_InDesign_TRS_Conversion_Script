Establishing Routines (10 Min.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Creating a Collaborative Atmosphere within Reader’s Workshop

Have the Respectful Conversations chart and sentence stems available as you review the skills needed to have respectful conversations. You may decide to have students in a whole-group sitting area for this activity or after directions have been given, students could stand next to their partners.

Say: This idea of respectful conversations is so important that we are going to continue practicing it today and probably in the future again, too. Take a look at our chart from yesterday. Which skills on the chart do you think are most helpful to use in a conversation? Why?

Allow students the opportunity to share their thinking with the whole group. Remind students that respectful conversations happen both in small groups and in the large group, so there are many opportunities in the day to practice.

Say: I am going to put up another set of pictures to choose from. This time, you will work with a new partner. Take a moment to look at the pictures and decide which activity you like the best and why. Think about how you might share this information with a partner. Take turns speaking and listening. Remember, we are practicing respectful conversations so think about your body language, making eye contact, and trying out some of our stems.

Give students a few minutes to practice. Pay attention to what students are saying and how they are talking with one another. When the group is finished talking, take a moment to praise students for their good listening and speaking skills. Offer a few suggestions for how students can strengthen their skills.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.
  • Practice respectful conversations.

Additional Materials

  • Markers
  • Respectful Conversations anchor cChart
  • Sentence strips with stems for conversations
  • Picture cards or images

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (25 Min.)

Read-Aloud: Understanding Author’s Purpose (to inform)

Read any book from the suggested read-aloud list, or any other available book that is of interest to you. Choose a book that is meant to inform an audience. Have the Author’s Purpose Chart available to review before beginning the read-aloud.

Say: Yesterday we read a book that was meant to persuade. Today, we will read a story that the author wrote to inform the audience. What does it mean to inform?

Give students an opportunity to share what they know about the word.

Say: As I read, I invite you to think about what the author is trying to explain.

Read the book aloud to the class. Choose a few questions to monitor students’ understanding of the book and how it informs the reader.

Suggested Questions:
  • What is this story about?
  • What are some of facts we learned by reading the story?
  • Did the book teach us about ____________ or explain the topic in more detail?

Pay particular attention to positive attempts by students to use stems, appropriate body language, and eye contact. If needed, suggest skills the class can continue to work on.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.
  • Learn that some books are written to inform.

Additional Materials

  • Author’s Purpose Anchor Chart
  • Markers
  • Selected book to read aloud, chosen for the purpose of informing
  • Additional books to use as examples of books that inform

Independent Reading (25 Min.)

Introduce the Reading Workstation

Determine how the Reading Workstation will operate in your classroom. Where will materials be stored? Where will students sit when they are at this station? What are the expected behaviors in this station?

Say: We have been building stamina with our reading this week. Occasionally, you will have the opportunity to practice independent reading within a workstation. We have practiced some of the activities that you may choose to do when you are in the Reading Workstation. Let’s go over the expectations. Mainly, you will be reading. You might be reading a book you are currently in the middle of, previewing a new book from our classroom library, or completing a reading log.

Say: Whenever you are in the Reading Workstation, you will be expected to fill out your reading log. Later on, we will learn about buddy reading and that will also be something you can do at this workstation. What questions might you have about the Reading Workstation?

Take a moment to answer any questions.

Ask: What behaviors do you think I will expect of you when you are at the Reading Workstation?

Call on a few students to respond. Record any answers that match your expectations for behavior. Some suggestions can be found below:

Reading Workstation
Activities Expectations
  • independent reading (both silent and whisper-reading)
  • buddy reading
  • previewing a new book
  • researching a topic of interest
  • completing a graphic organizer
  • work independently or with a buddy
  • use an inside voice when working with a buddy
  • clean up after yourself
  • help others keep the station neat and organized
  • be purposeful
  • be productive

Say: Let me show you how the library is organized.

Talk about the different features of the classroom library. For example, explain how the books are organized (by author, level, interest, or author’s purpose). Talk about where to find a dictionary, thesaurus, extra reading log, and any additional resources they may need. Tell students where they can sit, whether it is in a carpeted area, around the room, or at desks.

Ask: What questions do you have?

Answer any questions the students may have. With any remaining time, allow students to practice at this workstation by beginning their independent reading books. Allow time at the end for students to fill in their reading logs.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Learn about the expectations while at the Reading Workstation.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers

Writing to Sources (20 Min.)

Model: Persuasive Writing

If needed, provide an opportunity for students to stretch or take a brain break. Have the graphic organizer from yesterday’s lesson available for all students to see.

Say: Yesterday we thought about the pros and cons of children being required to wear school uniforms. Let’s decide which letter we are going to write today. Raise your hand if you would like to write a letter about the benefits of school uniforms. Now raise your hand if you would prefer to write about the benefits of not having school uniforms. Now, who should we write this letter to? Ok, let’s take our ideas and turn them into a letter.

Topic: School Uniforms (for) Topic: School Uniforms (against)
Opinion: School uniforms would be very beneficial for students, parents, and administrators. Opinion: School uniforms should not be required at (school’s name).
Reason 1: Uniforms would reduce the time it takes to decide what to wear to school in the morning. Reason 1: School uniforms are expensive.
Reason 2: Popularity would not be an issue because all students would be dressed the same. Reason 2: Uniforms do not allow students to express their own personalities.
Reason 3: Administrators would not have students coming to the office for dress-code infractions. Reason 3: Uniforms would require parents to do more laundry.
Opinion: School uniforms offer many benefits to all members of the school community. Opinion: There are many challenges to requiring uniforms in school.

Model how you expect students to write letters. This may include a greeting, a date, and a closing. You may also choose to be very specific regarding the number of paragraphs. If desired, the content in the graphic organizer can be transferred directly to a one-paragraph letter, or it can be expanded to include more paragraphs with greater detail.

Independent Writing (30 Min.)

Persuasive Writing

Have students write independently for fifteen minutes while you monitor the class. Depending on the needs of your class, you may decide that they are ready to begin writing their own opinion letters. If the class needs a more structured writing experience, consider using the graphic organizer to write a letter from the opposing view.

If you choose to have students write a new opinion piece,

Say: Now that we have practiced using a graphic organizer to plan and write our letters, we are going to write our own. Take out your list of opinion writing topic ideas. Put a star next to the one you would like to start writing today. I will pass out a graphic organizer for you to start planning your piece. We will take about ten minutes to get our ideas down on paper.

Monitor students as they begin completing the graphic organizer. Support students who many need additional help narrowing down their topic or clarifying their position on the issue. After ten minutes, use a signal to gather the attention of the students.

Use a Stand-Up, Hand-Up, Pair-Up activity to divide the class into pairs. Tell partners to take turns discussing their topics. Remind students to practice having respectful conversations and refer to the anchor chart if needed.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Practice the skill of writing an opinion letter.

Additional Materials

  • Persuasive Writing Topic Chart from Day 4
  • Markers
  • Response journals or loose-leaf paper/writing folder
  • Respectful Conversations Anchor Chart
