Establishing Routines (5 MIN.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to themeeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Creating a Collaborative Atmosphere within Reader’s Workshop

Draw students’ attention to the Reader’s Workshop T-chart created yesterday.

Say: Let’s review the skills that help you be a successful reader in Reader’s Workshop.

Reader’s Workshop Needed Skills
  • practice reading
  • write about reading
  • talk about reading
  • work independently
  • work with a partner
  • work in small groups
  • work with the teacher
  • be able to follow directions
  • be able to listen actively
  • be able to talk about my reading
  • be able to write about my reading
  • be responsible for my learning
  • be responsible for others
  • be responsible for the classroom materials

Say: Today we are going to focus on the skill of listening.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Review the skills needed and expected during Reader’s Workshop.

Additional Materials

  • Reader’s Workshop chart

Support Tips

Reading, writing, and word study assessments may be administered throughout the week while students are working independently.

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (40 MIN.)

Read-Aloud: Build Good Listening Habits

Write the words “Active Listener” on a separate chart.

Say: Today we are going to read a book. As I read, think about what it means to be a good listener.

Read any book from the suggested read-aloud list, or any other available book.

Conduct a brief discussion about what people do to be active listeners. List suggestions given by the students on the anchor chart. Some important things to include could be:

Active Listener
  • Have eyes on the speaker.
  • Maintain a quiet body.
  • Use appropriate expressions to show interest.
  • Be patient while the speaker chooses what to say. Give enough wait time.
  • Think carefully about what the speaker is saying.

Say: In our discussions we are going to be mindful of how we listen. Now let’s practice. We are going to line up to find partners. When I tell you to, you are going to line up around the room according to your birth month. For example, if you were born in January, you would stand here. If you were born in December, you would stand over there. If you were born in June or July, you would line up somewhere in the middle.

Predetermine a starting and ending point for your line. Remind students to spread out across the room to give all students space. Determine what signal you will use to get the attention of the group. For example, you can put your hand in the air to signal to them that they need to put their hand up in the air, stop what they are doing, and look at you.

Say: Once everyone is in place, I will use this signal (clapping pattern or raised hand) to bring your attention back to me. Are there any questions about what we will be doing?

Answer any questions students might have.

Say: Let’s go ahead and line up according to your birth month.

Allow students a few minutes to get in their spaces and use one of the hand signals to get their attention. Praise students on how quickly and quietly they were able to line up.

Say: Now everyone will take turns sharing their name and the month they were born. For example, when it’s my turn, I will say, “Hi, I’m ____________ and I was born in _______.” As everyone shares, please remember to practice your active listening skills. Let’s pay particular attention to the skills of quiet bodies and eyes on the speaker.

Ask students to go down the line sharing their names and birth months. Praise students for showing good active listening skills during the activity. Next have the students count off 1-2-1-2. As they number off, direct the students to partner in their 1-2 combinations.

Decide what you want your students to be discussing in their partner groups:

  • Students can answer comprehension questions based on the read-aloud. These questions should be projected or somehow visible for all students.
  • Students could play Would You Rather? In this game, the teacher poses questions that give students a choice. These should be projected or somehow visible for all students. The students choose and justify their answers. For example: would you rather be able to stop time or fly? Would you rather always lose or never play?

Say: Now, in your partner group, each partner will take turns asking and answering a question. In round one, Partner 1 will start by asking the question while Partner 2 answers. Then Partner 2 will ask Partner 1 the same question and listen to Partner 1 answer. In round two, Partner 2 asks the question first while Partner 1 answers. Partner 1 asks Partner 2 the same question and listens to Partner 1 answer. Let’s begin. Partner 1, here’s your first question.

Give about thirty seconds for Partner 2 to answer before switching roles. Continue for two or three more questions. When students have had sufficient time to practice, call the class back to the whole-group meeting area to debrief.

Ask: How did it feel to practice listening? What was hard? What was easy? What questions do you have about how we are expected to listen while we are together?




Objectives

Students will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.
  • Discuss and practice how to be active listeners.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Selected book to read aloud

Independent Reading (20 MIN.)

Practice Independent Reading

Say: Yesterday, we each chose a good book for independent reading time and we practiced writing a response to our reading. Today, I will introduce the reading log.

Have an example of the reading log you plan to introduce. Be prepared to project the reading log so all can see, or have student copies available to distribute at this time.

Say: We use a reading log to document the reading we do both in school and out of school. This log helps us look at your book choices. Strong readers read many different genres. For example, a strong reader might read a fiction book, then a graphic novel, an anthology of poems, and a nonfiction book. This year we will be exposed to a wide range of genres and I hope that your reading log reflects a wide range of choices, too.

Take time to walk students through the reading log and how to fill it in accurately.

Say: We are going to practice completing our reading logs today after we read independently. Please take a moment to get your independent reading books and find a quiet place in the room to begin your reading. I will give you a signal when it is time to stop reading and begin filling in your reading log for today.

Allow students fifteen minutes to read independently before directing them to begin completing their reading logs. Monitor students as they complete the reading logs. If needed, provide time to answer any questions before moving on.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Practice reading independently for a predetermined amount of time.

Additional Materials

  • Books students selected for independent reading during the previous lesson
  • Reading Log

Writing to Sources (15 MIN.)

Model: A Response to Reading

Using the established signal, call students to the meeting area. Invite the students to think back to the read-aloud book shared earlier.

Writing can be done on chart paper, the board, or any projection device available so that the writing will be visible to the whole class.

Say: Today, we are going to continue practicing writing a response to our reading. We will do this by using a question jar.

Have two jars available, one for fiction books and one for nonfiction books. Some sample questions that can be written on scraps of paper include:

Fiction Question Jar:
  • Did this book make you laugh? Cry? Cringe? Smile? Cheer? Explain with evidence from the text.
  • What connections are there between the book and any other book you’ve read? Explain with evidence from the text.
  • What is the most important event in the book? The most important passage?
  • Do you like the ending of the book? Why or why not? Do you think there is more to tell?
  • What parts of the book seem most believable or unbelievable? Why?
  • How are you or people you know like any of the characters? Explain with evidence from the text.


Nonfiction Question Jar:
  • Make a list of what new things you have learned by reading this.
  • How is the information that you have read similar or different from other texts about the subject?
  • What is the main idea of your book? List three details that support the main idea.
  • What pictures, illustrations, diagrams or photographs are helpful? Explain how and why they help.
  • Explain why a friend should or should not read this book.
  • What part of the book was the most interesting? Why did this stick out?
  • What questions would you ask the author?
  • If you were the author, explain what you would do differently.
  • Compare and contrast two individuals, events, or concepts from the selection.

Say: Since our read-aloud book was a fiction book, we are going to pull a question from the Fiction Question Jar. Who would like to pull a question for us today?

Explain to students that sometimes a question that is pulled isn’t a good match for the book. For example, if a student hasn’t finished reading a book, a question about whether you liked the ending or not would not work. You might decide to let students choose two or three questions, then pick the one they are most interested in answering.

Once a question has been chosen, ask the student to read the question aloud.

Say: Let’s begin our response to the reading. Take a moment to think back to our readaloud. Think about how you would start your response to this book. Who would like to share their idea?

Allow two or three students an opportunity to share their ideas. Use these ideas to begin crafting a shared response to the read-aloud book. Remind students to use proper punctuation and complete sentences when writing.

Ask: What kinds of things do I need to keep in mind as I write?

Look for student answers about capital letters at the beginning of sentences, punctuation at the end of sentences, and writing in complete sentences.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Learn to write using a prompt.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Book read aloud earlier
  • Independent reading books
  • Two question jars, one for fiction questions and the other for nonfiction questions
  • Response journals or loose leaf paper/writing folder

Support Tips

Word study components can be added to the shared writing section based on your students’ needs. Some that could be taught this week are: vowel teams /ou/, /oi/ and /ô/, /oo/, homophones (to, two, too; they’re, their, there), silent letters (wr, kn, gn), and inflectional endings with spelling changes (drop final e, double final consonant).

Independent Writing (15 MIN.)

Response Journals

Invite the students to return to their seats and write a response to the book they chose for independent reading time using their response journals or loose leaf paper.

Say: Now it is your turn to write a response to the book you chose earlier. Could I get another volunteer to pull a new question from the question jar?

Choose another student to pull a question and read aloud to the class. If some students need a non-fiction question, allow a student to pull a non-fiction question also.

Say: Please remember to write in complete sentences while using appropriate punctuation and capital letters and be sure to answer the question as you write. Be sure to cite evidence from the text.

During the last five minutes, allow students the opportunity to share how it felt to write a response to reading using a question from the question jar. Share with students where the question jars will be kept in the room for independent use.
