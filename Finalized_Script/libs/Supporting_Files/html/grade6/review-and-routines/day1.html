Establishing Routines (15 Min.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction:Creating a Collaborative Atmosphere within Reader’s Workshop

Say: When you think about Reader’s Workshop, what comes to mind? Turn and talk with a partner about what Reader’s Workshop means to you.

Allow a few minutes for discussion. When you hear a lull in the conversation, invite students back to the whole group.

Ask: What are some ideas that you and your partner discussed?

Create a T-chart with one side labeled “Reader’s Workshop” and the other side labeled “Needed Skills.” Chart student responses on the Reader’s Workshop side of the chart. Generate a list of activities on chart paper that would be included in Reader’s Workshop as provided by the students. Once the chart has been created, ask the students to look over the chart.

Ask: What skills would help you be successful as a reader in Reader’s Workshop?

Chart student responses on the Needed Skills side of the chart.

Reader’s Workshop Needed Skills
  • practice reading
  • write about reading
  • talk about reading
  • work independently
  • work with a partner
  • work in small groups
  • work with the teacher
  • be able to follow directions
  • be able to listen actively
  • be able to talk about my reading
  • be able to write about my reading
  • be responsible for my learning
  • be responsible for others
  • be responsible for the classroom materials

Go over the Needed Skills side of the chart.

Say: These are the skills we will work on and will be expected to follow to help all of us be successful as readers during Reader’s Workshop.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Discuss the skills needed and expected during Reader’s Workshop.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers

Support Tips

Throughout the lessons, there are scripts available for the teacher to say aloud to the students. These suggested scripts and anchor charts can be modified to accommodate the needs of the class.

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (30 Min.)

Read-Aloud: Choosing a Good Book for Independent Reading

Read any book from the suggested read-aloud list, or any other available book that is of interest to you.

Say: Today we will be reading (say title and author/illustrator). Take a look at the cover. What do you think this book will be about? Why do you think I selected this book to read to you today?

Solicit a few student responses before explaining your decision for choosing this book. Example:

  • I chose _____ by _____ because it has to do with _____, which is of interest to me.
  • I chose _____ by _____ because the illustrations caught my eye.
  • I chose _____ by _____ because I have read another book by the same author.

Read the selected book aloud to the students.

Say: I chose this book to read to you today because (give reason why you chose the book). What are some other reasons why you would select a book to read?

On chart paper, list reasons why students would select a book to read, such as interest in topic, favorite author/illustrator, part of a series, recommendation, genre, and reading level.

Say: Now you will have the opportunity to select a book to read independently. You will have about five minutes to select a book from (the classroom library, bins around the room, or any other place in the classroom where books are available for selection).

After five minutes have passed, signal for the students to direct their attention to you. Ask the students to share with a partner the reason why they chose the book. This can be done by using a structure like Stand-Up, Hand-Up, Pair-Up.

Say: We are going to do an activity called Stand-Up, Hand-Up, Pair-Up. When I tell you to, you will stand up, put your hand up, and pair up with the first person you make eye contact with. Once everyone has a partner, take time to share your book title and the reason you chose this book.

Help students connect with a partner in the room and develop a group of three if there is an odd number of students in the class. Repeat the Stand-Up, Hand-Up, Pair-Up directions so that students have the opportunity to partner a couple of times. Then allow for independent reading for at least fifteen minutes. Decide and share with the students where the independent reading should take place.

Objectives

Students will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.
  • Discuss reasons to select a book to read.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Selected book to read aloud
  • Collection of leveled books for independent reading

Independent Reading (15 Min.)

Introduce Independent Reading

Students will read independently for fifteen minutes while you model reading independently and monitor the class.

Writing to Sources (15 Min.)

Model: A Response to Reading

Using the established signal, call students to the meeting area.

Invite the students to think back to the read-aloud book shared earlier.

Shared writing could be done on chart paper, the board, or any projection device available to you that will allow the writing to be visible to the whole class.

Say: Sometimes in Reader’s Workshop I might ask you to write a response to your reading that includes ideas, facts, events, and/or arguments presented in the text. Today I am going to model that with the read-aloud book I chose. Then you will be practicing on your own. Again, what are some of the reasons we would choose to read a book? Notice that I will cite text evidence in my response.

Review with the students the reason you chose this particular book and encourage them to help you compose a written response. While you are writing, state the name of the book and your reason for choosing it. Then include how you felt about the selection and whether or not you would do anything differently next time.

Ask: What kinds of things do I need to keep in mind as I write?

Look for student answers about writing capital letters at the beginning of sentences, using punctuation at the end of sentences, and writing in complete sentences.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Actively listen and participate in a group discussion about reasons to choose a book and express it in writing.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Book that was read aloud during previous section

Independent Writing (15 Min.)

Response Journals

Invite the students to return to their seats and write a response to the book they chose for independent reading time using their response journals or loose-leaf paper to be kept in a folder.

Say: Now it is your turn to write a response to the book you chose earlier. Please remember to write in complete sentences while using appropriate punctuation and capital letters. Please remember to cite evidence from the text as you write.

During the last five minutes, share with the students where their independent reading books and response journals or folders will be kept. This could be individual book boxes, book bags, desks, or cubbies.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Write independently about the reason they chose to read the book they selected.

Additional Materials

  • Response journals or loose-leaf paper/writing folder
  • Baskets of books for independent reading

Support Tips

Decide on where the students are going to keep their writing. They can keep loose papers in a writing folder. They can also have a response journal that could be divided into sections for multiple uses. The sections could include: brainstorming ideas, interesting words, response to reading, drafts, and reading comprehension strategies.
