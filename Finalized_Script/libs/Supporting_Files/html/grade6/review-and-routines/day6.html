Establishing Routines (20 MIN.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Activating Prior Knowledge

Choose a book that is meant to inform the reader, possibly one that is connected to a Social Studies or Science unit you are currently studying. Have copies of a blank KWL chart available to pass out to the class.

Say: Today, we are going to read the book (say name and author/illustrator). Take a look at the cover. What do you think this book is going to be about?

Say: Let’s take a look through the book at the different pictures, illustrations, and headings. What do the pictures and headings tell you?

Ask: What do you think the purpose of this book is?

Allow time for students to discuss in whole group.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.

Additional Materials

  • Chart Paper
  • Markers
  • Selected book for read-aloud

Support Tips

Reading, writing, and word study assessments may be administered throughout the week while students are working independently.

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (25 Min.)

Read-Aloud: Using a KWL Chart to Document Learning

Say: This is a KWL chart. We can use a chart like this to note what we learn from reading an informational book. The K stands for “know.” The W stands for “what I want to know.” The L stands for “what I learned.” What do you already know about this topic? Let’s write your answers down in the “know” section of the chart.

Allow students to share what they already know about the topic while you record their answers in the chart.

Book Topic

K

What I think I know

• W

What I want to know

L

What I learned

Say: Let’s think about what we might like to learn from reading this book. The book is about _______, so would it make sense that we would learn about ______? (choose a topic that would not be information learned from the book) It doesn’t make sense that we would learn ________ by reading this book. When filling out this section, we should use the all of the information we have found from the cover, the pictures, and the headings to complete the W section of the graphic organizer.

Say: Turn and talk with a partner about what you would like to learn by reading this book. Please remember our strategies for respectful conversations.

Give students enough time to share their ideas with one another.

Ask: Who would like to share something they would like to learn?

Add student responses to the W section of the chart.

Say: We will fill out the L section of the organizer after we read the story.

Read the book aloud while stopping to define unknown words or ask clarifying questions. Some possible questions might be:

  • What does the author mean by saying _________?
  • What do you think the word _______ means in this story? What clues helped you figure that out?

Say: Now that we have read the book, let’s finish the last section of the KWL chart. What did we learn from reading this story?

Add student responses to the L section of the chart.

Say: Look at all the information we were able to get from this book! We really learned a lot! I am guessing there are still some pieces of information that we can learn from rereading this book. I am going to put it in our classroom library so that you can read the book again while you are in the reading workstation.




Objectives

Student will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Selected book to read aloud
  • KWL chart

Indpenedent Reading (20 Min.)

Practice the Reading Workstation

Say: Yesterday we practiced the Reading Workstation. Take a moment to think about what we do in the Reading Workstation. What do you remember? What are things we might choose to do in this workstation?

Ask: What behaviors should I see when you are in this station?

Refer to the Reading Workstation chart as needed.

Reading Workstation
Activities Expectations
  • independent reading (both silent and whisper reading)
  • buddy reading
  • previewing a new book
  • researching a topic of interest
  • completing a graphic organizer
  • work independently or with a buddy
  • use an inside voice when working with a buddy
  • clean up after yourself
  • help others keep the station neat and organized
  • be purposeful
  • be productive

Say: We are going to practice this workstation again today. When I give you the signal, please get settled quickly and quietly.

Give enough time at the end for students to complete their reading logs. Answer any questions students might have about the reading workstation.

Building the Writing Process (15 Min.)

Model: Sharing Our Writing

Using the established signal, call students to the meeting area.

Say: Sharing our writing with others is an important part of the writing process. We get the opportunity to hear how other writers organize their thoughts and use words. The listener gets to make suggestions or offer advice to the writer. The writer gets an chance to see how others respond to their writing and to listen to feedback. Here are some ways you might choose to share your opinion:

  • I like the way you described ___________. It made me feel __________.
  • I liked the ending because ___________.
  • I liked the part where you talked about _____________.

You could choose to model what a listener does not do when listening to someone else share their writing (fidget, look around the room, ask if they are finished yet). Afterward, invite a student to model what a listener does do (face the speaker, listen carefully, make eye contact, say what they liked about the piece).

Ask: Are there any questions about how to share your writing?

Say: Let’s practice. We are going to take a moment to get a piece of our writing we would like to share. Right now, we have a few different responses to reading to choose from. You might choose your question jar writing, your lifting a line writing, or your opinion writing piece.

Say: Now that everyone has chosen a writing piece, let’s partner up to practice sharing our writing. Choose one person to go first. Remember, sit facing each other, pay attention to the reader, and be thoughtful with your feedback.

As students take turns sharing, walk around the room, monitor student conversation, praise and redirect when appropriate.

Look for student answers about capital letters at the beginning of sentences, punctuation at the end of sentences, and writing in complete sentences.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Practice meeting the expectations while at the Reading Workstation.

Additional Materials

  • Reading Workstation chart
  • Reading logs
  • Students’ independent reading books

Independent Writing (20 Min.)

Introduce the Writing Workstation

Say: Today, we are going to discuss the Writing Workstation. We have had lots of practice writing for different purposes so far this year. Think back to the different opportunities we have had to write. What do you remember?

Students might say things like questions in a jar, lifting a line, or opinion letters.

Say: We have also learned how to share our writing. All of these writing experiences are ones that you can choose to do in the writing workstation. Throughout the year, we will add to the list of choices. Let’s make a chart for what we have already. Now, let’s think about the expectations we will have for each other while in this station. We can use the Reading Workstation chart to help us.

Writing Workstation
Activities Expectations
  • question in a jar
  • lift a line
  • opinion letters
  • share your writing
  • writing to sources
  • begin a new piece of writing
  • work independently or with a buddy
  • use an inside voice when working with a buddy
  • practice respectful conversations
  • clean up after yourself
  • help others keep the station neat and organized
  • be purposeful
  • be productive

Say: We will have an opportunity to practice this workstation tomorrow.




Objectives

Student will:

  • Practice how to share their writing with others.

Additional Materials

  • Response journals or loose leaf paper/writing folders
  • Chart paper
  • Markers

Support Tips

Based on your students’ needs. word study components can be taught this week by being added to the shared writing section, for example: related root words (addition, additional), irregular plural nouns (feet, children), -er/-or and –y/-ly endings, and contractions (‘ll, ‘ve, ‘m)..
