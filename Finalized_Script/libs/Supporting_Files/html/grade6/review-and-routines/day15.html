Establishing Routines (5 Min.)

Establishing Workstation Routines

Have a Rotation chart with student assignments ready to share with the class.

Say: Once again, we are going to practice having a few different workstations going at the same time. We will use this workstation chart to help us know what station we are supposed to be working in.

Explain to the students how to read the chart and who will be going where first. Remind students of the expectations for each workstation and call students to move to the appropriate station to get started. Go over the signal they need to listen for that will mean they need to clean up their station and move to the next one. Also remind the students that when they get to each station, they can read the chart to remind them the choices they have once they are there.

As the students are working at the stations, you may walk around and monitor students as they work at the different stations. Answer any questions students may have or redirect students that need additional support.

If you and the students feel comfortable with how the workstations are working, you may call a few students to come to your guided reading table and start working with small groups. You may also use this time for reading, writing, or word study assessments.

Repeat the rotational process three more times so that students have practiced getting started, cleaning up, and rotating to a new station a total of four times.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between workstations.

Additional Materials

  • Rotation chart

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (25 Min.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Annotations

Say: Yesterday we went over some annotations to make while reading, which helps us understand the text better. Let’s go over what they each mean again.

Show the students the following chart from Texts for Close Reading:

Symbol Purpose
Identify a key detail.
Star an important idea in the margin.
Mark a sequence of events.
Circle a key word or phrase.
Mark a question you have about information in the text. Write your question in the margin.
Indicate an idea in the text you find interesting. Comment on this idea in the margin.

After discussing each, tell students that these notes should be written along the margins of the text. If the text is not theirs, then they can use sticky notes to do their annotating.

Say: I have another passage for us to read today. First I will read the whole passage aloud to you. Then I will have you partner up. I will give each pair a copy of the passage. You can reread the passage together and make the necessary annotations for you and your partner.

Read the passage aloud. Have the students pair up with a partner and give each pair of students a copy of the passage. Allow time for reading and annotating. Assign them to go back to their tables or let them choose an appropriate place around the room to work in.

Use your signal to call everyone back to the meeting place after about ten minutes and reread the first paragraph of the passage.

Ask: Did anyone make any annotations next to the first paragraph?

Have a pair of students share their annotation. Then have another pair share. Discuss how both annotations are important for the individuals reading the passage. Continue this activity with the rest of the paragraphs.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.
  • Practice annotating text.

Independent Reading Workstation
