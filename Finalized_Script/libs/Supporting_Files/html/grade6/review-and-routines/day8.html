Establishing Routines (20 MIN.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Making Connections to Text

Have the Text-to-Text Connections chart available for all students to see.

Tell students that there are many things in a book that they may make connections with. Invite them to add to the chart.

Say: Making connections is one strategy good readers use to understand a text. While reading, you may be reminded of something personal in your life, another story you have read, or possibly something that is happening or has happened in the world. Turn and talk to a partner about any connection you made to the book that I read aloud yesterday.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.

Additional Materials

  • Markers

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (25 Min.)

Read-Aloud: Making Connections to Text

Say: Today, I am going to read the story (say name and author/illustrator) and share with you the connections I make to other books that I have read.

As you read the story, stop and share the connections you are making. Think aloud using a two-part explanation for each connection:

  • The part about __________ in the book reminds me of ________ in the book ___________.
  • What I just read reminds me of __________ (story/book/movie/song) because __________.
  • The ideas in this text are similar to the ideas in __________ because __________.
  • The ideas in this text are different than the ideas in ____________ because ________.

Ask: How does making text-to-text connections help you understand and comprehend the text better?




Objectives

Students will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.

Additional Materials

  • Preselected book for read-aloud

Independent Reading (20 Min.)

Introducing the Listening Workstation

Determine how the Listening Workstation will operate in your classroom. Where will materials be stored? Where will students sit when they are at this station? What are the expected behaviors in this station?

Build a Listening Workstation chart with the class. Answer any questions students may have.

Listening Workstation
Activities Expectations
  • listen individually
  • listen with a buddy
  • work independently or with a buddy
  • use an inside voice when working with a buddy
  • troubleshoot equipment with a buddy before asking a teacher
  • clean up after yourself
  • help others keep the station neat and organized
  • be purposeful
  • be productive

Say: Let me show you how to use the computers and audio players.

Talk about the different features of the Listening Workstation. Explain how to access the books and how to troubleshoot when there is a problem with the equipment. Talk about where the students can sit, whether it is in a carpeted area, around the room, or at desks.

Ask: What questions do you have?

Answer any questions the students may have. With any remaining time, either allow groups of students to practice this workstation, or arrange for a whole-group listening opportunity. If your school does not have books on tape or CDs, there are many recorded books available online through video and audio-streaming websites, as well as at the public library.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Learn the expectations and activities to be accomplished at the Listening Workstation.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers

Writing to Sources (15 Min.)

Model: Writing about Connections to Text

Say: You have heard my text-to-text connections to the read-aloud book. Now, we are going to look at how to write about those connections. We are going to practice expanding a connection.

Flip through the book and remind students of the different connections you made while reading. Choose one to write about.

Think aloud as you write, stopping to ask questions or clarify student thinking.

Say: Now, think about the text-to-text connections you made when listening to the book. Turn and talk to a partner about one of your connections.

Once students are finished sharing with a partner, ask the class to share some of their text-to-text connections with the whole group.


Independent Writing (25 Min.)

Writing about Connections to Text

Say: If you have a good idea of what text-to-text connection you are going to write about, you may go back to your seats quietly and begin writing.

Work with any students still sitting in the whole-group meeting area to clarify their thinking and help determine the connection they will write about.

If there is time at the end, allow students the opportunity to pair off and share writing.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Learn how to write about connections made while reading.

Additional Materials

  • Book read aloud previously
  • Chart paper
  • Markers
