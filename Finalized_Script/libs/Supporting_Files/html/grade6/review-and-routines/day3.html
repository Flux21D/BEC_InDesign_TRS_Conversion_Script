Establishing Routines (5 MIN.)

Smooth Transitions

If students sit at tables or in groups, you may want to call one table at a time to transition to the whole-group meeting area by using a signal, such as a hand bell, clapping, or flashing the lights. Remind students to push the chair in quietly and walk silently to the meeting area.

Getting Ready for Whole-Group Instruction: Creating a Collaborative Atmosphere within Reader’s Workshop

Draw students’ attention to the Active Listener anchor chart created previously.

Say: We have been working on listening actively and writing about our reading. Today we will continue to practice both.

Active Listener
  • Have eyes on the speaker.
  • Maintain a quiet body.
  • Use appropriate expressions to show interest.
  • Be patient while the speaker chooses what to say. Give enough wait time.
  • Think carefully about what the speaker is saying.

Say: We are going to play a listening game. I am going to start with a word. The next person in line (identify student by name) will say a different word that begins with last letter in my word. The following student (identify second student by name) will say a new word that begins with the last letter of the word said by (Student 1). For example, I might say “pail.” (Student 1) might say “letter.” (Student 2) might say “read.” What questions do you have?

Say: We are going to be patient and wait quietly while our classmates choose their word. An important part of listening includes waiting for someone to think about their answers or ideas. Please listen carefully to everyone and pay attention for your turn.

Choose a word to start off the game. Once everyone has had a turn, you can choose to go around again or identify a student to pick a word to start a new round. Make sure students are speaking loudly enough to be heard by everyone in the group. Praise students for following directions and actively listening to their classmates.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Transition efficiently between mini-lessons.
  • Practice active listening.

Additional Materials

  • Active Listener anchor chart

Read-Aloud and Book Discussion (25 MIN.)

Read-Aloud: Understanding Author’s Purpose (to entertain)

Read any book from the suggested read-aloud list, or any other available book that is of interest to you. Choose a book that is meant to entertain an audience. Have a three-column chart available to fill in after the read-aloud. Leave enough space at the bottom of the chart for students to add sticky notes with the names of the books they are reading as examples of the different purposes for reading.

Author’s Purpose

To Inform

to teach or give information to the reader

To Entertain

to hold the interest of the reader through enjoyment

To Persuade

to convince the reader of a certain point of view
(student examples) (student examples) (student examples)

Say: Books are written for a variety of reasons. Thinking about the author’s purpose helps readers understand the book better. Today we are going to read a book called (say title and author/illustrator). As I read, I invite you to think about why the author may have written this story. After I am finished reading, we will share our thinking.

Read the book aloud to the class. Afterward, discuss with the class what the story was about. Some questions you might ask include: Where did the story take place? How did the character(s) change in this story? What did you learn from this book?

Say: Think about the story. Why do you think the author wrote this book?

Allow time for students to turn and talk, then invite students to share their ideas with the whole group. Complete the “To Entertain” section of the Author’s Purpose anchor chart. You can then share the other titles you have chosen as examples of books that inform or persuade. Consider reading the title, author, back cover, or even an important excerpt from the book to help students make connections between the different purposes for reading. Complete the other two sections of the anchor chart.

Ask students to retrieve the book they have been reading independently over the past few days. Students can either come back to the whole-group meeting area or stay at their desks.

Say: Take a look through your independent reading books. Do any of you think you have a book whose purpose it is to entertain?

Call on some students to share their books and their reasoning. If you agree, ask students to write their name, the name of their book, and the author on a self- note to be placed in the appropriate spot on the anchor chart. You might consider asking students to write whether the book is a fiction or nonfiction book as well.

Continue asking students to share and add their self-stick notes to the chart. Follow the same process for books that persuade and then again for books that inform.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Listen and respond to a story.
  • Learn that some books are written to entertain.

Additional Materials

  • Markers
  • Three-column chart
  • Selected book to read aloud, chosen for the purpose of entertainment
  • Students’ books used for independent reading
  • Additional books to use as examples of books that either inform or persuade

Independent Reading (25 MIN.)

Independent Reading: Previewing Books

Using the established signal, call students to the meeting area. Have a few books that you have chosen in advance to use when modeling the skills of previewing.

Say: Over the past few days, we have practiced choosing good books to read. We talked about choosing a book because of its topic, front cover, and even the author. Today, we are going to learn a few more strategies for choosing a good book. We are going to learn how to preview books. Watch and listen to me as I preview a book.

Depending on the book, you may choose to model any of the suggested strategies from the sample anchor chart or create some of your own. Once you have modeled previewing a book, ask students to share what they saw and heard. Generate a Previewing anchor chart together.

Previewing a New Book
  • Read the title.
  • Look at the cover art and design.
  • Read the back cover.
  • Browse the pictures and illustrations.
  • Read chapter titles or table of contents.
  • Read the first paragraph. Does it draw you in or make you curious to read more?
  • Make predictions about what you might learn from reading this book.

Say: Now that we have practiced previewing, let’s spend some time reading independently. Find a quiet spot in the room.

If necessary, remind students to transition to their independent reading spots quickly and quietly. Set a time limit for students who may need to choose a new book. Remind them to preview the book before settling down to read. Consider reminding students that they are building reading stamina. Setting a specific amount of time for the class to read and building up that amount of time can be motivating for students.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Learn how to preview a book.

Additional Materials

  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Preselected books to model previewing
  • Basket of books for students to practice previewing

Writing to Sources (10 MIN.)

Model: A Response to Reading

Using the established signal, call students to the meeting area. Ask them to bring their independent reading books with them.

Say: Yesterday we used a question jar to guide our response to reading. Today we will learn a strategy called lifting a line. We will practice with our read-aloud book from earlier. While I read this book, I thought the part about ________ was interesting.

Choose a sentence in the book that lends itself to a response to reading. Reread that sentence to the class.

Say: I am going to lift this line of the book to use as a topic.

Model writing the lifted text onto chart paper or the board.

Say: I chose this section to lift because of (its descriptive language, how it paints a clear picture, how it helps describe the character, or how it was thought provoking).

Model writing your thoughts about the passage. You may choose to connect to previous knowledge, write down questions the line made you think about, or discuss how the author’s choice of words helped paint a clear picture in your head.

Ask: What questions do you have about lifting a line?

Once all questions have been answered, direct students to look at a section of their own independent reading books to identify a line they would like to lift.

Say: Take a moment to look through your independent reading books. Find a line you would like to lift and use as a free-write. Would anyone like to share the line they plan to write about and explain why they are choosing it?

Allow a few students to share, then invite all students to return back to their desks to begin writing.




Objectives

Students will:

  • Participate in the writing strategy of lifting a line.

Additional Materials

  • Read-aloud book from earlier
  • Chart paper
  • Markers
  • Students’ independent reading books
  • Response journals or loose-leaf paper/writing folder

Independent Writing (15 MIN.)

A Response to Reading

While students are writing, walk around and confer with students about their progress. Help clarify thinking or answer any questions as you walk around the room. Allow five to ten minutes for this free-writing activity..

After ten minutes, invite some students to share their responses with a partner. Clarify any questions and praise students for the work they have just done. Highlight successes in writing where appropriate, specifically with language choice.
