Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Read and analyze a Writing to Sources prompt, including its purpose.
  • Engage in collaborative conversation to build on and share ideas.
  • Recognize the need to gather information to respond to the writing prompt.

Observation Checklist for Productive Engagement

As partners discuss the prompt, look for evidence that they are truly engaged in the task.

Partners are engaged productively if they . . .

  • ask questions and use feedback to address the task.
  • demonstrate engagement and motivation.
  • apply strategies with some success.

If the engagement is productive, continue the task. Then move to Share.

Partners are not engaged productively if they . . .

  • apply no strategies to the task.
  • show frustration or anger.
  • give up.

If the engagement is unproductive, end the task, and provide support.

3 Read and Analyze An Informative Prompt (15–20 MIN.) W.5.5, W.5.8, W.5.9b, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Tell students that they will write an informative essay this week based on the prompt on page 11 of their Texts for Close Reading. To help prepare them, explain that you will model how to read and analyze a writing prompt.

Ask: Why is it helpful to carefully read and analyze a writing prompt before you start writing?

Model

Display the model lesson prompt you will use during this week’s lessons. (Note and explain to students that this is a different prompt than the one students will analyze and write to independently.)

In a brief essay, explain James Madison’s role in creating the U.S. Constitution. Support your explanation with facts, definitions, and details from “Creating the Constitution.”

Model Lesson Prompt

Display the Analyze the Writing Prompt questions and model how you would answer each question.

Sample modeling: The first question asks me what type of text I will be writing. It is important that I know this so that I know how to structure my writing. The first sentence in the prompt says, “In a brief essay. . .” I know that an essay is a short piece of writing on a topic. What topic should I write about? The sentence goes on to state. . . “explain James Madison’s role in creating the U.S. Constitution.” Now I know I’ll be writing an informative essay on the topic of James Madison and the role he played in creating the U.S. Constitution.

Sample modeling: In my essay, I need to give facts and details from “Creating the Constitution” that inform the reader about things James Madison did to contribute to writing the U.S. Constitution.

Analyze the Writing Prompt Questions

Answers

What type of text are you being asked to write?

Text type: informative/explanatory essay

What are the characteristics of this writing form?

to define or to inform; to explain a process or reasons why

What source must you use as the basis for your writing?

“Creating the Constitution”

What details or evidence from the source will you need to look for?

information about what James Madison did to help create the U.S. Constitution

How will you use the details or evidence?

I will use them to decide how his contribution was important.

Sample Analyze the Writing Prompt chart

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask students to turn to the writing prompt on page 11 of their Texts for Close Reading. Ask a volunteer to read aloud the prompt as others follow along. Then invite partners to analyze the prompt by answering the questions on the Analyze the Writing Prompt chart. Suggest that students take turns leading the partner discussion.

Share

Invite different sets of partners to share their answers to each question with the class. Prompt other students to build on classmates’ ideas by agreeing or disagreeing in a constructive way. Use the conversation to discuss students’ understanding of the place of the Constitution in the United States. Make sure that all students understand the week’s independent writing objective and identify any students who need additional support.

Manage Independent Writing

Encourage students to keep the writing prompt in mind as they reread “Creating the Constitution.” Explain that in your next writing lesson you will model how to read a source text closely to find text evidence they will need to use in their essays.
