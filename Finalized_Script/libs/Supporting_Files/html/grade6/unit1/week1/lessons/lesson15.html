Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Say: Writers avoid run-on sentences. One way to do this is to use a subordinate conjunction to logically connect two ideas. Creating two sentences by placing a period at the end of the first complete thought and capitalizing the first word of the second one is another.

Subordinate Conjunctions

because, since

cause-effect relationship

Read from the revised essay and indicate the two complete thoughts in each sentence.

Ask: How does the revision connect the two thoughts?

Ask groups to respond.

  • It uses the subordinate conjunction _____ to show a _____ relationship.

The other delegates were very interested in Madison’s ideas because he had created a state constitution for Virginia. According to “Creating the Constitution,” at the time of the convention, the thirteen states had a lot of power to govern themselves. Madison wanted to change this since he believed too much state power made it difficult for the national government to collect taxes and form a military.

Ask partners to identify other transition words.

  • We found _____ and _____.

Moderate Support

Say: Writers can show relationships between thoughts and ideas by using subordinate conjunctions. Because and since show cause-effect relationships.

Assign partners to find transition words and phrases in the revision that show cause-effect, reference, and time order.

  • We found an example of _____, using the word(s) _____.

Ask partners to tell how they could use transitions to revise.

  • We could use _____ to join _____ and _____ to show _____.

Light Support

  • Assign groups to list words that show relationships between ideas or thoughts in the essay and brainstorm others. Encourage groups to list words in the following categories: cause-effect, reference, and time order. Ask partners to use transitions to revise their essay and explain.
  • We created a _____ relationship by joining _____ and _____ with _____.

15 Revise and Edit (15–20 MIN.) W.5.2d, W.5.5, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d, L.5.1a, L.5.2e, L.5.3a

Engage Thinking

Remind students that skillful writers go back to what they have written to revise and edit it. When they revise, they look for ways to make their writing clearer and more interesting. When writers edit, they correct mistakes in capitalization, punctuation, grammar, and spelling. Explain that in this lesson, you will model how you revise a text to correct run-on sentences. You will model how to edit for specific language conventions.

Ask: When you reread something you have written for school, what kinds of things do you fix?

Revise: Fix Incomplete or Run-On Sentences

Share the sentences below from the Exemplar Informative Essay with students. Read aloud the original sentences and then read aloud the revised sentences. Ask students to comment on differences they notice between the two texts. If necessary, point out specific ways in which the revision corrects run-on sentences.

Review that a run-on sentence is one that has two sentences or complete ideas combined into one without separating them correctly. Explain that when drafting a text, a writer may be eager to get his or her ideas down, and as a result may create a run-on sentence that has too many ideas. During the revision stage, the writer can correct this.

Point out the following revision techniques for dealing with run-on sentences:

  • Revise the run-on sentence by adding a conjunction to join two complete ideas. (In this example, the writer added because and since.) Other conjunctions a writer can use to correct a run-on sentence include after, as, so, if, although, where, or whether.
  • Create two sentences by placing a period at the end of the first complete thought and capitalizing the first word of the second complete thought.

Original Sentence

The other delegates were very interested in Madison’s ideas he had created a state constitution for Virginia.

According to “Creating the Constitution,” at the time of the convention, the thirteen states had a lot of power to govern themselves Madison wanted to change this he believed too much state power made it difficult for the government to collect taxes and form a military.

Revised Sentences

The other delegates were very interested in Madison’s ideas because he had created a state constitution for Virginia.

The other delegates were very interested in Madison’s ideas. He had created a state constitution for Virginia.

According to “Creating the Constitution,” at the time of the convention, the thirteen states had a lot of power to govern themselves. Madison wanted to change this because he believed too much state power made it difficult for the government to collect taxes and form a military.

Edit: Use an Editing Checklist

Explain to students that after writers have revised a text, they edit the text.

Ask: What do writers do when they edit?

Point out that it is helpful for writers to have an editing checklist to ensure they catch and correct errors based on their knowledge of English language conventions. Provide a checklist similar to the one below:

  • Did I use capital letters correctly?
  • Did I use correct punctuation?
  • Is my grammar correct?
  • Is my spelling correct?

Display and read aloud a section of the exemplar essay. Review the sentence with respect to each item on the Editing Checklist.

Sample modeling: First I’ll read the text to see if the writer used capital letters correctly. I see that the sentence begins with a capital letter (In) and all proper nouns begin with capital letters (Madison, Philadelphia, Constitutional Convention). Now I will focus on the other items in the checklist.

In 1787, James Madison was part of a group of delegates who came together in Philadelphia at the Constitutional Convention.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Invite students to read part of their independent writing with a partner to look for opportunities to fix incomplete or run-on sentences. Ask them to check each other’s work to ensure that they have covered all the items on the Editing Checklist.

Share

Invite students to share the revisions and edits they made during partner collaboration.

Manage Independent Writing

Encourage students to keep this lesson in mind as they continue drafting or revising their informative essays. Use conferring time to meet one-on-one with students who may need support with their writing, and make time for students to engage in additional peer review during independent time. When students are happy with their essays, have them use the Editing Checklist for their final edit.
