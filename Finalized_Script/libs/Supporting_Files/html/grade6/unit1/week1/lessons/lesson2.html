Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Say: Titles and visuals can help introduce the main idea. By putting these together, the main idea will probably be about the Constitution and who created it.

Ask students to point to the visuals, read the title and caption, and discuss how they help.

Focus on paragraphs 1–2 to identify the article’s main idea. Challenge students to find two sentences about creating the Constitution. Have them turn to partners to explain why the two probably state the main idea. Explain that established is a way to say created, and became the principal writer is a way to say framer.

Have partners share.

  • The _____ sentence in paragraph _____ and the _____ one in paragraph _____ give the main idea because _____.

Help students find key details about the main idea with sentence stems.

  • _____ the reason for the U.S. Constitution.
  • _____ called the Constitutional Convention.
  • _____ changes to the government.
  • _____ his ideas.

Moderate Support

Say: Paragraphs 3–5 should support the main idea of the article.

Say: After a sentence with the main idea is found, the rest of the sentences in the same paragraph are probably details.

Assign partners to paragraphs 3, 4, 5 to find a sentence about the Constitution and its framer and decide if it gives the main idea for paragraphs 3–5.

  • We think the _____ sentence in paragraph _____ gives the main idea because _____.

Then ask partners to identify key details.

  • The _____ sentence is about _____ and supports the main idea because _____.

Light Support

Assign groups to give main ideas for the paragraphs as a way of summarizing the article.

Have groups provide key words that lead to main ideas.

  • established, by the people
  • principal writer, or framer
  • followed. . . .
  • helped persuade . . . to accept
  • all his work, has been called

Challenge students to make generalizations about how the title, visuals, and key words led to main ideas.

Ways to Scaffold the First Reading

Use your observational assessment to determine the intensity of scaffolding your students need.

IF . . . THEN consider . . .

Students are English learners who may struggle with vocabulary and language demands . . .

Read the text TO students.

  • Conduct a before-reading picture walk to introduce vocabulary and concepts.
  • Stop after meaningful chunks to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase difficult sentences.

Students are struggling readers who may decode with little comprehension . . .

Read the text WITH students.

  • Stop after meaningful chunks to ask who, what, when, where, and how questions.
  • Work with students to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase key ideas.

Students need some support to read unfamiliar texts with comprehension . . .

Have students PARTNER-READ.
Partners should:

  • take turns reading aloud meaningful chunks.
  • ask each other who, what, when, where, and how questions about the text.
  • circle unfamiliar words and define them using context clues.

2 “Creating the Constitution”: Identify Main Idea and Key Details (15–20 MIN.) RF.5.4a, RI.5.1, RI.5.2, RI.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d, SL.5.4

Model: Preview the Genre

Display and ask students to open to “Creating the Constitution.” Point out that skillful readers preview a text and make predictions about it before they read. Model how you use prior knowledge as you skim and scan “Creating the Constitution.” Point out features of the text and make predictions about the genre and content using your observations.

Sample modeling: The title tells me this is about the Constitution. The text begins with a quotation and includes a portrait. By skimming the quotation, I can see that it is part of the Constitution. The caption tells me the portrait is of James Madison, our fourth president. I’m curious about what he has to do with the Constitution. I predict I will learn about how the Constitution came into being and the role James Madison played.

Invite students to turn and tell a partner what they observe about the text on the Constitution. Invite a few students to share their predictions about the text with the class.

Model: Read to Find Key Details

Display and read aloud the purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read the quotation and paragraphs 1–2 of “Creating the Constitution” to learn about the creation of the Constitution.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Ask students to follow along as you read aloud, model thinking, and annotate the quotation and paragraphs 1 and 2.

Sample modeling, sentences 1–3 of paragraph 1: The first sentence explains that the quotation is taken from the Constitution. Sentences 2–3 explain the importance of the Constitution. I think these details will be relevant to understanding how the Constitution was created, so I will underline “Never before had a country established a set of laws for running its government by the people themselves.” Now that I’ve read that, I notice that the introduction to the Constitution begins with the words “We the People.”

Sample modeling, sentences 4–5 of paragraph 1: I read that the Constitution has had a great impact on other countries. This is interesting, but I don’t think it is key to my understanding of how the Constitution was created, so I won’t underline it.

Sample modeling, paragraph 2: Here are some important details that explain how Madison became the framer of the Constitution. I will underline these words: “In 1787, James Madison and fifty-six other delegates . . . Madison became the principal writer . . . of the Constitution.” I think these are important details that will help me construct a main idea sentence.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 3–5. Identify key details and determine the main idea in this portion of “Creating the Constitution” to find out James Madison’s role in establishing the Constitution.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Give students three to five minutes to read and annotate. Observe their annotations to assess their ability to identify important versus unimportant information. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as they look for key details. For example:

  • Reread the first sentence of paragraph 3. What kind of government did Madison want? Did the rest of the delegates go along with that? What details tell why?
  • Reread paragraph 4. Are the details in that paragraph important or simply interesting? How can you tell the difference?

Collaborate with students to construct a main idea statement for the section.

Section

Key Details

Main Idea

Introduction, Paragraphs 1–2

The Constitution was the first set of laws established “by a country for running its government by the people themselves.”

In 1787 James Madison and other delegates held meetings to “discuss changes to the government.” The delegates were interested in Madison’s ideas, and he became the main writer of the Constitution.

The Constitution was the first set of laws for a country written by the people themselves, and James Madison was the principal writer of the Constitution.

Paragraphs 3–5

Madison wanted a “strong central government.” When the delegates decided on a final document, “they mostly followed Madison’s Virginia Plan and established a stronger federal government.” Madison helped write the “Federalist Papers” to “persuade readers to accept the new Constitution.” Madison helped create the Bill of Rights.

James Madison has been called the “Father of the Constitution.”

Reread and Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to reread paragraphs 3–5 to underline key details and generate a main idea statement. Ask students to write what they understand about James Madison and his role in establishing the Constitution. Use their ideas to help you make instructional decisions.

Challenge Activity: Tell students that the words that introduce and explain the reason for the Constitution are called the preamble. Discuss the meaning of any words in the preamble with which students are unfamiliar. Then have students memorize and recite the preamble, printed in the box at the beginning of “Creating the Constitution.”
