Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Read “Susan B. Anthony” together. Then invite students to partner-read it and list any unfamiliar words or ones they can’t pronounce. Model for students how to pronounce unpopular.

Say: I can break it into syllables: un-pop-u-lar. The first two syllables are closed so I’ll use short u and short o. The next two syllables are unstressed so I’ll use the schwa sound. It’s pronounced unpopular.

Decoding Multisyllabic Words

Break the word into syllables. Use a dictionary, if needed.

Is the syllable closed? Try using a short vowel sound. Remember that y can stand for a vowel sound in words.

Is the syllable unstressed? Try using the schwa sound.

Other rules may apply, too. If unsure, use a dictionary.

Then ask partners to use the chart and show understanding by decoding these words and underlining any short vowel sounds (one has none):

influenced, benefited, abolition, suffrage, activists, political, imprisonment, association, eventually, synonymous.

Provide frames for words:

  • The word synonymous has two short vowel sound(s): short i and short o.

Moderate Support

Provide the above chart and multisyllabic words from “Susan B. Anthony” for partners to read and sort by short vowel sounds. Remind them that some words may be listed for more than one sound.

abolition amendments citizens demand impressive insults legacy positive ready suffrage unpopular

Tell partners to underline letters that stand for short vowel sounds and use the frame.

  • The word abolition has a short a sound and a short i sound.

Light Support

Encourage partners to ask and answer riddles about multisyllabic words. Model for students.

Say: I’m thinking of a word that has three syllables and one short vowel sound. It begins with a schwa sound. It has a short e sound. It means “an addition.” There were ten of them at first . . . .

Ask students to respond with a frame.

  • Is it amendments?

Sample Short Vowel Word-Sort Chart

Remind students that understanding syllable patterns can help them read and pronounce words correctly. To illustrate this, direct students to paragraph 7 of “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address” and have them follow along as you read the paragraph aloud. Draw students’ attention to the sentence “Many of the issues of civil rights are very complex and most difficult.”

Say: When you come to an unfamiliar multisyllabic word such as complex or difficult, you can use your knowledge of syllable patterns to help you decode the word. Break the word into syllables and think about the pattern for each syllable.

Sample modeling: You can break complex into two syllables: com-plex. Notice that both are closed syllables. Although there are some exceptions, the vowel in these patterns usually has the short sound. So the word complex has a short o and a short e sound. Now you can pronounce the word.

Sample modeling: You can break difficult into three syllables: dif-fi-cult. What do you notice about each syllable? The first and last syllables are closed syllables and have a short vowel. The middle syllable is an exception. It is an unstressed syllable, so the i is neither short nor long. We call this the schwa sound.

Productive Engagement: Read and Annotate

Ask students to read “Susan B. Anthony.” As they read, tell them to circle multisyllabic words that have short vowel sounds and highlight the letter or letters in each word that stand for the short vowel sound. Students should use their knowledge of syllabication patterns as well as the context of the selection to help them pronounce and understand the meaning of each word. Suggest they write their definitions in the notation column of the page next to the word.

Give students approximately five minutes to read and annotate the selection. You may wish to have students read independently or with a partner. Because there are many short-vowel words, you may suggest students find at least five of each vowel sound. Students will find the following words in the text:

Share Word Knowledge and Ideas

Bring students together. Invite individuals or partners to share words they circled in the text and provide definitions for the words. Engage students in a discussion of the text to support and assess their understanding of the information. Use question prompts such as the following:

  • How is the topic of women’s suffrage similar to and different from the voting rights issue covered in “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address”?
  • From what you read in this selection and in “Creating the Constitution,” how were Susan B. Anthony and James Madison alike? How were they different?
  • How did understanding the meaning of “suffrage” give you more insight into the Constitution?

Spelling

Review the spelling words for the week. Then distribute copies of the Spelling Practice. Challenge students to remember their spelling words, their reading of “Susan B. Anthony,” and their knowledge of multisyllabic words with short vowels to select, fill in, and correctly spell the words. Finally ask students to work with a partner to generate their own spelling words.

  1. Today we think of Susan B. Anthony as a(n) _____ woman who had a great influence on U.S. history. (impressive)
  2. She spoke out on many_____ including abolishing slavery and giving women the right to vote. (topics)
  3. At the time, the general _____ did not support many of her political views. (public)
  4. When she gave speeches, it was not unusual for people to shout ____ at her. (insults)
  5. Her work, along with that of other _____, has _____ many people. (activists) (benefited)
  6. Susan B. Anthony’s support influenced the passage of _____ to the Constitution, including those abolishing slavery and granting women the right to vote. (amendments)
  7. She is one of a few _____ who have influenced the U.S. Constitution. (citizens)
