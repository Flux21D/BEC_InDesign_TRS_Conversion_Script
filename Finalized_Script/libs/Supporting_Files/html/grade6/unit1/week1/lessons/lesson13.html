13 “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address”: Identify and Analyze Reasons and Evidence  (15–20 MIN.) RI.5.1, RI.5.2, RI.5.3, RI.5.8, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Remind students that “President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address” is a persuasive speech. Its purpose was to convince Congress to pass a new law that would end the discriminatory practices that kept African Americans from voting. Explain that in today’s lesson, students will learn to recognize how an author uses reasons and evidence to support points in a text.

Ask: How does President Johnson’s speech try to persuade listeners that passing the Voting Rights Act is important?

Model

Read aloud paragraph 3 as students follow along. Use a three-column chart (see p. 29) to model how you identify an author’s point and the evidence he or she uses to support it and how you complete the chart.

Sample modeling, sentence 1: In sentence 1, President Johnson makes the point that there are times in history when important events happen that have an impact on our quest for freedom. I’m going to write that in the first column of my chart.

Sample modeling, sentence 2: Here President Johnson refers to Lexington and Concord. These towns were the sites of the first battles in the American Revolution. I know that the American Revolution was fought by the colonists to gain freedom from Great Britain. So I think President Johnson mentioned Lexington and Concord to remind listeners that Americans have fought for freedom from the beginning of our nation. I’ll add it to the second column in my chart.

Sample modeling, sentence 3: This sentence provides another example of a historical event: Appomattox, the site of the last battle of the Civil War. One issue in that war was the abolition of slavery, granting slaves their freedom. Again, President Johnson reminds his listeners that Americans have fought for freedom.

Sample modeling, sentence 4: Here President Johnson uses a more recent event. Selma, Alabama, is where protesters marched for the right of African Americans to vote. President Johnson is saying that what happened in Selma was an important event in our quest for freedom. It was as important as the American Revolution and the Civil War.

Guided Practice

Ask partners to reread paragraphs 5 and 6 to determine the point the author makes and identify the reasons or evidence he uses to support that point. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as needed. For example:

  • What quotations do you see in paragraph 5? What do they have to do with the founding principles of our country?
  • What is the connection between the ideas in paragraphs 3 and 5?

Display the Reasons and Evidence chart. Ask students to share their annotations and add information to the class chart.

Reflect on the Strategy

Ask students to reflect on the strategy of finding reasons and evidence to support points in texts they read across content areas in school.

Ask: What are some other historical texts you have read in which the author makes a point or tries to convince you to believe something? Why is it important to identify reasons and evidence to support those points?

Call on volunteers to provide examples. Reinforce students’ understanding that recognizing the reasons and evidence an author uses to support particular points in a text will help them determine if the author is making a valid point.

Apply Understanding of Reasons and Evidence

Ask students to write a paragraph summarizing the points that President Johnson makes to support his argument. How does Johnson’s speech reflect the idea that laws continue to evolve?

Challenge Activity: Ask students to read an editorial in the newspaper or a magazine. Then have them write a paragraph to answer the following questions: What point is the author making? What reasons or evidence does the author give to support that point?
