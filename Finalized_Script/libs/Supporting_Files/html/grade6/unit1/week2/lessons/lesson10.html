10Plan and Organize a First-Person Reflection (15–20 min.) W.5.3a, W.5.3b, W.5.3e, W.5.4, W.5.5, W.5.8, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Remind students that you have modeled how to analyze a narrative prompt and how to read a source to gather text evidence for writing a narrative. The next step is to plan the writing. In this lesson, you will model how to plan a narrative that will address the prompt and engage the reader.

Model

Display a two-column chart to construct a Reflection Planning chart. Read aloud the elements in the left column. Model your thinking, similar to the sample steps provided below, to show how you plan your fictional reflection.

Sample modeling: The prompt tells me who my narrator is—Eliza Emerson. I will be writing about her thoughts and feelings.

Sample modeling: I need to help my reader understand the time and place the reflection is based on, which is an important part of the narrative. I remember from reading that this is in the years before the Civil War in the United States. Slavery is a huge and divisive issue.

Sample modeling: Eliza Emerson is a slave owner and a widow who has inherited her husband’s slaves. One of her slaves, Dred Scott, has sued for freedom for himself and his family, possibly because Eliza Emerson wants the Scotts to keep bringing in income and so refuses to let the Scotts buy their freedom.

Sample modeling: At the end of many years of court battles, Dred Scott loses. However, descendants of his first owner buy and free the Scotts. Eliza Emerson has fought for years and in the end wins the case but loses her slaves. She is tired after the years of struggle, and she is just glad it is all finally over.

Reflection Elements

My Ideas/Text Evidence

Narrator (Person Who Is Reflecting)
  • Eliza Emerson, a widow and slave owner

Situation Description
  • The issue of slavery divides the country. In 1846, the narrator’s slave, Dred Scott, sues for freedom for himself and his family.
  • After 11 years, Scott loses his case but gains his freedom.

Narrator’s Role/Chronology of Events
  • Some people say narrator refuses to let Scott buy his freedom, so he is forced to go to court. In the first court case in 1847, Scott loses. In a new trial, Scott wins.
  • Narrator appeals and wins in 1852. Scott goes to higher court and loses in 1854. Scott takes his case to the Supreme Court and in 1857 loses. He is still a slave.
  • Descendants of his original owner buy and free the Scotts.

Narrator’s Thoughts and Feelings
  • Narrator likes the Scotts and never wanted them to be unhappy, but she wanted to keep her slaves. She needed the money she got from hiring out.
  • The process took much longer than she expected and she is tired of all of it. She hopes the Scotts will be happy now that they are free.

Sample Reflection Planning Chart

Productive Engagement: Partner

Distribute blank copies of a two-column chart. Ask partners to reread the narrative prompt on page 21 of their Texts for Close Reading and work together to plan their reflections. Partners should quickly establish who the narrator is (Dred Scott) and then tell each other their ideas for how to describe the situation and the narrator’s thoughts and feelings.

You may wish to model questions students can use with their partners. For example:

  • What do you think made Dred Scott finally sue for his freedom?
  • How do you think he feels when he loses the first case? The last case?
  • What does he think when the Blow family tells him he his free?

Share

Invite several students to share the ideas they discussed with their partners. Ask other students to comment on how working collaboratively with a partner helped them decide what to include in their own reflection. Use this discussion to determine which students may need additional help planning their writing.

Manage Independent/Partner Research

During independent time or as homework, have students add details to their writing plan. Remind them to look back at the text for evidence that supports their ideas. Students who feel ready may begin their draft. Use conferring time to meet with students who may need support.
