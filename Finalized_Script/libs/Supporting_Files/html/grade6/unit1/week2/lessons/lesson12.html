Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Recognize and pronounce words with long vowel patterns.
  • Sort words by long vowel patterns.
  • Collaborate to list and pronounce words with long vowels.
  • Ask clarifying questions.
  • Spell words with long vowel patterns.

Additional Materials

  • Long Vowel Word-Sort chart (Lesson 9)

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Help students partner-read “Mrs. Stowe and the President” and list unfamiliar words or ones they can’t pronounce.

Model for students.

Say: I can break a word into syllables: pre vi ous ly. The first two syllables and last are open so I’ll use long vowel sounds (long e). The third syllable is unstressed so I’ll use the schwa sound. It’s pronounced previously.

Decoding Multisyllabic Words

Break the word into syllables. Use a dictionary.

Is the syllable open or closed? If open, use a long vowel sound. If closed, try a short vowel sound.

Is there a CVCe pattern? Try a long vowel sound.

Are there two vowels (ee, ue, ea) or a spelling like igh? It’s probably a long vowel sound.

Is the syllable unstressed? Try the schwa sound.

Other rules may apply, too.

Help groups use the chart to decode these words (and any others they listed), underlining long vowel sounds: between, enslaved, primarily, terrifying, newspaper, outrage, explained, replied, devoted, myself, future. Provide frames.

  • Primarily has two long vowel sounds long i and e.

Moderate Support

Provide the above chart and multisyllabic words from “Mrs. Stowe and the President” for partners to read in context and sort by long vowel sounds. Point out some words that may be listed for more than one sound.

arrived, between, enslave, exclaimed, explained, fireplace, invited, newspaper, previously, primarily, terrifying

Tell partners to underline letters/spellings that stand for long vowel sounds.

  • Outrage has a long a sound in the second syllable.

Light Support

Invite partners to ask and answer riddles about multisyllabic words.
Model for students

Say: I’m thinking of a word with four syllables and one long vowel sound. It has two short vowel sounds and a schwa sound. It has long i spelled y. It describes chapters in Mrs. Stowe’s book . . . .

Have students respond with a frame.

  • Is it terrifying?

Remind students that recognizing the spelling patterns for long vowel sounds can help them read and pronounce words with those patterns. Direct students to paragraph 2 of “The Dred Scott Decision” and have them follow along as you read aloud. Draw students’ attention to the first sentence: “Slavery was a hotly debated issue in the country and each day the debate seemed to grow more complex and heated.”

Say:Recognizing long vowel sound-spellings helps me pronounce words as I come to them in a text. I know that the a in slavery is long because of the CVCe pattern. I know that y at the end of words is often pronounced /ee/. I know that ue together stands for the long u sound and that ee and ea can stand for long e. As I read, I think about the spelling patterns in order to read and pronounce the words.

Share Word Knowledge and Ideas

Bring students back together and have them share with the class the words they underlined with long vowel patterns. Ask them to pronounce the words.

Discuss “Mrs. Stowe and the President” with students, using the following as prompts:

  • Based on what you read in “The Dred Scott Decision,” why do you think Stowe’s book “sparked great outrage around the country years before war broke out”?
  • How did understanding long vowel spelling patterns make “Mrs. Stowe and the President” easier to read?
  • What did you learn from this selection about President Lincoln?

Spelling

Distribute copies of the Spelling Practice. Challenge students to remember their spelling words, their reading of “Mrs. Stowe and the President,” and their knowledge of long vowel spelling patterns to select, fill in, and correctly spell the words.

Finally, have students work with a partner to generate their own sentences using the spelling words.

1. Mrs. Stowe’s book _____ many details about the lives of enslaved people.

(contained)

2. Both the President and Mrs. Stowe hoped for a better _____ for the country. (future)

3. In 1862, many former slaves celebrated their _____. (freedom)

4. Mrs. Stowe’s book gave many people a _____ to work against slavery. (reason)

5. Citizens should know what their _____ and responsibilities are. (rights)

6. Many _____ readers took Mrs. Stowe’s words as a call to action. (devoted)

7. The issue of slavery divided the _____. (nation)

8. Mrs. Stowe _____ to the President, “I only hoped to speak the truth.” (replied)
