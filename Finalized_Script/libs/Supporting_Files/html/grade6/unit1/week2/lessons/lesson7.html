Engage Thinking

Remind students that once they have carefully read and understood a writing prompt, they are ready to gather information they will need from the source text. To do this, they will reread the source carefully. Display and reread the lesson prompt and remind students that this prompt asks them to use details from “The Dred Scott Decision” to write a reflection. Today, you will model how you reread the text to find this information.

Now that you’ve read “The Dred Scott Decision,” write a first-person reflection from Eliza Emerson’s point of view. Recount your thoughts and feelings about what has happened to you.

Model Lesson Prompt

Model

Model skimming the text looking for the words “Emerson” and “she.” Stop to scan paragraph 7, the first place Eliza Emerson is mentioned. Then read aloud paragraphs 7–10 while students follow along. Think aloud to model how you identify details that will help you imagine Eliza Emerson’s thoughts and feelings.

Sample modeling: I am looking for details that will help me understand how Eliza. Emerson might have felt during this time so that I can write from her point of view. In paragraph 7, I read that she married John Emerson in Louisiana. I know Louisiana was a slave state. I also know that the Scotts were already the property of John Emerson. I think Eliza Emerson was used to having slaves. John Emerson died in 1843, and in his will he left the Scotts and all his property to his wife.

Sample modeling: In paragraph 9, I read that the Scotts were living in Missouri three years after John Emerson’s death. This is when and where Dred Scott first went to court to try to win his freedom. I wonder how Eliza Emerson felt about that. In paragraph 10, the author writes, “Some say that Scott may have tried to buy his freedom from Emerson’s widow but she refused.” That’s interesting. I’ll write that down.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask partners to reread paragraphs 11 and 12 to find any additional details that help them understand the case from Eliza Emerson’s point of view. Partners should identify the text evidence and write their related idea in the annotation column of their Texts for Close Reading or on a two-column chart. Partners should be prepared to share their evidence and ideas with the whole class.

Text Evidence

Ideas for Narrative

“Some say that Scott may have tried to buy his freedom from Emerson’s widow but she refused.” (10)

Eliza Emerson (the narrator) did not want to let her slaves go. She was used to having slaves. It was her way of life.

“Eliza Emerson was not happy with this decision. Some say she didn’t want to lose the earnings she made by hiring the Scotts out to others. She appealed the decision. . . .” (12)

Eliza Emerson was a widow. She needed the money she got from owning the Scotts, which allowed her to be paid by others needing the Scotts’ help. Maybe she was scared to let the Scotts go.

Sample Narrative Ideas Chart

Share

Bring students together to share their ideas based on text evidence. Discuss with students how a narrative prompt gives them an opportunity to be more creative and imaginative with their writing. They will need to put themselves into a time and place in order to imagine what might have happened and how a person might have thought and felt. These details will not be found in the source material, but the source can provide a strong starting point. Invite students to share some of their original ideas. Use this time to ensure that all students understand how to gather text evidence for writing.

Manage Independent Writing

Ask students to reread “The Dred Scott Decision” to find evidence to use in their first-person reflection from Dred Scott’s point of view. Remind them to review the entire writing prompt on page 21 of their Texts for Close Reading. Students can read and annotate the text during independent time or as homework.

Use conferring time to meet one-on-one with students who may need support finding and recognizing text evidence, and make time for students to engage in peer collaboration.
