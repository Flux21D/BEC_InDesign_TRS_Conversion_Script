Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Reflect on and share my knowledge about the U.S. Constitution.
  • Summarize my partner’s ideas using formal English.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Reread as needed and aid students to complete.

“Creating the Constitution”

In 1787, Madison _____ the Constitution.

Then he wrote _____ articles to _____ it.

“President Lyndon Johnson’s Voting Rights Act Address”

On March 30, 1870, the _____ was _____, giving _____.

On March 15, 1965, Johnson _____, urging Congress to _____ the Constitution.

In it, Johnson said the Constitution _____ and _____.

On August 6, 1965, Johnson _____ into law.

Moderate Support

Ask partners to supply dates/time-order words and then discuss why laws changed.

_____ Madison helped write the Constitution.

_____ he helped create the Bill of Rights.

_____ the Fifteenth Amendment to the Constitution was made law.

_____ Johnson gave a speech to Congress.

_____ Johnson signed the Voting Rights Act into law.

Light Support

Ask partners to arrange the key events from each reading on a timeline and discuss why laws evolve.

ELD.PI.5.6, ELD.PI.5.12a, ELD.PII.5.2b

1Build Knowledge and Integrate Ideas (10 min.) SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d, SL.5.2, SL.5.6

Engage Thinking

Ask students to turn to Build, Reflect, Write. Remind students that in these readings they are considering the U.S. Constitution and how the laws in the Constitution continue to evolve. Last week, they read an informational text about the creation of the Constitution and a speech given by President Johnson about the 15th Amendment. This week they will read about an interpretation of the Constitution and a decision by the Supreme Court in 1857.

Say: Let’s think about what we read last week and what we’ve learned and know about the Constitution.

Turn and Talk to Share Knowledge

Ask students to engage in a brief conversation with a partner to answer two questions. Invite students to refer to their Build, Reflect, Write notes.

  • What did you learn about the Constitution last week?
  • How does your knowledge of the Constitution affect your understanding of the Essential Question?

Tell students that you will call on several of them to summarize what their partners learned last week, so they should be prepared to briefly restate their partners’ ideas.

Share

Bring students together and call on several students to briefly summarize what they learned about “America’s problem” as President Johnson described it in the “Voting Rights Act Address.” Ask students what President Johnson meant when he described that time when “history and fate meet at a single time in a single place to share a turning point in man’s unending search for freedom.” Encourage them to tell what was happening at that moment in history. Then turn their focus to how people come to decisions at important moments in history. Invite students to make predictions about what their next reading, “The Dred Scott Decision,” could add to this discussion of laws to protect citizens’ rights.
