Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Read and analyze a writing prompt, including its purpose.
  • Share ideas and build on ideas through collaborative conversation.
  • Consider details that will be useful in my written response as I reread a text.

Additional Materials

  • Analyze the Writing Prompt chart

Observation Checklist for Collaborative Engagement

As partners discuss the narrative prompt, look for evidence that they are truly engaged in the task.

Partners are engaged productively if they . . .

  • ask questions and use feedback to address the task.
  • demonstrate engagement and motivation.
  • apply strategies with some success.

If the engagement is productive, continue the task. Then move to Share.

Partners are not engaged productively if they . . .

  • apply no strategies to the task.
  • show frustration or anger.
  • give up.

If the engagement is unproductive, end the task and provide support.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Reread narrative prompts with students as they underline key phrases. Model paraphrasing. Ask students to paraphrase or define, using synonyms.

Word or Phrase – Synonym or Paraphrase

  • narrative = story, account of events (true or made up)
  • first-person reflection = observations told by you as if you were the character; about the character’s thoughts and feelings; uses words I, me, my, mine
  • point of view = a way of considering things or looking at them from a certain perspective
  • recount = tell or relate

Ask partners to use frames/stems to compare.

  • One is told from _____’s point of view, and the other is told from _____’s point of view. Mine will be about _____.

Moderate Support

Ask students to reread the prompt and underline the words/phrases: first-person reflection, point of view, and recount.

Ask students to paraphrase narrative-prompt tasks on a chart.

Ask: From which character’s point of view will you recount your narrative? Elicit “from Dred Scott’s.”

4Read and Analyze a Narrative Prompt  (15–20 min.) W.5.5, W.5.8, W.5.9b, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Tell students that this week they will write a narrative text based on the prompt on page 21 of their Texts for Close Reading. During today’s writing lesson, you will model how to read and think about what a writing prompt is asking you to do. Remind students that analyzing the writing prompt will help them make sure they are completing the writing task correctly.

Ask: What do you know to do when you first read a writing prompt?

Model

Display the writing prompt you will use during this week’s lesson. (Note: This is a different prompt than the one students will analyze and write to independently.)

Now that you’ve read “The Dred Scott Decision,” write a first-person reflection from Eliza Emerson’s point of view. Recount your thoughts and feelings about what has happened to you.

Model Lesson Prompt

Display Analyze the Writing Prompt questions and use sample modeling for how you answer each question in order to understand the writing assignment.

Sample modeling: The first thing I need to think about is what kind of text I’m asked to write. I don’t want to write the wrong kind of response. The prompt says “write a first-person reflection.” A reflection is like a journal entry. That’s a narrative form of writing. I’m asked to write in first person, which means I will use words such as “I” and “me.” I am supposed to write from Mrs. Emerson’s point of view, so I will have to imagine what she was thinking and feeling.

Analyze the Prompt Question Answer

Answer

What type of text are you being asked to write?

Text type: narrative

Genre: first-person reflection

What are the characteristics of this writing form?

fiction; use of the character of Emerson; plot will be based on events in Emerson’s life’s setting in 1850s; will have Emerson’s personal thoughts and feelings

What source must you use as the basis for your writing?

“The Dred Scott Decision”

What details or evidence from the source will you need to look for?

Details about Emerson, including who she is and how she is affected by the case and the decision

How will you use the details and evidence?

I will use the details about Emerson to imagine what she might have thought and felt so I can write about the case from her point of view.

Sample Analyze the Writing Prompt chart

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask students to open to the writing prompt on page 21 of their Texts for Close Reading. Invite a volunteer to read aloud the prompt as the other students follow along.

Distribute the Analyze the Writing Prompt chart and ask partners to analyze the prompt by answering the questions on this chart. Point out that even though students have not read the entire text, they can begin thinking about the writing prompt and how they will address the task. Encourage students to take turns leading the partner discussion.

Share

Invite different pairs of students to share their answers to each question. Invite other students to build on classmates’ ideas by asking clarifying questions or adding their own interpretations in a constructive way. Use the conversation to gain an understanding of how students are beginning to understand the need for laws to ensure all people’s rights. Check to see that all students understand the week’s independent writing objective.

Manage Independent/Partner Research

Invite students to reread paragraphs 1–10 of “The Dred Scott Decision” and think about the narrative text they will write from Dred Scott’s point of view. Explain that in the next lesson, they will read the remainder of the selection. As they read, they should keep the writing assignment in mind.
