Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Restate and write the point taken from the model that describes the opening paragraph, using simplified language.

  • State your opinion. Provide information about the topic.

Model simple examples for students.

Opinion:

  • The Sweatt case was more important.
  • The Brown v. Board of Education case was more important.

Information:

  • Sweatt fought to enter law school by going to court.
  • The Brown v. Board of Education case decided that people of all races must be allowed in any public school.
  • The Sweatt case took place before the Brown case.

Invite pairs to write one opinion and one information statement for each argument. Ask them to include a word or phrase that modifies each sentence to make it more descriptive. Example: The Sweatt case proved to be most important.

Moderate Support

Provide students with the point taken from the model as a guideline for writing.

  • The essay begins with a strong introduction paragraph that provides information about the topic and clearly states the writer’s opinion.

Ask partners to work together to draft an opinion statement and information about the topic. Provide for both of the opposing arguments.

Encourage pairs to read each other’s material and ask questions to clarify or suggest modifications to make stronger statements before sharing with the class.

Light Support

Ask partners to draft introductory paragraphs to support each of the opposing arguments, taking into consideration the points that were discussed. Review what is needed if necessary. Have partners suggest modifications to each other’s statements that strengthen the language and share them with the class.

13 Draft an Opinion Text (15–20 min.) W.5.1a, W.5.1b, W.5.1c, W.5.1d, W.5.4, W.5.5, W.5.9b, W.5.10, L.5.1d, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Remind students that a draft is an organized first version of a piece of writing. Review that a draft is not a perfect, complete work. Explain that today you will discuss with students a first draft of the prompt-based opinion essay you have been modeling.

Ask: What do you find appealing about the drafting stage of the writing process?

Model

Display and read aloud an Exemplar Opinion Text.

Introduction states topic and writer’s clear opinion.

Body paragraphs each give a reason for the opinion and flow in logical order; text details provide support.

Shifts between present and past are clear with shifts in verb tense.

Opinion and supporting reasons are clearly linked with words, phrases, clauses.

Conclusion restates the opinion and sums up writer’s ideas.

Heman Sweatt was a young African American man who wanted to attend law school at the University of Texas. He was denied admission based on the color of his skin. Thurgood Marshall, a lawyer who would go on to serve on the U.S. Supreme Court, took Sweatt’s case and represented him in Sweatt v. Painter. The Supreme Court decisions laid out in Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. the Board of Education were both critical steps in the fight for equal rights for African Americans in the United States. However, Sweatt v. Painter was the more important of the two cases.

In his “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech,” Marshall describes Sweatt as “an ordinary man who had an extraordinary dream.” The fact that Sweatt was an ordinary man is part of what makes this case so important. He, and the case, set an example for the other “ordinary” African American men and women who might otherwise have given up on their own dreams.

In his speech, Marshall also highlights Sweatt’s “determination to attain the best and a refusal to settle for anything less.” This determination to fight against the “separate but equal” segregation laws was a key component in the long, hard battle to gain equal rights. As a specific example, the Sweatt v. Painter case showed others that they could fight and win.

Finally, Sweatt v. Painter was argued before Brown v. the Board of Education. Without the precedent set by the Sweatt case, the decision in Brown might have been different. The Sweatt case paved the way to greater opportunities in all areas of education.

It is hard to believe that the court cases of Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. the Board of Education happened fewer than 70 years ago. Both decisions changed the course of history for African Americans in the United States. However, Sweatt v. Painter influenced the outcome of Brown, making it the more important of the two.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Invite partners to work together to draft the opening paragraph of another opinion essay about which case was more important. For their opening, ask students to state the opposite opinion from your model text. Direct one partner to act as notetaker as students discuss and finalize ideas. Remind them to include in their introduction a summary of the topic and a clearly stated opinion.

Share

Invite several pairs to read aloud their opening paragraph. Invite other students to provide constructive feedback.

Build Language: Recognize Shifts in Verb Tense

Review with students that sometimes in their writing, they may need to shift between writing about the present and writing about the past. When these shifts occur, the verb tense students use will also need to shift. Point out and reread this example from the draft essay:

In his speech, Marshall also highlights Sweatt’s “determination to attain the best and a refusal to settle for anything less.” This determination to fight was a key component in the long, hard battle to gain equal rights.

Point out the verbs highlights and was and explain that the shift happens between writing about Marshall’s speech (present) and Sweatt’s character (past). Writing about the present requires a present tense verb. Writing about the past requires a past tense verb. Work with students to find other examples in the exemplar text.

Manage Independent Writing

Ask students to draft their own opinion essay using the prompt on page 31 of their Texts for Close Reading. Remind students to use the plan they created in lesson 10 as they draft. Provide one-on-one support for those students who need it.
