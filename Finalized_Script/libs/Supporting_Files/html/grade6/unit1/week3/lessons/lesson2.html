Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Identify features of a persuasive speech.
  • Explain how a persuasive speech is different from a personal narrative.
  • Share my ideas with peers.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Ask students to share what they understand about persuasive speeches and personal narratives. Provide sentence frames with the following basic language to help them express ideas.

  • A persuasive speech tells an opinion.
  • A persuasive speech tries to change people’s opinion.
  • A persuasive speech uses language to influence people.
  • A personal narrative tells a story.
  • A personal narrative tries to describe what happened.
  • A personal narrative uses language to describe things.

Moderate Support

Ask students to describe persuasive speeches and personal narratives. Support their language production by providing sentence frames that include formal reporting language.

  • A persuasive speech provides an opinion.
  • A persuasive speech attempts to influence people.
  • A persuasive speech employs language that convinces people.
  • A personal narrative provides an account of events.
  • A personal narrative attempts to inform people.
  • A personal narrative employs language that describes events.

Light Support

Ask students to describe the characteristics of persuasive speeches and personal narratives. Ask them to use formal classroom language to report their ideas. Examples:

  • A persuasive speech [provides/attempts to/employs language that] …
  • A personal narrative [provides/attempts to/employs language that] …

Engage Thinking

Remind students that during Week 1, they read a persuasive speech given by President Lyndon Johnson about the Voting Rights Act. This week, they will read another persuasive speech. As they read the speech, they will apply strategies they learned in Week 1.

Turn and Talk to Reflect on Strategies

Ask students to turn and talk with a partner about the elements of a persuasive speech. Ask students to consider what makes a persuasive speech different from a regular personal narrative. Give partners one or two minutes to share ideas.

Share

Bring students together and call on several pairs to share their ideas using formal English. Use their answers to assess whether they understand the features of a persuasive speech and how it differs from a personal narrative.

If necessary, prompt students with questions such as these:

  • What is the main objective of a persuasive speech?
  • Why might presidents or other public figures give persuasive speeches? To whom are they speaking?
  • What kind of language is usually included?

You may want to review these features of a persuasive speech:

  • Has a strong position, or point of view
  • Suggests solutions or actions
  • Uses powerful words to influence the listener
  • Uses facts and evidence to make a case
  • Has a specific audience in mind
