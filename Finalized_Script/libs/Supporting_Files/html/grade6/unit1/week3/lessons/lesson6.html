Moderate Support

Ask students to work with the chart modeled. Expect them to use complete sentences to describe features, and explain their importance. Provide the following sentence starters to support their language production for the explanations.

  • The sign in the business window illustrates… the separation of races. how life was in the South.
  • It provides information… not in the text. to illustrate Marshall’s points.
  • The photo shows… fires burning. racial unrest.
  • According to Marshall, this is a result of… inequality. lack of freedom.
  • The footnote/photo explains… Marshall was talking about riots.

Light Support

Ask students to complete the chart modeled, using detailed descriptions. Have them explain why these features are important, using the format:

  • The photo of … shows us …
  • The footnote explains …
  • These add information by …

ELD.PI.5.6a, ELD.PI.5.7, PII.5.6

6 Analyze Graphic Features: Photos, Captions, and Footnotes (15–20 min.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, RI.5.7, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Ask students to think about ways in which writers can give information other than in paragraphs. Explain that graphic features such as photographs, captions, and footnotes are all ways that writers share information. Graphic features sometimes provide totally new information and sometimes extend information already in the text. In this lesson, you will model for students how to use graphic features to gain more meaning from the text.

Ask: How can graphic features help readers better understand a text?

Model

Display a blank Graphic Features chart and have students open to the photo of the “Jim Crow” sign on page 24 of “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.” Model how you use this photo and its caption to understand more about the speech.

Sample modeling: In paragraphs 4 and 5, Justice Marshall talks about Heman Sweatt and the prejudice he faced when he applied to law school. The photograph of the “We cater to white trade only” sign gives me concrete evidence of how Sweatt and other African Americans were treated and what their everyday lives were like.

Sample modeling: The caption explains to me that signs like this were part of what separated the races in the South. This feature adds information for me. The sign is not explicitly discussed in the speech, so the photo and caption help me understand more clearly the points that Justice Marshall makes.

Add information to the graphic features chart.

Guided Practice

Ask partners to read paragraphs 8 and 9 and discuss how the photograph/caption and the footnote support and extend their understanding of the text. Provide directive or corrective feedback as needed. For example:

  • What does the photograph show? How does the caption help you understand the photo? How does the photo connect to and extend the text?
  • Why is the footnote important? Would a person who read the speech in 1992 have needed the footnote? Why not? Who added the footnote?

Bring students back together to share their ideas. Remind students to follow the rules for discussions. Add information to the chart.

Feature (page)

Part(s) of Text It Supports

How It Supports My Understanding of Concepts

Photo and caption (24)

Paragraphs 4–5

The photo shows a sign in a business window, and the caption explains this kind of sign kept races separate in the South. This information is not in the text. It helps me understand what life was like for Heman Sweatt and other African Americans.

Photo and caption (27)

Paragraph 9, whole text

The photo shows fires burning. The caption explains that this was in Los Angeles in 1992, which is when Justice Marshall gave his speech. It helps the reader understand the context for Justice Marshall’s words, especially “a price to be paid” and the part about tension and rage.

Footnote (27)

Paragraph 9

Paragraph 9 The footnote explains the “recent events” Justice Marshall refers to. It explains that the events were riots. Because we are reading the speech years after it happened, we need the footnote to give us the historical context.

Reflect on the Strategy

Ask several students to discuss why paying attention to graphic features is an important strategy and how the graphic features in this selection helped them better understand the speech.

Apply Understanding of Graphic Features

Ask students to look at the photographs of Thurgood Marshall on pages 22 and 28 of the selection. Ask them to explain in a written paragraph whether these photographs extend their understanding of the speech. If they find the photos helpful, they should explain why. If they find the photos are not helpful, they should suggest an alternative graphic feature that would be better.

Challenge Activity: Ask students to look back at “The Dred Scott Decision” and find an example of a graphic feature. Invite students to write a paragraph explaining how the graphic feature extends or supports the ideas in the text.
