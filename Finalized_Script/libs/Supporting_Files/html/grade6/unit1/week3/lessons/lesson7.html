  • He fought for his goal with a court case.

Moderate Support

  • This evidence shows…
  • This is important because…
  • All men and women should…because…
  • Sweatt encouraged others…
  • Sweatt’s case influenced…by…

Engage Thinking

Say: You’ve read and analyzed the writing prompt. What should be your next step?

Review that the next step is rereading the source material to find the information they need. Display and reread the prompt from Lesson 4 and remind students that the prompt requires forming an opinion and using details and evidence from “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” to support that opinion. Today you will model how to reread the text to find this information.

In the “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech,” Thurgood Marshall mentions two important Supreme Court cases: Sweatt v. Painter, which integrated law schools, and Brown v. Board of Education, which integrated public schools. Which of these two cases do you think was more important? Write an opinion text in which you answer this question, using details and evidence from “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.”

Model Lesson Prompt

Model

Read aloud paragraph 4 in the “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” while students follow along.

Sample modeling: I am looking for facts and details to support an opinion about which of the two Supreme Court cases I think was more important. The cases are Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. Board of Education. I see that paragraph 4 is about Heman Sweatt. I will look for details about him and his case. I read that he was “an ordinary man who had an extraordinary dream.” He wanted to go to the University of Texas Law School but was denied admission because “his color was not theirs.” I’ll write these details down. They could be used to support the opinion that Sweatt v. Painter was more important.

Sample modeling: Now I will look for information about Brown v. Board of Education. I find it in the sidebar on page 24. Here, it says the Brown case is called a “landmark” court case. I will note this; it shows the importance of the Brown v. Board of Education ruling.

Remind students that in their last research and writing lesson they analyzed writing prompts. Explain that today they will learn how to select key terms from a prompt to search for and organize information that supports an opinion.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask partners to reread paragraph 5 and the sidebar “Sweatt v. Painter” to find at least one additional detail that could be used to support an opinion about which case was more important. Partners should identify the text evidence and write their related idea in the annotation column of their Texts for Close Reading or on a two-column chart. Partners should be prepared to share their evidence and ideas with the whole class.

Text Evidence

Ideas for Supporting an Opinion

Sweatt was “an ordinary man who had an extraordinary dream” of going to the University of Texas Law School. He was denied admission because he was black. (paragraph 4)

This is evidence that the Sweatt case showed other “ordinary” African American men and women that they should have the same educational opportunities as whites.

Sweatt held on to his “determination to attain the best and a refusal to settle for anything less.” (5)

This is more evidence that the Sweatt case was important. Sweatt didn’t give up, and his case probably encouraged others to keep fighting.

Marshall won Sweatt v. Painter in 1950, which influenced the outcome of Brown v. the Board of Education in 1954. (sidebar)

Without the Sweatt case, the outcome of Brown v. Board of Education might have been different.

Share

Bring students together to share their ideas based on text evidence. Discuss with students how in an opinion essay they eventually have to decide on an opinion and look for facts to support that opinion. In the beginning, however, they can look for supporting facts about both options before deciding on their opinion.

Manage Independent Writing

Ask students to reread to gather text evidence to support an opinion for their own essay during independent time or as homework.

Use conferring time to meet with students who may need additional support in formulating and supporting an opinion.
