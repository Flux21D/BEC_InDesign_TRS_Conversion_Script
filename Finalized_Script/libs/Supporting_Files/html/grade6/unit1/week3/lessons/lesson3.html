  • Share my thinking with peers.

Ways to Scaffold the First Reading

Use your observational assessment to determine the intensity of scaffolding your students need.

IF . . .

THEN consider . . .

Students are English learners who may struggle with vocabulary and language demands . . .

Read the text TO students.

  • Conduct a before-reading picture walk to introduce vocabulary and concepts.
  • Stop after meaningful chunks to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase difficult sentences.

Students are struggling readers who may decode with little comprehension . . .

Read the text WITH students.

  • Stop after meaningful chunks to ask who, what, when, where, how questions.
  • Work with students to define unfamiliar words and paraphrase key ideas.

Students need some support to read unfamiliar texts with comprehension . . .

Have students PARTNER-READ.

Partners should:

  • take turns reading aloud meaningful chunks.
  • ask each other who, what, when, where, how questions about the text.
  • circle unfamiliar words and define them using context clues.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Provide students with a sample chart for recording key details. Once they finish providing details, have them summarize in one main idea. Provide them with words to help them connect and combine their ideas, such as so that, in order to, etc.

Main Idea

Introduction

The fight for equality is ongoing.

Marshall tells stories of people who fought for freedom so that people know what needed to change and in order to present what he thinks may still need to change.

2

Examples of people who fought to be free

Moderate Support

Provide students with the same chart, but expect more sophisticated language when they report their main idea. Provide examples to support their language production.

  • Marshall expresses/shares.
  • He gives accounts/provides examples.
  • He prepares the reader/listener.

Light Support

Ask students to list key details from the introduction and each of the first three paragraphs. Have them consider all the details they have recorded, and present one main idea that can be deduced from the information.

3 “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech,” Part 1: Identify Key Details and Main Idea (15–20 min.) RF.5.4a, RI.5.1, RI.5.2, RI.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Model: Preview the Genre

Display and ask students to open to “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.” Review that skillful readers preview and make predictions about a text before they begin reading. Model how you skim and scan to notice graphic and text features, ask questions, and make predictions.

Sample modeling:I learn from the title what this selection is—it’s an acceptance speech. I can skim the introductory paragraph to learn who gave the speech and when it was given. Thurgood Marshall was the first African American Supreme Court Justice. I can predict that he had some ideas to share with the audience. I see some sidebar information and photographs. I wonder how those relate to the topic of the speech.

Invite students to turn and tell a partner what they observe and predict. Invite a few students to share their predictions with the class.

Model: Read to Find Key Details

Display and read aloud the purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read the introduction and paragraphs 1–3 to find out the purpose of Justice Marshall’s speech. Identify key details and determine the main idea of this part of the speech.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Display and ask students to follow along as you read aloud, model, and annotate this section of “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.”

Sample modeling, introduction: This paragraph tells me that Thurgood Marshall gave this speech in 1992 to accept the Liberty Medal and to remind people that “the fight for Constitutional freedoms and equality is an ongoing battle.”

Sample modeling, paragraph 1: In the introduction, Justice Marshall says, “independence is a concept near and dear to my heart.” I’ll underline that.

Sample modeling, paragraph 2: Here, Justice Marshall tells a joke by saying that he’s “not” giving a speech. Instead, he says he wants to share stories of “people who have understood the meaning of liberty and struggled against the odds to become free.”

Sample modeling, paragraph 3: In paragraph 3, Justice Marshall says his purpose is not to “dwell on the past, but to gain inspiration for ‘what can be’.”

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second purpose for reading and annotation instructions.

Purpose: Read paragraphs 4–6 to find out the significance of Heman Sweatt and the Shelleys. Identify key details and determine the main idea of these paragraphs.

Annotate! Underline key details as you read.

Give students time to read and annotate. Observe their annotations to assess their ability to identify important versus unimportant information. Provide directive and/or corrective feedback as they look for key details. For example:

  • Who was Heman Sweatt? What key details do you need to know about him?
  • Read the sidebar. What information do they give you?

Collaborate with students to construct a main idea statement for the paragraphs based on the key details they found.

Paragraphs Key Details Possible Main Idea
Introduction, 1–3

1. Thurgood Marshall’s speech to accept the Liberty Medal says, “the fight for Constitutional freedoms and equality is an ongoing battle.”

2. “. . . independence is a concept near and dear to my heart!”

3. Marshall says he will share stories of people who understood liberty and “struggled against the odds to become free.”

Thurgood Marshall said his speech would be stories about real people who fought for freedom, a reminder of what has changed and what still needs to change.
4–5

4. Heman Sweatt was denied admission to law school “because his color was not theirs”

5. Sweatt knew that, “none of us Afro, white, or blue will ever rest until we are truly free.”

6. Marshall won Sweatt v. Painter; Heman was admitted to law school.

Heman Sweatt took his personal battle to court to gain his constitutional rights.

Read to Apply the Strategy

Ask students to read paragraph 6 to underline key details and generate a main idea statement on their own. Ask students to write their key details and main idea statement on paper to hand in. Use their writing to evaluate their strategy use and development.
