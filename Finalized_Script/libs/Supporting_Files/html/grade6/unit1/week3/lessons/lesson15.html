Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Revise my writing to include complete sentences.
  • Edit my writing to check for correct shifts in verb tense as well as correct spelling, grammar, punctuation, and capitalization.
  • Share my work with a partner.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Remind students that verbs express action or a state of being and that they can talk about the past, present, or future. Write the model sentence given on the board. (“It is hard to believe…”) Underline the verbs in the sentence (is, happened) Ask what time period each verb talks about and provide sentence frames to support their answers. Explain that the tense of the verb represents the time period.

The verb is talks about the present.

The verb happened talks about the past.

Have students read their writing and underline the verbs. They should write each verb in a list and the time period it represents beside it. Then decide if the tense is appropriate. If not, they should change it as needed.

Moderate Support

Ask students what verbs do and what time periods they can talk about. Ask them what tense means.

  • Verbs express … or …
  • They can talk about the time periods … or … or …
  • Tense is the form of the verb that addresses …

Ask students to read their writing and underline the verbs, deciding if the tense is appropriate. If not, they should change it as needed.

Light Support

Ask students what verbs do and what time periods they can talk about. Ask them what tense means.

Encourage students to read their writing and identify the verbs, deciding if the tense is appropriate. If not, they should change it as needed.

Engage Thinking

Say: You’ve written the draft of your opinion text. What will you do next?

Review that after drafting, writers revise and edit their work. When they revise, they look for ways to improve the structure of the writing and to make the writing clearer. When they edit, they correct errors in spelling, punctuation, grammar, and capitalization. Explain that in this lesson you will model how to revise run-on sentences or sentence fragments and correct shifts in verb tense.

Revise: Produce Complete Sentences

Display a revised section of the Exemplar Opinion Essay. Ask students to listen as you first read aloud a draft text without including the revisions. Then read the revised text. Ask students to comment on changes that were made. Point out specific ways in which the revisions improve text structure and clarify meaning.

Sample modeling: When writers draft, they try to get ideas down on paper. Sometimes incomplete or run-on sentences appear. The sentence “Gave greater opportunities in all areas of education.” is a fragment. It does not have a subject. In the revision, replacing the word “gave” with the word “to” connects the fragment to the sentence before it, creating one longer sentence that explains what the Sweatt case did.

Point out the following revisions:

  • Taking out the period and adding to removes the sentence fragment and clarifies meaning.
  • Adding a period and the capitalized word Both revises the run-on sentence and makes the paragraph easier to read.
  • Adding a comma and the phrase making it, as well as deleting the unnecessary words and so then because of that, corrects the run-on and ends the paragraph in a clearer, more persuasive way.

The Sweatt case paved the way. Gave greater opportunities in all areas of education. It is hard to believe that the court cases of Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. the Board of Education happened fewer than 70 years ago the decisions changed the course of history for African Americans in the United States. However, Sweatt v. Painter influenced the outcome of Brown and so it then because of that the more important of the two.

The Sweatt case paved the way to greater opportunities in all areas of education. It is hard to believe that the court cases of Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. the Board of Education happened fewer than 70 years ago. Both decisions changed the course of history for African Americans in the United States. However, Sweatt v. Painter influenced the outcome of Brown, making it the more important of the two.

Revised Exemplar Opinion Essay

Edit: Correct Shifts in Verb Tense

Draw students’ attention to and read aloud the sentence “It is hard to believe that the court cases of Sweatt v. Painter and Brown v. the Board of Education happened fewer than 70 years ago.” Ask students to name the two verbs in the sentence and what they notice about them. Point out that is is present tense and refers to the writer’s own thoughts in the present. Happened is past tense and refers to the court cases that occurred in the past. The writer has used correct shifts in verb tense.

Remind students that during the editing process, they should check to be sure they have used correct verb tense shifts when necessary. Also remind them to check for any additional errors in spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and grammar as they edit their work.

Productive Engagement: Partner

Ask students to read part or all of their draft opinion essay to a partner to look for places where incomplete sentences should be revised to clarify meaning. Have them check each other’s work for correct use of verb tenses.

Share

Invite students to share a revision or edit they made during partner collaboration. Use this opportunity to clarify skills as needed.

Manage Independent Writing

Invite students to continue revising and editing their opinion essays. Remind them to keep sentence structure in mind. Use conferring time to meet with students who may need additional support, and allow time for students to engage in additional peer review.
