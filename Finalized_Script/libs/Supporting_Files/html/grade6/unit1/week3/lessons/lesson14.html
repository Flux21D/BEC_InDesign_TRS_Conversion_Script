Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Reread to annotate texts in order to compare and contrast ideas.
  • Use my understanding of two texts to respond to a close-reading prompt.
  • Share my ideas in collaborative conversation.
  • Organize my ideas into a clearly written response.

Additional Materials

  • Compare-Contrast Evidence chart

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Ask students what idea must be supported by the two texts. Provide the following sentence frame to help them express the idea.

  • We can and should look to our past in our fight to improve our present.
  • Ask them to explain this using their own words.
  • We must look at the past in order to improve our life today.

Invite them to return to both texts to find examples that support this opinion, and write them in a two-column chart. (See example on p. 94.)

Moderate Support

Ask students to paraphrase the idea that must be supported by the two texts. Provide a sentence frame if they need help.

  • We can and should ____ because ____.
  • We must ____ in order to ____.

Encourage them to return to the text to find examples that support this idea.

Light Support

Ask students to complete the two-column chart by citing examples from both texts that support the idea that we should look to the past to help improve the future.

14 Close Reading: Integrate Information from Multiple Texts on the Same Topic (15–20 min.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, RI.5.8, RI.5.9, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Review with students that sometimes they will be asked to analyze information from multiple texts on the same topic. Explain that in today’s lesson, students will compare and contrast the two persuasive speeches they read in this unit.

Ask: How can having multiple sources of information help a reader gain a broader understanding of a topic?

Model: Reread to Find Text Evidence

Display and read aloud the close reading question and annotation instructions. Model how you think about the prompt.

Sample modeling: This question asks me to reread parts of two different texts and to compare and contrast. What am I to compare and contrast? The “reasons and evidence” each man gave to support the idea that “we can and should look to our past in our fight to improve our present.” I am to use a two-column chart to help me think about how evidence in the two paragraphs is alike and different.

Close Reading Question: Reread paragraphs 3–5 of the “Voting Rights Act Address” and paragraphs 2–4 of the “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.” Compare and contrast the reasons and evidence Lyndon Johnson and Thurgood Marshall gave to support the idea that we can and should look to our past in our fight to improve our present.

Annotate! Jot ideas in a two-column chart to help you compare.

Display a blank two-column chart and fill in the headings as shown. Read aloud the listed paragraphs as students follow along. Model how you think about the text.

Sample modeling: As I read, I look for evidence that each man used to support the idea that we can and should learn from the past. In his speech, President Johnson refers to the American Revolutionary War and the Civil War as past examples of a “search for freedom.” He also mentions events in Selma, Alabama, and reminds listeners of our nation’s purpose. Justice Marshall also sees the value of the past, but he looks only at the recent past. He says he wants to use stories of people to “gain inspiration for ‘what can be.’”

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second Close Reading Prompt.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 7–12 of the “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” and paragraphs 6–11 of the “Voting Rights Act Address.” Compare and contrast the reasons and evidence Thurgood Marshall and Lyndon Johnson used to support their claims that American laws and society needed to evolve.

Annotate! Create a two-column chart to help you compare the ways Thurgood Marshall and Lyndon Johnson supported their positions.

Ask students to reread and annotate the text independently or with a partner and be prepared to share their responses to the prompt. Observe students’ notes and annotations to determine if they need additional modeling or corrective feedback.

Share

Call on several students to share their answers to the close reading question. Encourage other students to build on their ideas, ask clarifying questions, or express conflicting ideas. Use this opportunity to provide additional modeling, corrective feedback, or validations based on students’ responses.

Students should create a two-column chart with evidence from each speech that supports the idea that laws and society needed to evolve. Students should understand that Johnson was speaking about one freedom: the right to vote; Marshall was speaking in broader terms. From the “Voting Rights Act Address,” students might include: “. . . every citizen . . . shall share in the dignity of man.”; “. . . his right to be treated as a man equal in opportunity to all others.”; “Every American citizen must have an equal right to vote . .
. . we have to ensure that right.”; “. . . in many places in this country men and women are kept from voting simply because they are Negroes.” From the “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” students might include: “I see not a nation of unity but of division . . . .”; “But there is a price to be paid for division . . . .”; “Democracy just cannot flourish amid fear.”; any of the “We must dissent” statements; “ . . . our fates are bound together.”

Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to write a one- or two-paragraph answer to the close reading question, citing the reasons and evidence used by each man. Use their writing to help you evaluate their ability to respond successfully to a prompt and to compare and contrast texts.

Challenge Activity: Ask students to use text evidence from two of the unit’s selections to compare and contrast two amendments to the Constitution, telling how they helped our laws evolve.
