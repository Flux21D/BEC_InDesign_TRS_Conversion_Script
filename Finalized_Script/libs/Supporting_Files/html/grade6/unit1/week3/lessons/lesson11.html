Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Reread a text to draw an inference based on text evidence.
  • Respond to a Close Reading Prompt.
  • Share my ideas in collaborative conversation.
  • Communicate my inference and text evidence in writing.

Additional Materials

  • Text Evidence/Inference chart

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Ask students to describe what they believe Marshall feels when he gives his speech.

  • I believe that he thinks we need more progress.
  • It appears that he is disappointed with what we have not achieved.

Prompt them to return to the text to find examples of what he says that makes them have these opinions. (See examples given in the Sample Text Evidence/ Inference chart.) Have them explain how these examples support their opinion.

  • “I wish I could say racism and prejudice were recent memories” shows that he believes they still exist.
  • “I wish I could say this nation has traveled far on the road to social justice” means he does not believe it has progressed enough.

Moderate Support

Ask students: What is the main point that Marshall is trying to make?

  • He believes that.
  • He wishes that.

Ask students: How do you know this? Write what he says that makes you think this.

Have them return to the text to find examples of what Marshall says that makes them have these opinions. (See examples given in the Sample Text Evidence/ Inference chart.) Encourage them to explain how these support their opinion.

  • He believes racism and prejudice still exist because he says.
  • He feels we need more progress with social justice because he says.

Light Support

Invite students to state what they believe Marshall’s main point is. Ask them to reread the text for evidence that supports what they believe. Instruct them how to use the evidence and their opinion to complete the Sample Text Evidence/Inference chart.

11 Close Reading: Use Text Evidence to Make Inferences  (15–20 MIN.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Ask students what it means to draw an inference. Review that readers draw inferences when they use evidence from the text to understand an idea that is not directly stated in the text. In this close reading, students will draw inferences using evidence from “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech.”

Ask:Why is effectively drawing an inference an important skill for readers?

Model: Reread to Find Text Evidence

Display and read aloud the close reading prompt and annotation instructions. Model how you think about the prompt.

Sample modeling: The prompt tells me to reread paragraph 7 and asks: “What can you infer,” which tells me I need to draw an inference. I will have to figure out how Thurgood Marshall feels, based on his speech, about “progress made toward equality in the United States.” I will look for evidence in his speech.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraph 7. What can you infer about how Thurgood Marshall feels about the progress made toward equality in the United States? Refer to specific text evidence to support your inference.

Annotate! Underline words or phrases that support your inference.

Display a Text Evidence/Inference chart. Read aloud paragraph 7 as students follow along. Model how you think about the text.

Sample modeling: As I read, I look for words and phrases that help me understand Justice Marshall’s feelings about progress our nation has made toward equality. In sentence 7, I read that he “would have predicted” that the nation would have made great progress by now.

Sample modeling: I keep reading and see the words “I wish I could say” many times. He wishes he could say that racism and prejudice “were only distant memories,” but he can’t because it’s not true. He and others worked so hard to make things better, but not enough progress has been made. I can infer that he is disappointed.

Sample Text Evidence (sentence)

Sample Inference

“. . . I would have predicted that I would have spoken with much pride and optimism of the enormous progress this nation has made.” (7)

In paragraph 7, Justice Marshall says he expected that “enormous progress” would have been made toward equality in our nation by now. And, he goes on to say, “I wish I could say that racism and prejudice were only distant memories” and “that this nation has traveled far along the road to social justice.” We can infer that because he cannot say those things, he is disappointed and disheartened with where we stand right now.

“But as I survey the world Heman Sweatt and the Shelleys left behind, I wish I could say that racism and prejudice were only distant memories.” (8)

“I wish I could say that this nation had traveled far along the road to social justice . . . .” (9)

“I wish I could say that America has come to appreciate diversity and to see and accept similarity.” (10)

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second Close Reading Prompt.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraphs 10–11. What can you infer about the specific policies Thurgood Marshall believed would help “knock down the fences that divide” Americans? Refer to specific text evidence to support your inference.

Annotate! Underline words or phrases that support your inference.

Ask students to reread and annotate the text independently or with a partner and be prepared to share their answer to the close reading question. Observe students’ notes and annotations to determine if they need additional modeling or corrective feedback.

Share

Call on several students to share their answers to the close reading question. Encourage other students to build on their ideas, ask clarifying questions, or express conflicting ideas. Use this opportunity to provide additional modeling, corrective feedback, or validations based on students’ responses.

Students should formulate a response similar to this: In paragraph 10, Marshall states that “we must dissent from a nation that has buried its head in the sand, waiting in vain for the needs of its poor, its elderly, and its sick to disappear and just blow away. We must dissent from a government that has left its young without jobs, education or hope.” From this, we can infer that Marshall thinks that Americans can promote liberty and democracy by addressing the needs of the poor and sick, and by creating more education and employment opportunities for young people.

Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to write their answer to the close reading question independently, citing the specific text evidence they used to draw their inference. Use their writing to help you evaluate their ability to use text evidence to draw inferences.

RI.5.1 Quote accurately from a text when explaining what the text says explicitly and when drawing inferences from the text. RI.5.3 Explain the relationships or interactions between two or more individuals, events, ideas, or concepts in a historical, scientific, or technical text based on specific information in the text. W.5.10 Write routinely over extended time frames (time for research, reflection, and revision) and shorter time frames (a single sitting or a day or two) for a range of discipline-specific tasks, purposes, and audiences..
