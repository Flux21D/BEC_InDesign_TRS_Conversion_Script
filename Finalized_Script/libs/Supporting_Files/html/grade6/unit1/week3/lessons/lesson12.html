Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Recognize and pronounce words with r-controlled vowel spelling patterns.
  • Collaborate to list and pronounce words with r-controlled vowels.
  • Spell words with r-controlled vowel patterns.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Write the bold words found in the text on the board and have pairs copy each word on an individual strip of paper. Ask them to create the chart modeled on page 90 and place each word in the appropriate column.

Encourage partners to alternate reading the words to each other. Have each student ask simple questions using each word that a partner then answers. Examples:

  • When did it happen? It happened later.
  • Which case was first? Sweatt’s case was first.

Moderate Support

Ask students to find words in the text that make the sound /er/. (See chart on p. 90.) Then have them place the words in the appropriate columns of their charts.

Invite partners to alternate reading aloud each sentence from the text that contains the chosen words. Then create and ask a question for each sentence, for which that sentence provides the answer.

Light Support

Ask students to locate the words in the text that make the sound /er/ and assign them to the appropriate column in the chart. (See chart on p. 90.)

Encourage partners to alternate paraphrasing aloud each sentence from the text that contains the chosen words, using the word in the paraphrase. Partners should ask a follow-up question about the sentence that asks for clarification or expansion of what it says.

Review

Display the word-sort chart from Lesson 9. Review with students that r-controlled vowels have a different sound that is neither long nor short and that the sound /r/ is represented by several different spellings.

Remind students that recognizing the spelling patterns for r-controlled vowels can help them read and pronounce words with those patterns. Direct students to paragraph 1 of “Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” and have them follow along as you read aloud the first sentence: “It is a pleasure to speak here on the anniversary of our nation’s independence.”

Say: Recognizing common spelling patterns for r-controlled vowels helps me pronounce words as I come to them in the text. I know that the letters -ure together often stand for the /r/ sound, so I can correctly pronounce the word pleasure. I see the letters -er together in anniversary. I know that those letters also stand for /er/. I can look for other spelling patterns as I continue to read.

Productive Engagement: Read and Annotate

Ask students to read “The Presidential Medal of Freedom” on page 30 of their Texts for Close Reading. As they read, tell them to underline words with r-controlled vowel spelling patterns that stand for the /r/ sound. If time permits, have students sort the words by spelling pattern. Note: Some students may underline words with /er/ spelled or as in work and world. Explain that this is a less common pattern for the sound /er/.

Give students approximately five minutes to read and annotate the selection. You may wish to have students read independently or with a partner. Students will find the following words in the text:

Next, have partners take turns rereading the selection to build fluency, paying careful attention to punctuation and their pronunciation of the words with r-controlled spelling patterns for the sound /r/.

Share Word Knowledge and Ideas

Bring students back together and have them share with the class the words they underlined with r-controlled vowel spelling patterns for the sound /er/. Ask them to pronounce the words.

Discuss “The Presidential Medal of Freedom” with students, using the following as prompts:

  • How did understanding r-controlled vowel spelling patterns make the text easier to read?
  • What did you learn from this selection that affects the way you understand Justice Marshall’s speech?
  • Based on what you read in “The Presidential Medal of Freedom” and what you already know from reading the speech, do you think Justice Marshall deserved the award?

Spelling

Review this week’s words. Distribute copies of the Spelling Practice. Challenge students to remember their spelling words, their reading of “The Presidential Medal of Freedom,” and their knowledge of r-controlled vowel spelling patterns to select, fill in, and correctly spell the words.

  1. Each _____ who receives the medal has done important work. (person)
  2. Thurgood Marshall valued the idea of _____ for all people. (liberty)
  3. Justice Marshall _____ the Presidential Medal of Freedom. (earned)
  4. President Johnson awarded more than _____ medals. (thirty)
  5. Neil Armstrong, the _____ man on the moon, received a medal. (first)
  6. Presidents have _____to sports and the arts to award hundreds of medals. (turned)
  7. People from many_____ have been honored for their contributions. (cultures)
  8. Without _____ it’s hard to overcome difficult situations. (determination)
