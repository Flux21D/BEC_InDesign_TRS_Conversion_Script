Student Objectives

I will be able to:

  • Read to identify and annotate examples of powerful words in a persuasive text.
  • Use my understanding of powerful words to answer a close reading question.
  • Share my ideas with a partner and/or class.
  • Write a clear response to a question about the text.

Integrated ELD

Substantial Support

Give students examples of powerful words from the reading and ask them to explain their meaning. Provide sentence structures to help them formulate their answers.

  • “constrained by the shackles of prejudice”
  • “devastating blow”
  • “stinging rejection”
  • “painful reminder”
  • “chasm”
  • Shackles are tools to restrain people.
  • Prejudice is to have an opinion before knowing someone.
  • Devastating means harmful or difficult.
  • Blow means a hit.
  • Stinging means hurting badly.
  • Rejection means to be turned away.
  • Chasm means a big hole or gap.

Moderate Support

Ask students to locate powerful words in the reading. Help them explain their meanings using the following sentence starters:

  • Shackles are tools that.
  • Prejudice is when.
  • Devastating means.
  • A blow is.
  • Stinging feels like.
  • Rejection is when.
  • A chasm is a big.

Light Support

Ask students to provide synonyms for the powerful words they locate. Some examples follow.

  • Shackles = restraints
  • Prejudice = bias
  • Devastating = painful emotionally
  • Blow = a hard hit
  • Stinging = painful in a sharp way
  • Rejection = turn away
  • Chasm = gap, divide

8 Close Reading: Identify Elements of Persuasive Text (15–20 min.) RI.5.1, RI.5.3, W.5.10, SL.5.1a, SL.5.1b, SL.5.1c, SL.5.1d

Engage Thinking

Invite students to identify parts of a text like “Thurgood Marshall’s Liberty Medal Acceptance Speech” as persuasive. Review that one of the elements of a persuasive text is the use of powerful words meant to influence the way the reader thinks. Today you will model recognizing powerful words and thinking about how those words affect you as a reader.

Ask: What effect do powerful words have on you when listening to a speech?

Model: Reread to Find Text Evidence

Display and read aloud the Close Reading Prompt and annotation instructions. Model how you think about the prompt.

Sample modeling: The prompt tells me to reread paragraph 4, so the first thing I need to do is locate the correct paragraph. Then I can figure out what the prompt asks for. It says “cite specific examples,” so I know I need to quote directly from the text. I’m looking for examples of “how Thurgood Marshall incorporates this technique.” What does “this technique” refer to? I’ll read the prompt again. “This technique” refers to how persuasive texts use powerful words to influence readers. Now I understand that I’m looking for powerful words that affect how I think about the topic.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraph 4. You’ve learned that persuasive texts use powerful words to influence readers. Cite specific examples of how Thurgood Marshall incorporates this technique in this paragraph.

Annotate! Underline any words or phrases that are examples of “powerful words.”

Read aloud paragraph 4 as students follow along. Model how you think about the text.

Sample modeling:As I read, I look for strong, powerful, memorable words that give me a clear sense of what Justice Marshall is trying to get me to think. In sentence 4, I read that officials were “constrained by the shackles of prejudice.” That is a powerful phrase that makes Justice Marshall’s opinion clear to me, so I will underline it.

Sample modeling: In the last sentence, I read the decision was a “devastating blow” and a “stinging rejection.” Those phrases are meant to make me feel Heman Sweatt’s pain and convince me that the law school officials did not treat him well. In addition to those phrases, I’ll underline the powerful words “painful reminder” and “chasm” near the end of the sentence.

Guided Practice

Display and read aloud a second Close Reading Prompt.

Close Reading Prompt: Reread paragraph 11. What powerful words and phrases does Thurgood Marshall incorporate into this paragraph? Cite specific examples.

Annotate! Underline any words or phrases that are examples of “powerful words.”

Invite students to reread and annotate the text independently or with a partner. Observe students’ notes and annotations to determine if they need additional modeling or corrective feedback.

Share

Call on several students to share their answers to the close reading question. Encourage other students to build on their ideas, ask clarifying questions, or express conflicting ideas. Use this opportunity to provide additional modeling, corrective feedback, or validations based on students’ responses.

Students should find several examples of powerful language, including at least some of the following: “force open doors,” “knock down walls,” “cannot escape each other,” “muster the courage,” “knock down the fences that divide,” “tear down the walls that imprison.”

Write to Apply Understanding

Ask students to write a paragraph explaining how Justice Marshall’s use of powerful words influenced them as readers. Students should cite specific examples from paragraph 11.

Challenge Activity: Ask students to explain in writing which of paragraphs 4 or 11 they found most persuasive and why.
